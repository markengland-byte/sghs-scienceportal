The Appendicular Skeleton
The appendicular skeleton includes all 126 bones of the upper and lower limbs, plus the girdle bones that attach each limb to the axial skeleton. Our upper limbs are built for mobility; our lower limbs for stability and locomotion.

Upper Limb · Section 8.1
The Pectoral Girdle
The pectoral girdle consists of the clavicle and scapula. These two bones attach the upper limb to the axial skeleton and provide the remarkable range of motion at the shoulder.

Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 8.1
The Clavicle

The clavicle (collarbone) is an S-shaped bone located on the anterior side of the shoulder. It serves as a strut to keep the scapula in position so the arm can hang freely. Medially, it articulates with the manubrium of the sternum at the sternoclavicular joint. Laterally, it articulates with the acromion of the scapula at the acromioclavicular joint. The clavicle transmits forces from the upper limb to the axial skeleton and protects the underlying nerves and blood vessels. It is the most commonly fractured bone in the body.

The Scapula

The scapula (shoulder blade) is a flat, triangular bone on the posterior shoulder. It does not directly articulate with the axial skeleton — it is held in position by muscles only. Its posterior surface features a prominent ridge called the spine of the scapula, which extends laterally to form the acromion (the bony tip of the shoulder). The coracoid process is a short, hook-like projection that extends anteriorly. The glenoid cavity is a shallow depression on the lateral scapula that receives the head of the humerus to form the glenohumeral (shoulder) joint.

🔑
Pectoral vs. Pelvic Girdle
The pectoral girdle prioritizes mobility — it is lightly built and attaches to the axial skeleton at only one point (sternoclavicular joint). The pelvic girdle prioritizes stability — it is massive and firmly attached to the sacrum. This reflects the different functions of the upper (manipulation) and lower (weight-bearing) limbs.

Upper Limb · Section 8.2
Bones of the Upper Limb
The upper limb contains 30 bones in three regions: the arm (humerus), the forearm (radius and ulna), and the hand (carpals, metacarpals, and phalanges).

Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 8.2
The Humerus

The humerus is the single bone of the upper arm. Its rounded head articulates with the glenoid cavity of the scapula to form the shoulder joint. The greater tubercle and lesser tubercle serve as attachment sites for rotator cuff muscles, separated by the intertubercular groove (bicipital groove). The deltoid tuberosity on the lateral shaft is the attachment point for the deltoid muscle. Distally, the trochlea articulates with the ulna and the capitulum articulates with the radius to form the elbow joint.

The Ulna and Radius

The ulna is the medial bone of the forearm. Its prominent olecranon process forms the bony tip of the elbow, and the C-shaped trochlear notch wraps around the trochlea of the humerus. The radius is the lateral forearm bone with a disc-shaped head. The shaft has a slight curve and an interosseous border along its medial side for attachment of the interosseous membrane that firmly links the radius and ulna.

The Wrist and Hand

Eight carpal bones form two rows at the base of the hand. The proximal row contains the scaphoid, lunate, triquetrum, and pisiform. The distal row contains the trapezium, trapezoid, capitate, and hamate. Five metacarpal bones form the palm, and 14 phalanges form the fingers — the thumb has two (proximal and distal), while fingers 2–5 each have three (proximal, middle, and distal). Each upper limb contains 30 bones; both together total 60.

The Pelvic Girdle & Pelvis
The pelvic girdle consists of a single hip bone (coxal bone) on each side, formed by the fusion of three bones: the ilium, ischium, and pubis. Together with the sacrum and coccyx, they form the bony pelvis.

Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 8.3
The Hip Bone

The pelvic girdle consists of a single bone, the hip bone or coxal bone, on each side. The hip bone is formed by the fusion of three bones: the ilium, ischium, and pubis. These three bones fuse during the late teenage years. The two hip bones, together with the sacrum and coccyx, form the bony pelvis.

The ilium is the fan-shaped superior portion and the largest of the three hip bone regions. Its superior margin is the iliac crest, which can be palpated along the waistline. The ischium forms the posterolateral region. The ischial tuberosity is the roughened area that bears body weight when sitting. The pubis forms the anterior portion. The two pubic bones are joined at the midline by the pubic symphysis, a cartilaginous joint.

The three regions converge to form the acetabulum, a deep, cup-shaped cavity that articulates with the head of the femur to form the hip joint. The obturator foramen is the large opening between the ischium and pubis.

Bones of the Lower Limb
The lower limb contains 30 bones: the femur (thigh), patella, tibia and fibula (leg), and the 7 tarsals, 5 metatarsals, and 14 phalanges of the foot. The femur is the longest and strongest bone in the body.

Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 8.4
The Femur

The femur is the single bone of the thigh region. It is the longest and strongest bone of the body, accounting for approximately one-quarter of a person's total height. The head of the femur articulates with the acetabulum of the hip bone. The fovea capitis is a small pit on the femoral head for ligament attachment. The neck is a common fracture site, especially in the elderly. The greater trochanter and lesser trochanter are attachment sites for muscles that move the hip. Distally, the medial and lateral condyles articulate with the tibia to form the knee joint.

The Patella, Tibia, and Fibula

The patella (kneecap) is the largest sesamoid bone of the body, embedded within the quadriceps femoris tendon. It articulates with the patellar surface of the femur but does not contact the tibia. The tibia (shin bone) is the large, medial, weight-bearing bone of the leg. Its medial malleolus forms the medial ankle prominence. The fibula is the thin, lateral, non-weight-bearing bone. Its lateral malleolus forms the lateral ankle prominence. The tibia and fibula are firmly linked by an interosseous membrane.

The Foot

The posterior half of the foot is formed by seven tarsal bones. The talus articulates with the tibia and fibula to form the ankle joint and transmits body weight to the foot. The calcaneus (heel bone) is the largest tarsal bone. Five metatarsal bones form the anterior foot, and 14 phalanges form the toes (the big toe has 2; the others have 3 each). Each foot contains 26 bones.

The foot has three arches — the medial longitudinal, lateral longitudinal, and transverse arch — that distribute body weight and absorb shock. During running, forces on the foot can reach 2.5 times body weight. Overstretched supporting ligaments can cause pes planus (flat feet).

Development · Section 8.5
Development of the Appendicular Skeleton
The appendicular skeleton arises from mesenchyme during embryonic development. Limb buds appear around week four, and by week twelve, primary ossification centers have appeared in the shafts of all limb bones.
⏱ ~20 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 8.5

  • Discuss the appearance of primary and secondary ossification centers
Key Vocabulary

tap

Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 8.5
Limb Development

Embryologically, the appendicular skeleton arises from mesenchyme, a type of embryonic tissue that can differentiate into many types of tissues, including bone or muscle tissue. Limbs begin as small lateral bulges called limb buds, appearing around week four of development (upper limb buds precede lower by several days). The ectoderm at the end of the limb bud thickens to form a narrow crest called the apical ectodermal ridge, which stimulates mesenchymal growth.

By week six, the distal limb regions flatten into paddle shapes (future hands and feet). Constrictions appear at the wrist/ankle and elbow/knee sites. Cell death between the digital rays creates finger and toe separations. During week seven, the limbs rotate: upper limbs rotate laterally 90° (elbows point posteriorly, palms face anteriorly); lower limbs rotate medially 90° (knees point anteriorly, big toe moves medially).

Ossification

All appendicular bones (except the clavicle) form through endochondral ossification — mesenchyme becomes hyaline cartilage models that are gradually replaced by bone. Primary ossification centers appear in bone shafts by week twelve. Secondary ossification centers develop in the epiphyses later — some before birth, most after. The epiphyseal plate allows continued lengthening until bones reach adult size.

The clavicle is the exception — it develops via intramembranous ossification (mesenchyme directly becomes bone without a cartilage model). Ossification begins at week five, but the clavicle is the last bone to complete ossification, around age 25.

⚠️
Homeostatic Imbalance: Congenital Clubfoot
Clubfoot (talipes) is the most common congenital deformity of the lower limb, affecting ~1 in 1,000 births (twice as common in males). The foot is twisted inward at a sharp angle. Modern treatment with corrective casting beginning within the first 2 weeks after birth achieves success without surgery in ~90% of cases. Cigarette smoking during pregnancy has been linked to its development.
