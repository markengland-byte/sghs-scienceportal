The Autonomic Nervous System
The autonomic nervous system operates below conscious awareness, controlling cardiac muscle, smooth muscle, and glands. Its two divisions — sympathetic (fight-or-flight) and parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) — maintain homeostasis through opposing effects.

Divisions & Reflexes · Section 15.1
Divisions of the Autonomic Nervous System
The sympathetic division prepares the body for emergencies (fight-or-flight). The parasympathetic division promotes rest-and-digest activities. Both use a two-neuron chain: preganglionic to postganglionic.

Both divisions use a two-neuron efferent pathway: a preganglionic neuron (cell body in CNS) synapses in a ganglion onto a postganglionic neuron, which then innervates the target organ. All preganglionic fibers — both sympathetic and parasympathetic — release acetylcholine onto nicotinic receptors. The divisions differ at the postganglionic level: sympathetic postganglionic fibers release norepinephrine (adrenergic), while parasympathetic postganglionic fibers release acetylcholine onto muscarinic receptors.

🔑
The Adrenal Medulla — A Modified Ganglion
The adrenal medulla receives direct sympathetic preganglionic innervation (no postganglionic neuron needed). It releases epinephrine (80%) and norepinephrine (20%) directly into the bloodstream, amplifying and prolonging the fight-or-flight response throughout the entire body.

Preganglionic parasympathetic fibers are the longest because terminal ganglia are located near or within the target organ — so the preganglionic fiber must travel all the way from the brainstem or sacral cord to the organ.

Acetylcholine is released by parasympathetic postganglionic fibers to stimulate digestion (rest-and-digest). Epinephrine/norepinephrine/adrenaline are sympathetic and inhibit digestion.

The facial nerve (CN VII) carries preganglionic parasympathetic fibers to the submandibular and pterygopalatine ganglia, controlling salivation and lacrimation (tears). CN III, VII, IX, and X all carry parasympathetic fibers.

Divisions & Reflexes · Section 15.2
Autonomic Reflexes & Homeostasis
Visceral reflexes use the two-neuron autonomic pathway to maintain homeostasis. The two divisions compete at target organs through different neurotransmitter-receptor combinations, and visceral pain can be "referred" to unexpected body surface locations.
⏱ ~20 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 15.2
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Describe the components of a visceral reflex
  • Explain referred pain
  • Distinguish between short and long reflexes
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 15.2
Visceral Reflexes and Referred Pain

Visceral reflexes use a two-neuron efferent pathway (preganglionic → postganglionic) to control smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands. At most target organs, both sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions provide dual innervation, competing through different neurotransmitter-receptor systems to maintain homeostasis.

Referred pain occurs when visceral pain is perceived at a body surface location rather than the organ itself. This happens because visceral sensory fibers enter the spinal cord at the same level as somatic sensory fibers from the skin. The brain misinterprets the visceral signal as coming from the skin dermatome. For example, heart pain (angina) is often felt in the left shoulder and arm because cardiac visceral fibers and arm somatic fibers enter the spinal cord at the same thoracic levels.

Short vs. Long Reflexes

Long reflexes involve the CNS — sensory information travels to the brain or spinal cord, is integrated, and autonomic commands travel back to the target. Short reflexes bypass the CNS entirely — they occur through direct connections within autonomic ganglia, particularly in the enteric nervous system of the digestive tract.

🧠
Pupillary Reflex — Dual Innervation in Action
In dim light: sympathetic NE → radial muscle → pupil dilates (mydriasis). In bright light: parasympathetic ACh → sphincter muscle → pupil constricts (miosis). Same organ, competing neurotransmitters, opposite effects — the essence of dual innervation.

Control & Drugs · Section 15.3
Central Control
The hypothalamus is the master autonomic control center, integrating homeostatic mechanisms. The amygdala triggers fear-based sympathetic activation, and the medullary cardiovascular center regulates heart rate and blood pressure.
⏱ ~20 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 15.3
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Describe the role of the hypothalamus in autonomic control
  • Explain the amygdala's role in fear responses
  • Describe the medullary cardiovascular center
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 15.3
Forebrain Regulation

The hypothalamus is the primary autonomic control center, integrating information from the olfactory cortex, septal nuclei, and amygdala through the medial forebrain bundle. It coordinates both parasympathetic (via brainstem nuclei) and sympathetic (via thoracolumbar spinal cord) output through two main pathways: the dorsal longitudinal fasciculus and the medial forebrain bundle.

The amygdala, located in the medial temporal lobe, is part of the limbic system and processes emotions — particularly fear and anxiety. When activated, it signals the hypothalamus to trigger the sympathetic fight-or-flight response and release stress hormones through endocrine pathways.

Medullary Control

The medullary cardiovascular center regulates heart rate and blood vessel constriction. It receives baroreceptor input about blood pressure and adjusts autonomic output accordingly. The cardiac accelerator nerve (sympathetic preganglionic fibers) increases heart rate, while the vagus nerve (parasympathetic) decreases it. Additional medullary nuclei coordinate respiratory and digestive autonomic functions.

Control & Drugs · Section 15.4
Drugs that Affect the Autonomic System
Drugs can mimic or block autonomic neurotransmitters at their receptors. Agonists enhance neurotransmitter effects; antagonists block them. This principle underlies treatments for heart disease, asthma, glaucoma, and more.
⏱ ~20 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 15.4
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Define agonist and antagonist
  • Describe sympathomimetic and parasympathomimetic drugs
  • Explain how nicotine affects the autonomic system
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 15.4
Agonists and Antagonists

An agonist acts like the endogenous neurotransmitter by binding to and activating its receptor. An antagonist blocks the receptor, preventing the neurotransmitter from having its effect. Drugs targeting the autonomic system work by selectively affecting cholinergic (ACh) or adrenergic (NE/epi) receptors.

Drug TypeActionExampleClinical Use
SympathomimeticMimics sympathetic activation (adrenergic agonist)Phenylephrine (alpha-1 agonist)Nasal decongestant, pupil dilation for eye exams
SympatholyticBlocks sympathetic effects (adrenergic antagonist)Propranolol, metoprolol (beta-blockers)Hypertension, heart failure, anxiety
ParasympathomimeticMimics parasympathetic activation (muscarinic agonist)PilocarpineGlaucoma (constricts pupil, lowers eye pressure)
AnticholinergicBlocks parasympathetic effects (muscarinic antagonist)AtropinePupil dilation, bradycardia treatment, preoperative
Nicotine

Nicotine binds to nicotinic ACh receptors in ganglia of both sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions. Since both divisions are stimulated simultaneously, most organ effects cancel out — but the cardiovascular system is vulnerable because the parasympathetic division has minimal direct vascular control. The result: nicotine increases blood pressure and heart rate, increasing cardiovascular disease risk.

⚠️
Clinical: Why Smoking Damages the Heart
Nicotine stimulates both divisions, but unopposed sympathetic activation of blood vessels raises blood pressure. Combined with increased heart rate and potential arrhythmias from conflicting cardiac signals, smoking significantly elevates cardiovascular disease risk.

Section 15.4 Quiz — Drugs & the ANS

5 questions

1A drug that affects both divisions of the autonomic system is going to bind to, or block, which type of receptor?
  • A
    nicotinic
  • B
    muscarinic
  • C
    alpha-adrenergic
  • D
    beta-adrenergic
Nicotinic receptors are found in ganglia of BOTH the sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions (all preganglionic fibers release ACh onto nicotinic receptors). So a nicotinic drug affects both divisions simultaneously.
2A drug is called an agonist if it ________.
  • A
    blocks a receptor
  • B
    interferes with neurotransmitter reuptake
  • C
    acts like the endogenous neurotransmitter by binding to its receptor
  • D
    blocks the voltage-gated calcium ion channel
An agonist mimics the natural neurotransmitter by binding to and activating the same receptor. An antagonist blocks the receptor without activating it.
3Which type of drug would be an antidote to atropine poisoning?
  • A
    nicotinic agonist
  • B
    anticholinergic
  • C
    muscarinic agonist
  • D
    alpha-blocker
Atropine is a muscarinic antagonist (anticholinergic). The antidote would be a muscarinic agonist (parasympathomimetic) to compete for and activate the same receptors that atropine is blocking.
4Which kind of drug would have anti-anxiety effects?
  • A
    nicotinic agonist
  • B
    anticholinergic
  • C
    muscarinic agonist
  • D
    alpha-blocker
Anticholinergic drugs can reduce anxiety by dampening parasympathetic-mediated symptoms of anxiety (though alpha-adrenergic agents like clonidine also address anxiety by modulating sympathetic output).
5Which type of drug could be used to treat asthma by opening airways wider?
  • A
    sympatholytic drug
  • B
    sympathomimetic drug
  • C
    anticholinergic drug
  • D
    parasympathomimetic drug
A sympathomimetic drug (beta-2 adrenergic agonist like albuterol) mimics sympathetic activation of the bronchioles, causing bronchodilation — opening the airways wider for easier breathing.
