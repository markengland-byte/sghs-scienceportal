Basic Structure & Function of the Nervous System
The nervous system is divided into the central nervous system (brain and spinal cord) and the peripheral nervous system (everything else). It performs three overlapping functions: sensation, integration, and response.

Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 12.1
Two Anatomical Divisions

The nervous system is divided into two anatomical regions. The central nervous system (CNS) consists of the brain and spinal cord — it is the integration and command center. The peripheral nervous system (PNS) consists of all neural structures outside the CNS — the nerves and ganglia that carry signals to and from the CNS.

Nervous tissue contains two fundamental cell types: neurons (the communicating cells) and glial cells (the supporting cells). The neuron has a cell body (soma), dendrites that receive signals, and an axon that transmits signals to target cells.

Three Functions

The nervous system performs three integrated operations: sensation (detecting stimuli via receptors), integration (processing information, comparing with memories, making decisions), and response (generating motor output to muscles and glands). The somatic nervous system controls voluntary skeletal muscle. The autonomic nervous system controls involuntary functions (heart rate, digestion, gland secretion). The enteric nervous system independently manages the digestive tract.

Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 12.2
Neuron Anatomy

Neurons consist of three primary structural components. The soma (cell body) houses the nucleus and organelles. Dendrites are branched extensions that receive signals from other neurons at synapses. The axon is a single fiber that originates at the axon hillock and transmits nerve impulses to target cells. Many axons are wrapped in myelin, with gaps called nodes of Ranvier. The axon terminates at synaptic end bulbs that release neurotransmitters.

⚠️
Clinical: Multiple Sclerosis
Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a demyelinating disease of the CNS that destroys oligodendrocytes and their myelin sheaths. Without myelin, nerve signals slow dramatically or stop entirely, causing muscle weakness, vision problems, and coordination loss.

The Function of Nervous Tissue
The nervous system operates through a sensation-integration-response pathway. Sensory receptors detect stimuli, the CNS integrates the information, and motor neurons carry commands to muscles and glands.
⏱ ~20 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 12.3
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Describe the sensation-integration-response pathway
  • Distinguish between upper and lower motor neurons
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 12.3
Sensation → Integration → Response

The nervous system operates through a three-step pathway. Consider testing shower water temperature: (1) Sensation — thermoreceptors in your skin detect the water temperature, creating a graded potential proportional to the stimulus strength. (2) Integration — if the signal reaches threshold, it triggers an action potential that propagates along the axon to the synapse, where neurotransmitter is released. The signal travels through the thalamus to the cerebral cortex for conscious processing. (3) Response — motor commands travel via upper motor neurons from the cortex to the spinal cord, then via lower motor neurons to skeletal muscles, allowing you to adjust the faucet.

The entire sensory-integration-response pathway occurs within a fraction of a second. The cerebral cortex represents the highest level of integration — where conscious perception, decision-making, and voluntary motor commands originate.

StepFunctionStructures Involved
SensationDetect stimuli via receptorsSensory receptors → sensory neurons → spinal cord → thalamus
IntegrationProcess information, make decisionsThalamus → cerebral cortex (highest level)
ResponseGenerate motor outputUpper motor neurons → lower motor neurons → skeletal muscle

Signaling · Section 12.4
The Action Potential
The action potential is the fundamental electrical signal of the nervous system. It follows an all-or-none principle: either the threshold is reached and a full action potential fires, or nothing happens.
⏱ ~30 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 12.4
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Describe the resting membrane potential and the ions responsible
  • Explain the phases of the action potential
  • Define threshold and the all-or-none principle
  • Describe refractory periods and propagation
Key Vocabulary
Click Every Card to Flip It
Resting Potential
tap
The voltage across the membrane at rest: approximately -70 mV. Maintained by the Na+/K+ ATPase pump (3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in).
Threshold
tap
The membrane voltage (-55 mV) that must be reached to trigger an action potential. Below this, nothing happens (all-or-none).
Depolarization
tap
Phase where Na+ channels open, Na+ rushes in, and the membrane becomes less negative (from -70 mV toward +30 mV).
Repolarization
tap
Phase where K+ channels open, K+ exits the cell, and the membrane returns toward resting potential.
Saltatory Conduction
tap
Rapid signal propagation in myelinated axons where the action potential "jumps" between nodes of Ranvier. Much faster than continuous conduction.
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 12.4
The Three Phases

At rest, the neuron membrane sits at about -70 mV (resting membrane potential). When a stimulus is strong enough to reach threshold (-55 mV), voltage-gated sodium channels open and the action potential begins.

PhaseIon MovementVoltage Change
1. DepolarizationNa+ rushes INTO the cell through voltage-gated Na+ channels-70 mV → +30 mV (inside becomes positive)
2. RepolarizationK+ exits the cell through voltage-gated K+ channels+30 mV → -70 mV (inside becomes negative again)
3. HyperpolarizationK+ channels stay open briefly, too much K+ leavesOvershoots to ~-80 mV before returning to -70 mV
All-or-None Principle

A stronger stimulus does NOT produce a bigger action potential. If threshold is reached, a full action potential fires. If not, nothing happens. The nervous system encodes stimulus intensity by frequency of action potentials, not amplitude — a stronger stimulus causes more action potentials per second.

Propagation

In unmyelinated axons, the action potential propagates continuously along the membrane. In myelinated axons, it jumps between nodes of Ranvier — this saltatory conduction is much faster. The thickest, most heavily myelinated axons conduct signals fastest (up to 120 m/s).

🔑
Speed Formula: Thick + Myelinated = Fast
Signal speed depends on two factors: axon diameter (thicker = less resistance = faster) and myelination (insulation allows saltatory conduction). A thick, myelinated axon is the fastest conductor in the body.

Communication Between Neurons
Neurons communicate at synapses by releasing neurotransmitters. The effect of a neurotransmitter depends entirely on the receptor — the same neurotransmitter can excite one neuron and inhibit another.

EPSP
tap
Excitatory Postsynaptic Potential — a graded depolarization that moves the membrane toward threshold, making an action potential more likely.
IPSP
tap

tap

Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 12.5
Graded Potentials and Summation

Graded potentials are localized membrane voltage changes that vary in size proportional to the stimulus. EPSPs (excitatory postsynaptic potentials) depolarize the membrane toward threshold, while IPSPs (inhibitory postsynaptic potentials) hyperpolarize the membrane away from threshold. Multiple graded potentials combine through summation at the axon hillock — spatial summation (inputs from different locations) or temporal summation (rapid inputs from the same source). If the sum reaches threshold, an action potential fires.

The Chemical Synapse

At a chemical synapse, the presynaptic neuron releases neurotransmitter molecules from synaptic vesicles into the synaptic cleft. The neurotransmitter diffuses across the narrow gap and binds to receptor proteins on the postsynaptic cell. The critical principle: the effect of a neurotransmitter is entirely dependent on the receptor — the same molecule can excite or inhibit depending on the receptor type.
