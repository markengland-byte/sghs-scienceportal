The Skin as an Organ

Most people think of their skin as just an outer covering, but it is one of the body's most complex and important organs. The skin is the largest organ of the body, covering approximately 22 square feet and weighing about 8 pounds in an average adult — roughly 15% of total body weight. It is a living, dynamic organ that is constantly regenerating itself.

According to OpenStax A&P 2e, "the skin and its accessory structures make up the integumentary system, which provides the body with overall protection." Beyond protection, the skin performs at least seven major functions that are critical for survival, including thermoregulation, sensory reception, vitamin D synthesis, and immune defense. We will explore all of these in Lesson 7.

The Two Main Layers

The skin has two distinct layers. The outer epidermis is made of stratified squamous epithelium — layers of closely-packed epithelial cells that form a tough, waterproof barrier. The epidermis is avascular (has no blood vessels); it receives nutrients by diffusion from the dermis below.

The deeper dermis is made of dense irregular connective tissue and is well vascularized (contains many blood vessels). It also contains sensory nerve fibers, hair follicles, sweat glands, and sebaceous glands. The dermis provides structural support and flexibility to the skin through a mesh of collagen and elastin fibers.

The Hypodermis

Below the dermis lies the hypodermis (also called subcutaneous fascia or superficial fascia). It is not technically part of the skin, but it connects the skin to the underlying muscle and bone. The hypodermis is composed of loose areolar connective tissue and adipose (fat) tissue. It insulates the body against heat loss, cushions against mechanical shock, and stores energy. Body regions with more adipose tissue in the hypodermis provide more insulation and padding.

🔑
Skin vs. Integumentary System
The skin = epidermis + dermis only. The integumentary system = skin + hypodermis + hair + nails + glands. The hypodermis is considered a separate anatomical structure even though it is continuous with the dermis. This distinction is commonly tested.

Stratum Basale
tap
DEEPEST layer of the epidermis. Single row of stem cells (mainly keratinocytes) that constantly divide to produce new cells. Also contains melanocytes and Merkel cells. Attached to the basement membrane. "Basale" = base.
Stratum Spinosum
tap
8–10 layers of keratinocytes connected by desmosomes (look spiny under microscope — hence "spinosum"). Contains Langerhans cells (immune defense). Keratinocytes here begin producing keratin filaments.
Stratum Granulosum
tap
3–5 layers of flattening cells packed with keratohyalin granules (which help form keratin) and lamellar granules (which release waterproofing lipids). Cells begin to die here. "Granulosum" = grainy.
Stratum Lucidum
tap
Present ONLY in thick skin (palms, soles). A thin, clear (lucid) layer of dead cells packed with eleidin (a precursor to keratin). Acts as a transition zone between the granulosum and corneum.
Stratum Corneum
tap
MOST SUPERFICIAL layer. 25–30 layers of dead, flat, keratinized cells (corneocytes) filled with keratin. The waterproof barrier. Cells are constantly shed (desquamation) and replaced from below. "Corneum" = horn/hard.
Melanocyte
tap
Pigment-producing cell found in the stratum basale. Produces melanin (in organelles called melanosomes) and transfers it to surrounding keratinocytes via long extensions. Melanin protects DNA from UV radiation damage.
Langerhans Cell
tap
Immune cells (dendritic cells) found in the stratum spinosum. Act as sentinels — they detect pathogens, antigens, or damaged cells and activate the immune response. Part of the skin's immunological barrier.
Keratinization
tap
The process by which keratinocytes migrate from the stratum basale toward the surface, progressively accumulating keratin and eventually dying to form the tough, waterproof corneum. Takes ~2–4 weeks for a cell to travel from basale to corneum.

Papillary Layer
tap
The SUPERFICIAL layer of the dermis, directly beneath the epidermis. Thin; made of loose areolar connective tissue. Contains dermal papillae — upward projections that interlock with the epidermis and house capillaries and sensory receptors.
Reticular Layer
tap
The DEEP layer of the dermis (~80% of total dermis). Made of dense irregular connective tissue with a thick network (reticulum) of collagen and elastin fibers. Gives skin its strength, extensibility, and resistance to tearing.
Collagen Fibers
tap
Most abundant protein in the dermis. Provides TENSILE STRENGTH — resistance to being pulled or torn. Produced by fibroblasts. Collagen breaks down with age and UV damage, causing wrinkles. Makes up ~70% of dry skin weight.
Elastin Fibers
tap
Protein fibers that give the dermis ELASTICITY — the ability to stretch and recoil. Allow skin to return to shape after being stretched. Decrease with age → skin becomes less elastic, contributing to sagging and wrinkles.
Dermal Papillae
tap
Upward projections of the papillary dermis into the epidermis, creating ridges. Contain capillaries (nourishing avascular epidermis), Meissner's corpuscles (light touch), and free nerve endings. Create fingerprints and toe prints on hands and feet.
Fibroblast
tap
The primary cell type of the dermis. Produces both collagen and elastin fibers. Critical for wound healing — fibroblasts proliferate and produce collagen to repair damaged dermis. Fibroblast activity decreases with age.
Core Content
The Two Layers of the Dermis

The dermis is the "core" of the integumentary system — it provides structural support, houses blood vessels, and contains the nerve endings and accessory structures (hair follicles, glands) that give skin its many functions. According to OpenStax, "it contains blood and lymph vessels, nerves, and other structures, such as hair follicles and sweat glands. The dermis is made of two layers of connective tissue that compose an interconnected mesh of elastin and collagenous fibers, produced by fibroblasts."

Papillary Layer (superficial ~20%)

  • Loose areolar connective tissue
  • Thin collagen + elastin fibers
  • Dermal papillae project into epidermis
  • Capillaries nourish the epidermis
  • Meissner's corpuscles: light touch
  • Free nerve endings: pain, temperature

Reticular Layer (deep ~80%)

  • Dense irregular connective tissue
  • Thick bundles of collagen + elastin
  • Gives skin its strength and flexibility
  • Contains hair follicles, glands, deep vessels
  • Pacinian corpuscles: deep pressure/vibration
  • Stretch marks occur here when fibers tear
🔑
Fingerprints Are Formed by Dermal Papillae
The ridges of dermal papillae in the fingertips push the overlying epidermis into ridges — creating fingerprints. These ridged patterns are unique to each individual, genetically determined, and form before birth. They also improve grip by increasing friction with surfaces.
🧠
Remembering Collagen vs. Elastin
Collagen = Countless Cords of Connective tissue → strength (resists tearing). Elastin = Elastic bands → stretch and recoil. Skin is like a wetsuit: collagen gives it toughness, elastin lets it stretch.

1A patient's fibroblasts are malfunctioning and not producing normal collagen. What would be the MOST likely result?
  • A
    Skin that tears easily and wounds that heal poorly, because collagen provides tensile strength and is essential for tissue repair
  • B
    Loss of skin color because collagen helps distribute melanin

    Inability to sweat because collagen lines the sweat gland ducts

Collagen provides tensile strength to the dermis — resistance to stretching and tearing. Fibroblasts produce collagen and are critical for wound healing (they proliferate and synthesize collagen at wound sites). Without normal collagen: skin tears easily, wounds heal poorly, and joints may be hypermobile. This is seen in Ehlers-Danlos syndrome — a collagen disorder causing hyperextensible skin.
2What causes stretch marks (striae), and in which layer of the skin do they occur?
  • A
    Rupture of the epidermis due to rapid skin growth
  • B
    Inflammation in the hypodermis causing fat cell death
  • C
    Tearing of collagen and elastin fibers in the reticular layer of the dermis when skin is stretched rapidly beyond its elastic capacity
  • D
    Separation of the papillary and reticular layers of the dermis
Stretch marks occur when skin is stretched rapidly (rapid weight gain, pregnancy, growth spurts), exceeding the elastic capacity of the reticular dermis. The collagen and elastin fiber bundles tear, leaving visible scars in the dermis. They appear reddish-purple initially (inflamed, vascular) and fade to silver-white over time as they scar over. They cannot be truly reversed because the torn fibers do not fully regenerate.

The result: dermis loses tensile strength and elasticity → skin sags and wrinkles. This is why sunscreen is the most evidence-based anti-aging intervention — it protects dermal collagen from UV damage.

Key Vocabulary
Click Every Card to Flip It
Melanin
tap
The primary pigment responsible for skin, hair, and eye color. Two types: eumelanin (brown/black) and pheomelanin (red/yellow). Produced by melanocytes and stored in organelles called melanosomes. Absorbs UV radiation to protect DNA.
Melanosome
tap
Organelle inside melanocytes that synthesizes and stores melanin. Melanosomes are transferred to surrounding keratinocytes via melanocyte dendrites (long extensions). The melanosomes cluster above the keratinocyte nucleus, acting like a "parasol" shielding the DNA from UV radiation.
Eumelanin vs. Pheomelanin
tap
Eumelanin: brown/black pigment. Provides strong UV protection. Dominant in darker skin tones and dark hair. Pheomelanin: red/yellow pigment. Provides LESS UV protection. Dominant in red/blonde hair and contributes to lighter skin tones with pink undertones.
Hemoglobin
tap
Oxygen-carrying protein in red blood cells. In the dermis capillaries, oxygenated hemoglobin (oxyhemoglobin) appears bright red → gives fair skin its pinkish undertone. Deoxygenated hemoglobin appears bluish → cyanosis (bluish skin) indicates poor oxygenation.
Carotene
tap
Yellow-orange pigment found in certain plant foods (carrots, sweet potatoes). Accumulates in the stratum corneum and hypodermis. Responsible for yellowish skin tones in people who eat large quantities of carotene-rich foods (carotenemia). Precursor to Vitamin A.
Albinism
tap
Genetic condition where melanocytes cannot produce melanin (due to absence or malfunction of the enzyme tyrosinase). Affected individuals have white/pink skin, white hair, and pale eyes. Very high risk of sunburn and skin cancer due to lack of UV protection.

Hair Structure and Growth

Each hair consists of a shaft (the visible portion above the skin surface) and a root (the portion embedded in the skin). The root sits inside the hair follicle — a tube of epidermal cells extending down into the dermis. At the base of the follicle is the expanded hair bulb, which encloses the hair papilla (a vascularized dermal projection that nourishes the hair). The actively dividing cells of the hair matrix produce new hair cells that push the shaft upward as they accumulate keratin and die.

The hair shaft has three layers: the outer cuticle (overlapping scale-like cells that protect the hair), the middle cortex (bulk of the shaft; contains melanin that gives hair its color), and the inner medulla (loose cells and air spaces; present in thick hairs). Hair color is determined by melanin type and amount in the cortex — eumelanin = dark hair; pheomelanin = red; absence of melanin = white/gray hair.

tap

tap

Apocrine Sweat Gland
tap

tap

tap
Modified sweat glands found only in the external ear canal. Produce cerumen (earwax) — a yellowish, waxy substance that traps dust, debris, and microorganisms, protecting the eardrum. Cerumen also has antimicrobial properties.

Thermoregulation
tap
Maintenance of core body temperature (~37°C). The skin achieves this via: (1) sweating (evaporative cooling); (2) vasodilation (increased blood flow to skin to radiate heat); (3) vasoconstriction (reduced blood flow to conserve heat); (4) arrector pili (piloerection for insulation, vestigial in humans).
Vasodilation / Vasoconstriction
tap
Vasodilation: blood vessels widen → increased blood flow to skin → more heat lost to environment → skin appears flushed/red. Vasoconstriction: blood vessels narrow → less blood flow to skin → less heat lost → skin appears pale. Both are controlled by the sympathetic nervous system via the hypothalamus.
Vitamin D Synthesis
tap
UV-B radiation converts 7-dehydrocholesterol (in epidermal cells) → cholecalciferol (Vitamin D3). This is then activated by the liver and kidneys. Vitamin D is essential for calcium absorption in the intestine → bone formation. Insufficient UV exposure → Vitamin D deficiency → rickets (children) or osteomalacia (adults).
Cutaneous Sensation
tap
The skin is a sensory organ. Receptors: Meissner's corpuscles (light touch, in dermal papillae), Pacinian corpuscles (deep pressure/vibration, in deep dermis), Ruffini endings (skin stretch), free nerve endings (pain, temperature), Merkel cells (sustained touch). Collectively = cutaneous sensation.
Blood Reservoir
tap
The dermis contains an extensive network of capillaries and venules that can hold 8–10% of the body's total blood volume. During exercise or blood loss, vasoconstriction diverts this blood to muscles and vital organs. This "blood reservoir" function allows rapid redistribution of blood.

The Four Phases of Wound Healing
Phase 1 — Hemostasis (Minutes)

Immediately after injury, damaged blood vessels constrict to reduce blood loss. Within seconds, platelets adhere to exposed collagen and aggregate, forming a temporary platelet plug. The coagulation cascade is activated → fibrin threads form → a stable blood clot develops. The clot hardens into a scab — a temporary physical barrier protecting the wound from further contamination while healing begins below.

Phase 2 — Inflammation (Hours to ~4 Days)

Chemicals released by damaged cells trigger vasodilation and increased capillary permeability → more blood flows to the wound → redness, heat, and swelling. Neutrophils arrive first (within hours) to phagocytize bacteria and debris. Macrophages arrive next, continuing clean-up and releasing growth factors that signal the next phase. The classic signs of inflammation: rubor (redness), calor (heat), tumor (swelling), dolor (pain).

Phase 3 — Proliferation (~4 Days to ~3 Weeks)

Fibroblasts migrate into the wound and produce new collagen, forming granulation tissue — a vascularized temporary scaffold. New capillaries grow into the wound (angiogenesis). Keratinocytes at the wound edges begin proliferating and migrating across the wound surface (re-epithelialization), covering the wound bed. Myofibroblasts contract the wound edges, reducing the wound size.

Phase 4 — Maturation/Remodeling (Weeks to Years)

Granulation tissue is progressively replaced by scar tissue. Collagen fibers are remodeled — randomly laid collagen is reorganized into aligned bundles for greater tensile strength. Blood vessel density decreases (scar fades from red to white). The mature scar may achieve ~80% of the original tissue's tensile strength but lacks hair follicles, glands, and sensory receptors.

⚠️
Why Deep Wounds Scar but Superficial Ones Don't
Superficial wounds (only epidermis damaged): keratinocytes from the stratum basale regenerate the lost epidermis completely — no scar. Deep wounds (dermis involved): keratinocytes cannot regenerate lost dermis, so fibroblasts fill in with collagen (scar tissue). Scars lack dermis structures (hair follicles, glands) because these cannot regenerate.

First-Degree Burn
tap
Superficial burn affecting only the EPIDERMIS. Appears red, dry, and painful. No blisters. Heals in 3–5 days without scarring. Example: mild sunburn. Skin barrier is intact — no infection risk from burn itself.

tap

Burns: Classification by Degree

According to OpenStax, "burns are classified by the degree of their severity." The degree of a burn describes how deeply it penetrates through the skin layers. Understanding burn depth is essential for predicting healing, treatment requirements, and infection risk.

💡
Why 3rd Degree Burns Are Painless
Third-degree burns destroy all nerve endings in the epidermis and dermis. The patient feels NO pain from the burned area itself — the pain receptors are gone. This seeming paradox (more severe = less pain) is a key clinical observation. Patients with 3rd degree burns often feel pain only at the edges of the burn where partial-thickness injury exists (nerve endings intact).
The Rule of Nines

The Rule of Nines rapidly estimates the percentage of total body surface area (TBSA) involved in a burn — critical for calculating fluid resuscitation requirements (Parkland formula: 4mL × weight in kg × %TBSA = first 24hr fluid need). Each region = 9% of TBSA: head = 9%, each arm = 9%, anterior trunk = 18%, posterior trunk = 18%, each leg = 18%, perineum = 1%.

🔑
Why Fluid Resuscitation is Critical
Burns destroy the skin barrier → massive fluid, protein, and electrolyte loss through the open wound. Burns also trigger systemic capillary leak → fluid leaves vessels → hypovolemia → shock. IV fluids replace this loss and maintain perfusion to vital organs. Burns covering >20% TBSA typically require formal fluid resuscitation. Without it, cardiovascular collapse and death can occur within hours.
