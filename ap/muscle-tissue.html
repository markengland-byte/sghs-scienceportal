Three Types of Muscle Tissue

Muscle tissue is characterized by properties that allow movement. All three muscle types are excitable (their plasma membranes can change electrical states and send action potentials), contractile (they can shorten forcefully), extensible (they can stretch), and elastic (they recoil to their original length). Contraction in all three types involves the interaction of actin (thin filaments) and myosin (thick filaments), powered by ATP and triggered by calcium ions (Ca++).

Connective Tissue Layers

Skeletal muscles are wrapped by three concentric connective tissue sheaths. The epimysium is the outermost layer of dense, irregular connective tissue that wraps the entire muscle. The perimysium surrounds each fascicle — a bundle of muscle fibers. The endomysium is the thin innermost layer surrounding each individual muscle fiber, containing extracellular fluid and nutrients.

From Fiber to Filament

Skeletal muscle fibers can be quite large — up to 100 micrometers in diameter and up to 30 cm in length. During development, embryonic myoblasts fuse to form multinucleated fibers. Each fiber contains hundreds to thousands of myofibrils — long, rod-like organelles composed of repeating units called sarcomeres. The sarcomere is the functional unit of the muscle fiber, bordered by structures called Z-discs. Within each sarcomere, thin filaments (actin) and thick filaments (myosin) overlap in an organized pattern that produces the characteristic striations.

Muscle Fiber Contraction & Relaxation
The sliding filament model explains how muscles contract: thin filaments slide past thick filaments, shortening the sarcomere. This requires calcium, ATP, and the cross-bridge cycle.

Sliding Filament Model
tap
The mechanism of contraction: thin filaments (actin) slide past thick filaments (myosin), shortening the sarcomere without the filaments themselves changing length.
Tropomyosin
tap
Protein that wraps around actin and blocks myosin-binding sites at rest. Moves aside when calcium binds to troponin, allowing contraction.
Troponin
tap
Regulatory protein on actin that binds calcium ions. When Ca++ binds, troponin changes shape and moves tropomyosin off the binding sites.
Sarcoplasmic Reticulum
tap
Specialized smooth ER in muscle fibers that stores and releases calcium ions. Ca++ release triggers contraction; Ca++ reuptake causes relaxation.
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 10.3
The Sliding Filament Model

According to the sliding filament model, thin filaments are pulled by myosin and slide past the thick filaments within the sarcomere. The filaments do not change length — instead, they overlap more, shortening the sarcomere. For this to happen, myosin-binding sites on actin must be exposed by removing the blocking protein tropomyosin.

Excitation-Contraction Coupling

The sequence begins when a motor neuron releases acetylcholine (ACh) at the neuromuscular junction, causing depolarization of the sarcolemma. The action potential travels along the sarcolemma and down T-tubules deep into the fiber, triggering calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum. Calcium binds to troponin, which shifts tropomyosin to expose myosin-binding sites on actin.

Cross-Bridge Cycling

The contraction cycle involves repetitive myosin head attachment and detachment — like rowing a boat. Each cycle has four steps: (1) cross-bridge formation — myosin head binds to actin; (2) power stroke — myosin head pivots, pulling actin toward the sarcomere center; (3) cross-bridge detachment — ATP binds to myosin, releasing it from actin; (4) myosin reactivation — ATP is hydrolyzed, re-cocking the myosin head. Without ATP, the myosin head cannot detach from actin — this explains rigor mortis.

Relaxation

Relaxation occurs when motor neuron signaling ceases, calcium channels in the SR close, and ATP-powered calcium pumps actively transport Ca++ back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum. As calcium is removed, tropomyosin re-covers the binding sites, preventing further cross-bridge formation.

🔑
ATP — The Currency of Contraction
ATP serves two critical roles: (1) powering the cross-bridge cycle (detachment + re-cocking), and (2) powering calcium pumps for relaxation. Without ATP, muscles cannot contract OR relax — which is why rigor mortis (stiff muscles after death) occurs when ATP is depleted.

Nervous System Control of Muscle Tension
The nervous system controls muscle tension by varying the number and size of motor units activated and the frequency of stimulation. This allows everything from delicate eye movements to powerful leg thrusts.

  • Distinguish between wave summation, incomplete tetanus, and complete tetanus
  • Define treppe and muscle tone
Key Vocabulary
Click Every Card to Flip It
Motor Unit
tap
A single motor neuron plus all the muscle fibers it innervates. Small units = fine control (eyes); large units = powerful movement (legs).
Tetanus
tap
Sustained contraction without relaxation, caused by rapid repeated stimulation. Incomplete tetanus has small relaxations; complete tetanus has none.
Recruitment
tap
Progressive activation of motor units — small units first, then larger ones — to increase force. Also called the size principle.
Treppe
tap
The "staircase effect" — gradual increase in contraction strength over the first several twitches of a previously resting muscle, likely due to rising Ca++ levels.
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 10.4
Motor Units and the Muscle Twitch

A motor unit consists of a single motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it innervates. Motor units vary in size — small units (10–20 fibers) in the eye muscles allow precise control, while large units (2,000+ fibers) in the leg muscles generate powerful forces. The nervous system varies the number and size of activated motor units through recruitment — small units fire first, then progressively larger ones are recruited as more force is needed.

A single stimulus produces a twitch with three phases: the latent period (excitation-contraction coupling, no visible tension), the contraction phase (peak tension), and the relaxation phase (tension decreases as calcium is pumped back into the SR).

Summation and Tetanus

When stimuli arrive rapidly, the effects are summed — this is wave summation, producing progressively stronger contractions. At very high frequencies, the relaxation phase disappears entirely, producing complete tetanus — a continuous, plateau-level contraction. Treppe (the staircase effect) occurs when the first several contractions of a resting muscle gradually increase in strength.

Isotonic vs. Isometric Contractions

Isotonic contractions maintain constant tension while changing muscle length — concentric (shortening, lifting) or eccentric (lengthening, lowering). Isometric contractions produce tension without changing joint angle — crucial for posture and stabilization.

Even at rest, muscles maintain slight tension through cyclical activation of motor units — this is muscle tone, essential for posture and joint stabilization.

Exercise & Muscle Performance
Exercise produces cellular adaptations that improve muscle performance. Endurance training enhances aerobic capacity; resistance training increases muscle size through hypertrophy.
⏱ ~20 min

Hypertrophy and Atrophy

Hypertrophy is the enlargement of muscles through the addition of structural proteins, increasing cell diameter. Atrophy is the loss of structural proteins and decreased muscle mass. Age-related atrophy is called sarcopenia. Over time, atrophied muscle fibers are replaced by connective tissue and adipose tissue.

Endurance vs. Resistance Training
FeatureEndurance TrainingResistance Training
Primary effectMore mitochondria, more myoglobin, more capillariesMore myofibrils (hypertrophy)

ATP Energy Systems

Muscles use three systems to produce ATP: (1) creatine phosphate — immediate energy for the first 15 seconds, transfers its phosphate to ADP; (2) anaerobic glycolysis — rapid but produces lactic acid, sustains moderate activity for ~2 minutes; (3) aerobic respiration — slow but efficient (36 ATP/glucose vs. 2 ATP), sustains prolonged activity. Muscle fatigue results from depletion of energy reserves and accumulation of lactic acid.

Cardiac & Smooth · Section 10.7
Cardiac Muscle Tissue
Cardiac muscle is found only in the heart. It is striated like skeletal muscle but involuntary, with unique features including intercalated discs, gap junctions, and autorhythmicity.
⏱ ~15 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 10.7
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Describe the unique structural features of cardiac muscle
  • Explain intercalated discs, gap junctions, and autorhythmicity
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 10.7
Cardiac Muscle — Structure and Function

Cardiac muscle tissue is found only in the heart. Like skeletal muscle, it is striated and contains sarcomeres, but cardiac fibers are shorter, have one to two centrally located nuclei, and branch to connect with neighboring cells. Cardiac muscle cells connect end-to-end at specialized junctions called intercalated discs.

Intercalated discs contain two critical structures: gap junctions allow the flow of depolarizing current between adjacent cells, enabling rapid action potential transmission — this creates a functional syncytium where the entire heart contracts in a coordinated wave. Desmosomes anchor cells together, preventing them from pulling apart during the stress of contraction.

Specialized pacemaker cells demonstrate autorhythmicity — the ability to spontaneously depolarize and generate action potentials at regular intervals. While the autonomic nervous system and hormones modulate heart rate, pacemaker cells function independently to establish a baseline rhythm. Cardiac muscle action potentials feature a prolonged depolarization plateau, producing longer contractions than skeletal muscle.

🔑
Why Cardiac Muscle Cannot Tetanize
The prolonged action potential in cardiac muscle creates a long refractory period that prevents re-stimulation before the previous contraction is complete. This means cardiac muscle cannot undergo tetanus — each heartbeat must be followed by relaxation (filling), which is essential for its pumping function.

Smooth Muscle
Smooth muscle is nonstriated, involuntary muscle found in the walls of hollow organs, blood vessels, and airways. It uses calmodulin instead of troponin and comes in two organizational types.
⏱ ~20 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 10.8

Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 10.8
Smooth Muscle Structure

Smooth muscle fibers are spindle-shaped with a single central nucleus, measuring 30–200 micrometers in length. They lack striations because their actin and myosin are not organized into regular sarcomeres. Instead, thin filaments anchor to dense bodies (analogous to Z-discs) attached to the sarcolemma and intermediate filaments. When myosin pulls on actin, the dense bodies are pulled together, causing a characteristic corkscrew-like shortening.

Contraction Mechanism

Unlike skeletal and cardiac muscle, smooth muscle lacks troponin. Instead, contraction is regulated by calmodulin. When calcium binds to calmodulin, this complex activates myosin light chain kinase (MLCK), which phosphorylates myosin heads, enabling cross-bridge formation with actin. Smooth muscle is involuntary — controlled by the autonomic nervous system, hormones, and local factors.

Development & Regeneration of Muscle Tissue
Most muscle develops from embryonic mesoderm. Myoblasts fuse to form multinucleated skeletal muscle fibers. Satellite cells enable limited repair, but regeneration capacity varies greatly by muscle type.

Embryonic Development

Most muscle tissue originates from embryonic mesoderm. Paraxial mesodermal cells form somites, which generate myoblasts — muscle-forming stem cells. Myoblasts migrate throughout the body and fuse to create multinucleated skeletal muscle fibers (myotubes). Cardiac and smooth muscle cells are not multinucleated because their myoblasts do not fuse.

Satellite Cells and Repair

Satellite cells are similar to myoblasts — they are stem cells incorporated into muscle fibers, located outside the sarcolemma. They respond to growth factors released during stress or damage, facilitating protein synthesis for repair and growth. However, they primarily repair existing cells rather than creating new ones. Extensive damage leads to fibrosis — replacement by scar tissue that cannot contract.
