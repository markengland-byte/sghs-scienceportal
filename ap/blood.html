A&P · Chapter 18 · Overview
The Cardiovascular System: Blood
Single-celled organisms do not need blood. They obtain nutrients directly from and excrete wastes directly into their environment. The human organism cannot do that. Our large, complex bodies need blood to deliver nutrients to and remove wastes from our trillions of cells. The heart pumps blood throughout the body in a network of blood vessels. Together, these three components—blood, heart, and vessels—makes up the cardiovascular system. This chapter focuses on the medium of transport: blood.

Composition · Section 18.1
An Overview of Blood
Like all connective tissues, blood is made up of cellular elements and an extracellular matrix. The cellular elements—referred to as the formed elements—include red blood cells (RBCs), white blood cells (WBCs), and cell fragments called platelets.

Functions of Blood
Transportation

Nutrients from the foods you eat are absorbed in the digestive tract. Most of these travel in the bloodstream directly to the liver, where they are processed and released back into the bloodstream for delivery to body cells. Oxygen from the air you breathe diffuses into the blood, which moves from the lungs to the heart, which then pumps it out to the rest of the body. Hormones produced by endocrine glands travel via the bloodstream to target cells. Cellular wastes are carried by blood to removal organs — carbon dioxide to the lungs, metabolic wastes to the kidneys.

Defense

White blood cells protect the body from many disease agents. Specialized WBCs provide defense against bacteria, viruses, parasites, and tumor cells. Platelets and certain plasma proteins block further blood loss when blood vessels are damaged.

Homeostasis

Blood helps regulate core body temperature by absorbing and distributing heat throughout the body and to the skin surface for dissipation. Plasma proteins and other blood solutes act as buffers to maintain blood pH between 7.35 and 7.45. Blood also regulates the water content of body cells.

Blood Plasma

Plasma is approximately 92 percent water. It also contains plasma proteins, which are the most abundant solutes in plasma. These include albumin (54–60% of plasma proteins, maintains osmotic pressure), globulins (35–38%, includes transport proteins and immunoglobulins/antibodies), and fibrinogen (4–7%, essential for blood clotting). All plasma proteins except gamma globulins are produced by the liver.

Other solutes include electrolytes (sodium, potassium, calcium ions), dissolved gases (oxygen, carbon dioxide, nitrogen), organic nutrients (vitamins, lipids, glucose, amino acids), and metabolic wastes.

Composition · Section 18.2
Production of the Formed Elements
The lifespan of the formed elements is very brief. Although one type of leukocyte called memory cells can survive for years, most erythrocytes, leukocytes, and platelets normally live only a few hours to a few weeks. Thus, the body must form new blood cells and platelets quickly and continuously.

Sites of Hemopoiesis

Prior to birth, hemopoiesis occurs in a number of tissues, beginning with the yolk sac of the developing embryo, and continuing in the fetal liver, spleen, lymphatic tissue, and eventually the red bone marrow. Following birth, most hemopoiesis occurs in the red marrow, a connective tissue within the spaces of spongy (cancellous) bone tissue. In children, hemopoiesis can occur in the medullary cavity of long bones; in adults, the process is largely restricted to the cranial and pelvic bones, the vertebrae, the sternum, and the proximal epiphyses of the femur and humerus.

Throughout adulthood, the liver and spleen maintain their ability to generate the formed elements. This process is referred to as extramedullary hemopoiesis (meaning hemopoiesis outside the medullary cavity of adult bones). When a disease such as bone cancer destroys the bone marrow, causing hemopoiesis to fail, extramedullary hemopoiesis may be initiated.

Differentiation of Formed Elements from Stem Cells

All formed elements arise from stem cells of the red bone marrow. Stem cells undergo mitosis plus cytokinesis to give rise to new daughter cells: one remains a stem cell and the other differentiates into one of any number of diverse cell types. Stem cells occupy a hierarchical system, with some loss of the ability to diversify at each step. The totipotent stem cell is the zygote. The pluripotent stem cell gives rise to multiple types of cells. The mesenchymal cell develops only into types of connective tissue. The hematopoietic stem cell, or hemocytoblast, gives rise to all formed elements of blood.

Hemopoiesis begins when the hematopoietic stem cell is exposed to appropriate chemical stimuli collectively called hemopoietic growth factors. One daughter cell remains a hematopoietic stem cell, allowing hemopoiesis to continue. The other daughter cell becomes either of two types of more specialized stem cells:

Lymphoid stem cells give rise to a class of leukocytes known as lymphocytes, which include the various T cells, B cells, and natural killer (NK) cells, all of which function in immunity. Lymphoid stem cells quickly migrate from the bone marrow to lymphatic tissues, including the lymph nodes, spleen, and thymus. B cells mature in the bone marrow, while T cells mature in the thymus.

Myeloid stem cells give rise to all the other formed elements, including the erythrocytes; megakaryocytes that produce platelets; and a myeloblast lineage that gives rise to monocytes and three forms of granular leukocytes: neutrophils, eosinophils, and basophils.

Hemopoietic Growth Factors

Erythropoietin (EPO) is a glycoprotein hormone secreted by the interstitial fibroblast cells of the kidneys in response to low oxygen levels. It prompts the production of erythrocytes. Some athletes use synthetic EPO as a performance-enhancing drug (called blood doping) to increase RBC counts and subsequently increase oxygen delivery to tissues throughout the body.

Thrombopoietin, another glycoprotein hormone, is produced by the liver and kidneys. It triggers the development of megakaryocytes into platelets.

Cytokines are glycoproteins secreted by a wide variety of cells, including red bone marrow, leukocytes, macrophages, fibroblasts, and endothelial cells. They act locally as autocrine or paracrine factors. Two major subtypes include colony-stimulating factors (CSFs), which trigger differentiation of myeloblasts into granular leukocytes and monocytes, and interleukins, signaling molecules important in hemopoiesis, immunity, and inflammation. To date, more than a dozen interleukins have been identified, numbered IL-1, IL-2, IL-3, etc.

🏃
Everyday Connection: Blood Doping
Blood doping was originally the practice of injecting supplemental RBCs into an individual to enhance sports performance. With the development of synthetic EPO in the 1980s, it became possible to artificially stimulate RBC production. Synthetic EPO can increase hematocrit to 70 or greater — this increased viscosity raises resistance and forces the heart to pump more powerfully; in extreme cases, it has resulted in death. Lance Armstrong, winner of seven Tour de France titles, was stripped of his victories and admitted to blood doping in 2013.
🏥
Bone Marrow Sampling and Transplants
A bone marrow biopsy is a diagnostic test of a sample of red bone marrow. A bone marrow transplant replaces faulty bone marrow with a donor's healthy marrow and its stem cells. These procedures assist in diagnosis and treatment of severe anemias (thalassemia major, sickle cell) and some cancers (leukemia). Now, stem cells can often be isolated from a blood sample rather than requiring painful needle insertion near the iliac crest. For transplants, a matching donor is essential to prevent tissue rejection.

Shape and Structure

Males have approximately 5.4 million erythrocytes per microliter of blood, and females approximately 4.8 million per microliter. Erythrocytes are biconcave discs — plump at their periphery and very thin in the center — measuring 7–8 micrometers in diameter. This shape optimizes the surface-area-to-volume ratio for gas exchange. Mature erythrocytes lack nuclei and most organelles. Without mitochondria, they rely on anaerobic respiration, which means they do not consume any of the oxygen they transport. They contain spectrin, a structural protein that enables shape flexibility.

Hemoglobin

Hemoglobin is a large molecule made up of proteins and iron. It consists of four folded chains of a protein called globin (two alpha chains and two beta chains). Each globin chain is bound to a red pigment molecule called heme, which contains an ion of iron (Fe²⁺). Each iron ion can bind to one oxygen molecule; therefore, each hemoglobin molecule can transport four oxygen molecules. An individual erythrocyte may contain about 300 million hemoglobin molecules, consequently binding up to 1.2 billion oxygen molecules.

In the lungs, hemoglobin picks up oxygen to form oxyhemoglobin (bright red). In the tissues, it releases oxygen and becomes darker deoxyhemoglobin. Approximately 23–24 percent of carbon dioxide binds to hemoglobin, forming carbaminohemoglobin.

Normal pulse oximeter readings range from 95–100 percent saturation. Hypoxemia indicates low blood oxygen saturation.

Lifecycle of Erythrocytes

Production of erythrocytes in the marrow occurs at a staggering rate of more than 2 million cells per second. Essential nutrients include iron (less than 20% of dietary iron is absorbed; heme iron from animal sources absorbs more efficiently), copper (enables iron absorption via hephaestin and ceruloplasmin), and zinc (co-enzyme for heme synthesis). Vitamins B₁₂ and folate are also essential.

Erythrocytes live up to 120 days in the circulation, after which worn-out cells are removed by macrophages in the bone marrow, liver, and spleen. Hemoglobin is broken down: globin into amino acids (recycled), iron stored as ferritin/hemosiderin or transported via transferrin, and the non-iron portion produces breakdown pigments — biliverdin (green, as in bruising), bilirubin (yellow, causes jaundice), stercobilins (brown color of feces), and urobilins (yellow color of urine).

Disorders of Erythrocytes

Anemia — deficiency of RBCs or hemoglobin — affects over 3.5 million Americans. Three categories: blood loss, faulty production, and excessive destruction. Sickle cell disease is a genetic disorder producing hemoglobin S, causing erythrocytes to assume a sickle (crescent) shape at low oxygen concentrations. Iron deficiency anemia is the most common type. Thalassemia is an inherited condition common in Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, African, and Southeast Asian populations. Polycythemia vera causes excessive production of immature erythrocytes, raising blood viscosity and cardiac workload.

Leukocytes & Platelets
Leukocytes are far less numerous than erythrocytes: typically there are only 5,000 to 10,000 per microliter. They are complete cells, with a nucleus and organelles, and are capable of moving on their own.

Characteristics of Leukocytes

Unlike erythrocytes, leukocytes are complete cells with a nucleus and organelles. They use the bloodstream mainly for transportation to sites where they are needed. When they arrive, they leave the capillaries through a process called emigration or diapedesis — squeezing between adjacent endothelial cells in the vessel wall. They are then attracted to injury or infection sites by positive chemotaxis — moving in the direction of chemical stimuli.

Disorders of Leukocytes

Leukocytosis is an excessive leukocyte proliferation — often a normal response to infection, allergies, or burns. Leukopenia is below-normal production of leukocytes. Leukemia is a cancer involving uncontrolled leukocyte production. Lymphoma is cancer where malignant lymphocytes collect in lymph nodes and organs.

Platelets

Platelets are not true cells but cell fragments shed from large megakaryocytes in the bone marrow. They are 2–4 µm in diameter, number 150,000–400,000 per µL, and have a lifespan of approximately 10 days. Platelets are critical for hemostasis — they form platelet plugs, secrete procoagulant chemicals, and dissolve blood clots when no longer needed. Thrombocytopenia (too few platelets) leads to excessive bleeding; thrombocytosis (too many) leads to abnormal clotting.

Three Steps of Hemostasis
Step 1: Vascular Spasm

When a blood vessel is damaged, vascular spasm occurs: the smooth muscle in the walls of the vessel contracts dramatically. This response is triggered by endothelins released by vessel-lining cells and typically lasts up to 30 minutes, significantly reducing blood flow and blood loss.

Step 2: Platelet Plug Formation

Platelets accumulate at the injury site, becoming spiked and sticky, and binding to exposed collagen fibers. Von Willebrand factor helps stabilize the growing platelet plug. Activated platelets release adenosine diphosphate (ADP), serotonin, prostaglandins, and phospholipids — recruiting additional platelets and sustaining the vascular spasm.

Step 3: Coagulation

Coagulation is the formation of a blood clot through complex cascading reactions, ultimately producing a gelatinous but robust clot made of a mesh of fibrin. There are 12 known clotting factors, including Ca²⁺ and vitamin K, numbered I through XIII. (Factor VI was once believed to be a distinct clotting factor, but is now thought to be identical to factor V.)

Fibrinolysis and Anticoagulation

Fibrinolysis is the gradual degradation of the clot. The inactive protein plasminogen is converted to active plasmin, which breaks down fibrin. Plasma anticoagulants — including protein C, tissue factor pathway inhibitor (TFPI), and antithrombin — limit clotting to the injury site.

Clotting Disorders

Thrombocytopenia is a condition of too few platelets, leading to excessive bleeding. Hemophilia is a genetic deficiency in clotting factors — hemophilia A (factor VIII deficiency) accounts for approximately 80 percent of cases. Thrombosis is excessive clot formation within intact vessels. When a portion of a thrombus breaks free, it becomes an embolus — potentially causing heart attacks, strokes, or pulmonary embolisms.

ABO Blood Group

Blood types A, B, AB, and O are determined by the presence or absence of A and B glycoproteins (antigens) on erythrocyte surfaces. Individuals naturally develop antibodies against the antigens they lack — without prior exposure. A type A person has anti-B antibodies; a type B person has anti-A antibodies; type O has both anti-A and anti-B; type AB has neither.

When incompatible blood is transfused, antibodies bind to foreign antigens on the donor RBCs, causing agglutination (clumping) and hemolysis (rupture). This can block blood vessels, release hemoglobin into the bloodstream, and potentially cause kidney failure and death.

Rh Blood Group

About 85 percent of Americans are Rh positive (possess the D antigen); the rest are Rh negative. Unlike ABO antibodies, Rh antibodies only develop after exposure through transfusion or pregnancy — they are not naturally occurring.

Hemolytic Disease of the Newborn (HDN)

When an Rh-negative pregnant person is exposed to Rh-positive fetal blood (usually during delivery), they may produce anti-Rh antibodies. In subsequent pregnancies with an Rh-positive fetus, these antibodies can cross the placenta and destroy fetal erythrocytes, causing HDN. RhoGAM medication prevents Rh sensitization, reducing HDN incidence from 13–14% to just 0.1% since its introduction in 1968.
