Two Divisions of the Skeleton

The skeleton is subdivided into two major divisions — the axial and appendicular skeleton. The axial skeleton forms the vertical, central axis of the body and includes all bones of the head, neck, chest, and back. It serves to protect the brain, spinal cord, heart, and lungs. It also serves as the attachment site for muscles that move the head, neck, and back, and for muscles that act across the shoulder and hip joints to move their corresponding limbs.

The axial skeleton of the adult consists of 80 bones, including the skull, the vertebral column, and the thoracic cage. The skull is formed by 22 bones. Also associated with the head are an additional seven bones, including the hyoid bone and the ear ossicles (three small bones in each middle ear). The vertebral column consists of 24 bones, each called a vertebra, plus the sacrum and coccyx. The thoracic cage includes the 12 pairs of ribs and the sternum, the flattened bone of the anterior chest.

The appendicular skeleton includes all bones of the upper and lower limbs, plus the bones that attach each limb to the axial skeleton. There are 126 bones in the appendicular skeleton of an adult.

Axial Skeleton80Skull (22), associated head bones (7), vertebral column (26), thoracic cage (25)
Appendicular Skeleton126Upper limbs + pectoral girdle (64), lower limbs + pelvic girdle (62)
Total206

The Brain Case

The brain case, or cranial vault, consists of eight bones: the paired parietal and temporal bones, and the unpaired frontal, occipital, sphenoid, and ethmoid bones. Together these bones enclose the cranial cavity, which houses and protects the brain. The interior of the skull base is divided into three cranial fossae — the anterior, middle, and posterior cranial fossa — which increase in depth from front to back.

Parietal2Form upper, lateral skull sides; joined at midline by sagittal suture

Sutures of the Skull

A suture is an immobile joint between adjacent bones of the skull. The narrow gap between the bones is filled with dense, fibrous connective tissue that unites the bones. The four major sutures are:

SutureBones UnitedLocation
CoronalFrontal to parietal bonesAcross the top of the skull
SagittalRight and left parietal bonesMidline of the skull top
LambdoidParietal bones to occipital boneInverted V-shape at posterior skull
SquamousParietal to temporal boneLateral skull
Facial Bones

The facial skeleton consists of 14 bones: the paired maxillae, zygomatic, nasal, lacrimal, palatine, and inferior nasal conchae bones, plus the unpaired vomer and mandible. The mandible is the largest and strongest facial bone and the only moveable bone of the skull, forming the lower jaw.

Paranasal Sinuses

The paranasal sinuses are hollow, air-filled spaces found within the frontal, maxillary, sphenoid, and ethmoid bones only. They function to warm and humidify inhaled air, reduce skull weight, and serve as resonance chambers for speech. They are connected to the nasal cavity.

The Hyoid Bone

The hyoid bone is an independent bone that does not contact any other bone and thus is not part of the skull. It is a small U-shaped bone located in the upper neck near the level of the inferior mandible. The hyoid serves as the base for the tongue above, and is attached to the larynx below and to the pharynx posteriorly.

The Vertebral Column
The vertebral column consists of 24 vertebrae plus the sacrum and coccyx. It protects the spinal cord while supporting the head and body, and provides flexibility for movement.

Regions of the Vertebral Column

The vertebral column originally develops as a series of 33 vertebrae, but this number is eventually reduced to 24 vertebrae, plus the sacrum and coccyx. The vertebral column is subdivided into five regions, with the vertebrae in each area named for that region and numbered in increasing order from the superior to inferior direction.

RegionVertebraeCharacteristics
Cervical7 (C1–C7)Smallest; transverse foramina; C1 (atlas) supports skull; C2 (axis) has dens for head rotation; C3–C7 are typical
Thoracic12 (T1–T12)Costal facets for rib articulation; long spinous processes; heart-shaped bodies
Lumbar5 (L1–L5)Largest bodies; bear maximum body weight; short, blunt spinous processes
Sacrum5 fusedTriangular; transmits body weight to hip bones; sacral foramina for nerves
Coccyx4 fusedTailbone; small; muscle attachment site
Curvatures of the Vertebral Column

The adult vertebral column has four curvatures that increase its strength, flexibility, and ability to absorb shock. The thoracic and sacrococcygeal curvatures are primary curvatures — remnants of the original fetal curvature. The cervical and lumbar curvatures are secondary curvatures that develop after birth. Abnormal curvatures include kyphosis (excessive thoracic curve), lordosis (excessive lumbar curve), and scoliosis (abnormal lateral curvature).

Intervertebral Discs

An intervertebral disc is found between the bodies of adjacent vertebrae. Each disc is made up of two parts. The anulus fibrosus is the tough, fibrous outer layer that strongly unites the adjacent vertebrae to each other. The nucleus pulposus is the gel-like center of the disc that provides padding and allows for movements between the vertebrae. With increasing age, the water content of the nucleus pulposus gradually declines, contributing to the loss of body height in older individuals.

Ligaments of the Vertebral Column

Several ligaments support the vertebral column, including the anterior longitudinal ligament running along the front of the vertebral bodies, the posterior longitudinal ligament along the back, the ligamentum flavum connecting adjacent laminae, and the supraspinous ligament connecting spinous processes. In the neck, the supraspinous ligament expands into the nuchal ligament, which is found only in the cervical region.

Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 7.4
The Sternum

The sternum is the elongated bony structure that anchors the anterior thoracic cage. It consists of three parts: the manubrium, the body, and the xiphoid process. The manubrium is the wider, superior portion. The top of the manubrium has a shallow, U-shaped border called the jugular (suprasternal) notch. The clavicular notch on each side receives the sternal end of the clavicle.

The manubrium and body meet at the sternal angle, so called because the junction between these two components is not flat, but forms a slight bend. The sternal angle is an important anatomical landmark because the second rib attaches here. Since the first rib is hidden behind the clavicle, the second rib found at the sternal angle is used to count ribs.

The inferior tip of the sternum is the xiphoid process. This small structure is cartilaginous early in life, but gradually becomes ossified starting during middle age.

Ribs

Each rib is a curved, flattened bone that contributes to the wall of the thorax. The ribs articulate posteriorly with the T1–T12 thoracic vertebrae. At the posterior end, each rib has a head (which articulates with the vertebral body), a neck, and a tubercle (which articulates with the transverse process). The angle of the rib is the point at which the rib has its greatest degree of curvature. The inferior margin has the costal groove, a shallow groove for blood vessels and a nerve.

Classification of Ribs
ClassificationRibsAttachment

🔑
Floating Ribs are a Subset of False Ribs
All floating ribs (11–12) are also classified as false ribs. The key distinction: true ribs (1–7) attach directly to the sternum, false ribs (8–12) do not. Within the false ribs, floating ribs (11–12) have no sternal attachment whatsoever.

Development · Section 7.5
Embryonic Development of the Axial Skeleton
The axial skeleton forms during embryonic development through two distinct ossification processes. The skull, vertebrae, ribs, and sternum each follow unique developmental pathways.

Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 7.5
Skull Development

The skull develops through two distinct mechanisms. The bones of the brain case (top and sides of the skull) develop via intramembranous ossification, in which mesenchymal cells accumulate and differentiate directly into bone-producing osteoblasts. During fetal development, the brain case bones are separated by areas of fibrous connective tissue called fontanelles — the "soft spots" on an infant's head. Fontanelles allow the skull to compress during birth passage and accommodate rapid brain growth during infancy.

The bones at the base of the skull and the facial bones develop via endochondral ossification, in which mesenchymal cells differentiate into cartilage first, creating a hyaline cartilage model that is gradually replaced by bone. At birth, the brain case is disproportionately large relative to the underdeveloped facial bones. During early childhood, the mandible fuses from two halves, the mastoid processes enlarge, and the paranasal sinuses expand as teeth emerge.

Vertebral Column, Ribs, and Sternum

Vertebrae develop from sclerotome mesenchyme (the medial portion of the somites) that accumulates around the notochord. These cells form hyaline cartilage models that ossify through endochondral ossification. Small remnants of the notochord persist between vertebrae, contributing to the nucleus pulposus of the intervertebral discs.

Ribs initially develop as part of the vertebral cartilage models but separate during week eight in the thorax region. The sternum forms from paired cartilage models beginning at week five that eventually fuse at the midline and ossify.

⚠️
Homeostatic Imbalance: Craniosynostosis
Premature closure (fusion) of a suture line is a condition called craniosynostosis, affecting approximately 1 in 2,000 births. This abnormal fusion restricts skull growth in one direction, causing compensatory growth in other areas. It can produce scaphocephaly (long, narrow heads) and may require surgical intervention to restore normal development.

1Embryonic development of the axial skeleton involves ________.
  • A
    intramembranous ossification, which forms the facial bones
  • B
    endochondral ossification, which forms the ribs and sternum
  • C
    the notochord, which produces the cartilage models for the vertebrae
  • D
    the formation of hyaline cartilage models, which give rise to the flat bones of the skull
The ribs and sternum form via endochondral ossification (cartilage → bone). Facial bones also form via endochondral ossification (not intramembranous). The sclerotome (not notochord) produces cartilage models for vertebrae. The flat skull bones form via intramembranous ossification, not from cartilage models.
2A fontanelle ________.
  • A
    is the cartilage model for a vertebra that later is converted into bone
  • B
    gives rise to the facial bones and vertebrae
  • C
    is the rod-like structure that runs the length of the early embryo
  • D
    is the area of fibrous connective tissue found at birth between the brain case bones
A fontanelle is an area of fibrous connective tissue found at birth between the brain case bones — the "soft spots" on an infant's head. Option C describes the notochord.
