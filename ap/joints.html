Joints
The body contains 206 bones, and except for the hyoid, each is connected to at least one other bone. Joints are the points where bones meet — some prioritize stability, others prioritize mobility, but no joint can maximize both.

Joint Types · Section 9.1
Classification of Joints
Joints can be classified by structure (what connects the bones) or by function (how much movement is allowed). These two systems overlap — structural type often predicts functional type.

Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 9.1
Two Classification Systems

Joints can be classified by their structure (what type of connective tissue binds the bones, or whether a joint cavity is present) or by their function (how much movement the joint allows). The structural classification divides joints into fibrous (bones united by fibrous connective tissue), cartilaginous (bones joined by hyaline cartilage or fibrocartilage), and synovial (bones articulate within a fluid-filled joint cavity).

The functional classification divides joints into three categories based on the amount of movement allowed: synarthrosis (immobile), amphiarthrosis (slightly mobile), and diarthrosis (freely mobile). Diarthroses are further subdivided by how many axes of motion they allow: uniaxial (one plane), biaxial (two planes), and multiaxial (three or more planes).

🔑
Stability vs. Mobility Tradeoff
A joint cannot maximize both stability and mobility simultaneously. The most stable joints (skull sutures) allow no movement. The most mobile joints (shoulder) are the least stable and most prone to dislocation. This inverse relationship is a fundamental principle of joint design.

Fibrous Joints
At a fibrous joint, the adjacent bones are directly connected to each other by fibrous connective tissue, and thus the bones do not have a joint cavity between them.

tap

Three Types of Fibrous Joints

At a fibrous joint, the adjacent bones are directly connected to each other by fibrous connective tissue, and thus the bones do not have a joint cavity between them. The three types of fibrous joints are sutures, syndesmoses, and gomphoses.

Sutures

A suture is a narrow fibrous joint found between most bones of the skull. Dense fibrous connective tissue fills the narrow gap. In newborns, the wider areas of connective tissue are called fontanelles. As the skull grows, the fontanelles narrow into sutures. With increasing age, the fibrous tissue may ossify completely, converting the suture into a synostosis (bone-to-bone fusion).

Syndesmosis

A syndesmosis is a fibrous joint that unites bones with a sheet of fibrous connective tissue — either a ligament or an interosseous membrane. The gap between the bones may be narrow (joined by ligaments) or wide (filled by a broad sheet of connective tissue, called an interosseous membrane). Examples include the interosseous membrane between the radius and ulna in the forearm, and between the tibia and fibula in the leg.

Gomphosis

A gomphosis is the type of fibrous joint that anchors each tooth in its bony socket within the maxillary bone or mandible. The tooth is connected to the socket by periodontal ligaments. Gomphoses are functionally classified as synarthroses (immobile).

Joint Types · Section 9.3
Cartilaginous Joints
At a cartilaginous joint, the bones are joined by hyaline cartilage or fibrocartilage. These joints lack a joint cavity and range from immobile to slightly mobile.
⏱ ~15 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 9.3
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Describe the two types of cartilaginous joints
  • Distinguish between a synchondrosis and a symphysis
Key Vocabulary
Click Every Card to Flip It
Synchondrosis
tap
Cartilaginous joint where bones are united by hyaline cartilage. Example: epiphyseal plate, first sternocostal joint. Synarthrosis (immobile).
Symphysis
tap
Cartilaginous joint where bones are united by fibrocartilage. Example: intervertebral discs, pubic symphysis. Amphiarthrosis (slightly moveable).
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 9.3
Two Types of Cartilaginous Joints
Synchondrosis

A synchondrosis is a cartilaginous joint where the bones are joined by hyaline cartilage. These can be temporary or permanent. A temporary synchondrosis is the epiphyseal plate — the region of growing hyaline cartilage that unites the diaphysis to the epiphysis. When growth ceases, the cartilage is replaced by bone, forming a synostosis. A permanent synchondrosis is the first sternocostal joint, where the first rib is directly united to the manubrium by hyaline cartilage. Synchondroses are functionally classified as synarthroses (immobile).

Symphysis

At a symphysis, the bones are joined by fibrocartilage, which is very strong because it contains numerous bundles of thick collagen fibers. Symphyses are found at the pubic symphysis and at the intervertebral discs. The intervertebral disc provides both structural support and cushioning. The strong fibrocartilage unites adjacent bones firmly while still allowing limited movement, so symphyses are functionally classified as amphiarthroses (slightly moveable).

Joint Types · Section 9.4
Synovial Joints
Synovial joints are the most common type of joint in the body. They are characterized by a fluid-filled joint cavity, articular cartilage, and the greatest range of motion of any joint type.
⏱ ~35 min

Synovial Joint
tap
Joint where articulating surfaces are located within a joint cavity formed by an articular capsule. Always a diarthrosis (freely moveable).
Synovial Fluid
tap
Thick, lubricating fluid that fills the interior of a synovial joint. Reduces friction and nourishes avascular articular cartilage.
Articular Cartilage
tap
Thin layer of hyaline cartilage covering each bone's articulating surface. Functions like a Teflon coating for smooth, low-friction movement.
Bursa
tap
Thin connective tissue sac filled with lubricating liquid. Prevents friction between skin, muscle tendons, or ligaments and bone.
Meniscus
tap
Fibrocartilage pad that provides padding between bones at a synovial joint (e.g., the medial and lateral menisci of the knee).
Ligament
tap
Strong band of dense connective tissue spanning between bones. Supports and strengthens joints.
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 9.4
Structural Features of Synovial Joints

Synovial joints are characterized by a joint cavity enclosed by an articular capsule. Articular cartilage covers each bone's articulating surface, functioning like a Teflon coating for smooth movement. The synovial membrane lines the inner surface of the capsule and secretes synovial fluid, a thick, lubricating fluid that reduces friction and nourishes the avascular articular cartilage.

Ligaments provide direct support — they are classified as extrinsic (outside the capsule), intrinsic (fused to the capsule), or intracapsular (inside the capsule). Bursae are thin fluid-filled sacs that prevent friction between structures. Tendon sheaths are similar to bursae but wrap around tendons at joint crossings. Menisci and articular discs are fibrocartilage pads that cushion the joint.

Movement & Anatomy · Section 9.5
Types of Body Movements
Body movements are always described in relation to the anatomical position. Synovial joints allow many types of movement, from simple bending to complex circular motions.
⏱ ~25 min

Flexion
tap
Movement in the sagittal plane that decreases the angle of a joint (bends the joint).
Extension
tap
Movement in the sagittal plane that increases the angle of a joint (straightens the joint). Hyperextension = excessive extension beyond normal range.
Abduction
tap
Movement in the coronal plane that moves a limb laterally away from the body midline.
Adduction
tap
Movement in the coronal plane that moves a limb medially toward or across the body midline.
Circumduction
tap
Circular motion combining flexion, adduction, extension, and abduction sequentially. Occurs at multiaxial joints (shoulder, hip).
Supination / Pronation
tap
Supination: forearm rotation turning palm anteriorly (anatomical position). Pronation: forearm rotation turning palm posteriorly.

Shoulder vs. Hip — The Stability-Mobility Tradeoff in Action
The shoulder has the greatest range of motion of any joint but is the most commonly dislocated. Its shallow glenoid cavity sacrifices stability for mobility. The hip has a deep acetabulum reinforced by strong ligaments — much more stable, but with less range of motion. Both are ball-and-socket joints, but their structural differences perfectly illustrate the stability-mobility tradeoff.

Joint Formation

Joints develop in parallel with bone formation. In the skull, intramembranous ossification produces the bones of the top and sides of the skull. Initially separated by wide gaps (fontanelles), these bones grow after birth until the gaps narrow into suture joints. Endochondral ossification produces the bones at the base of the skull and all limb bones — mesenchyme first becomes hyaline cartilage, which is gradually replaced by bone.

Synovial joints develop at areas called the joint interzone between adjacent cartilage models in developing limbs. Cells at the center of the interzone region undergo cell death to form the joint cavity, while the surrounding mesenchyme differentiates into the articular capsule and supporting ligaments.

Ossification of cartilage models begins by week twelve. At birth, most bones are well ossified, but the epiphyseal plate cartilage persists through adolescence to allow continued bone lengthening. Articular cartilage remains permanently on synovial joint surfaces and is never replaced by bone.
