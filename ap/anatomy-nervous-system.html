The Embryologic Perspective
The nervous system originates from the ectoderm. The neural tube forms the CNS — its anterior end develops into the brain through a series of vesicle expansions, while the posterior portion becomes the spinal cord.

Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 13.1
From Neural Tube to Brain

The nervous system develops from the ectoderm. During week 4, the neural plate folds inward to create a neural groove, which closes to form the neural tube. The neural crest cells separate and migrate to form PNS structures (ganglia, Schwann cells) and non-neural tissues (craniofacial bone, melanocytes).

⚠️
Clinical: Spina Bifida
Spina bifida results from incomplete neural tube closure. Three types: occulta (mildest — vertebral defect only), meningocele (meninges protrude), and myelomeningocele (nerve tissue involved — most severe). Folic acid supplementation before and during pregnancy significantly reduces risk.

Development & CNS · Section 13.2
The Central Nervous System
The brain has four major regions: the cerebrum (thought and voluntary movement), diencephalon (relay and homeostasis), brain stem (vital functions), and cerebellum (coordination). The spinal cord connects the brain to the body.
⏱ ~35 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 13.2
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Name the four major regions of the brain
  • Identify the four lobes of the cerebral cortex and their functions
  • Describe the diencephalon, brain stem, and cerebellum
  • Describe the spinal cord in cross-section
Key Vocabulary
Click Every Card to Flip It
Cerebrum
tap
Largest brain region. Wrinkled cerebral cortex divided into 4 lobes. Controls voluntary movement, sensation, thought, language, memory.
Thalamus
tap
Major relay station of the diencephalon. Routes all sensory information (except olfaction) to the appropriate cortical area.
Hypothalamus
tap
Master regulator of homeostasis. Controls autonomic functions, endocrine system (via pituitary), temperature, hunger, thirst, sleep.
Cerebellum
tap
Located posterior to the brain stem. Compares motor commands with sensory feedback to coordinate smooth, accurate movements and balance.
Basal Nuclei
tap
Deep cerebral structures (caudate, putamen, globus pallidus) that regulate movement through direct and indirect pathways. Damage → Parkinson's disease.

The Four Lobes of the Cerebral Cortex
LobeLocationPrimary FunctionsKey Area
FrontalAnterior to central sulcusMotor commands, planning, judgment, personality, speech productionPrecentral gyrus (primary motor cortex), Broca's area (speech)
ParietalPosterior to central sulcusSomatosensory processing (touch, pain, temperature, proprioception)Postcentral gyrus (primary somatosensory cortex)
TemporalInferior, behind earsAuditory processing, memory, language comprehensionWernicke's area (language comprehension)
OccipitalPosteriorVisual processingPrimary visual cortex

The cortex's folding pattern (gyri = ridges, sulci = grooves) maximizes surface area, fitting approximately one square meter of cortical tissue within the cranial cavity. The longitudinal fissure divides the cerebrum into left and right hemispheres, connected by the corpus callosum.

Brain Stem

The midbrain, pons, and medulla oblongata collectively form the brain stem, connecting the brain to the spinal cord. The medulla regulates vital functions including respiration, heart rate, and blood pressure. The pons serves as a major input pathway to the cerebellum.

Spinal Cord

In cross-section, the spinal cord shows an inner H-shaped region of gray matter (neuron cell bodies) surrounded by outer white matter (myelinated axons in organized columns). The anterior horn contains motor neurons; the posterior horn receives sensory input; the lateral horn (thoracic/lumbar only) contains autonomic neurons.

Development & CNS · Section 13.3
Circulation & the Central Nervous System
The brain is protected by three meningeal layers, bathed in cerebrospinal fluid, and supplied by a redundant arterial system (the circle of Willis) that guards against stroke.
⏱ ~25 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 13.3
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Name and describe the three meningeal layers
  • Describe the ventricular system and CSF circulation
  • Explain the circle of Willis and its clinical significance
Key Vocabulary
Click Every Card to Flip It
Dura Mater
tap
Thick, fibrous outermost meningeal layer. Forms a strong protective sheath and supports the dural sinuses for venous drainage.
Arachnoid Mater
tap
Middle meningeal layer. Spider-web-like trabeculae span the subarachnoid space, which is filled with circulating CSF.
Cerebrospinal Fluid
tap
Clear fluid produced by choroid plexuses (~500 mL/day). Cushions the brain, removes waste, circulates through ventricles and subarachnoid space.
Circle of Willis
tap
Arterial anastomosis at the base of the brain connecting internal carotid and vertebral artery systems. Provides redundant blood supply if one vessel is blocked.
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 13.3
The Three Meninges
LayerPositionDescription
Dura materOutermostThick fibrous layer; attaches to skull; surrounds and supports dural venous sinuses for blood drainage
Arachnoid materMiddleLoose sac with web-like trabeculae; subarachnoid space filled with CSF
Pia materInnermostDelicate membrane adhering directly to CNS surface; follows every gyrus and sulcus
Ventricular System & CSF

The brain contains four ventricles: two lateral ventricles (in the cerebrum), the third ventricle (in the diencephalon), and the fourth ventricle (between the cerebellum and brain stem). They are connected by the interventricular foramina and the cerebral aqueduct. Choroid plexuses (ependymal cells) filter blood to produce approximately 500 mL of CSF per day. CSF cushions the brain, removes metabolic waste, and is reabsorbed into dural sinuses via arachnoid granulations.

Blood Supply

The brain receives blood from the internal carotid arteries (anterior supply) and vertebral arteries (posterior supply). These merge at the base of the brain to form the circle of Willis — a redundant arterial ring that can maintain brain perfusion even if one vessel is partially blocked. A stroke occurs when blood supply to a brain region is disrupted, depriving tissue of oxygen and glucose.

⚠️
Clinical: FAST for Stroke
Face drooping · Arm weakness · Speech difficulty · Time to call emergency services. Rapid treatment can minimize brain damage and save lives.

The internal carotid artery enters the cranium through the carotid canal to supply the anterior brain. The common carotid is in the neck; the jugular vein drains blood; the aorta is in the thorax.

  • A
    dura mater
  • B
    arachnoid mater
  • C
    subarachnoid
  • D
    pia mater
The dura mater is the thick fibrous outer layer that surrounds and supports the dural venous sinuses — channels that drain blood from the brain.
3What type of glial cell filters blood to produce CSF at the choroid plexus?
  • A
    ependymal cell
  • B
    astrocyte
  • C
    oligodendrocyte
  • D
    Schwann cell
Ependymal cells line the ventricles and, at the choroid plexus, filter blood to produce cerebrospinal fluid (~500 mL/day).
4Which ventricle is found within the diencephalon?
  • A
    lateral ventricles
  • B
    third ventricle
  • C
    cerebral aqueduct
  • D
    fourth ventricle
The third ventricle is located within the diencephalon, between the thalamus on each side. The lateral ventricles are in the cerebrum; the fourth ventricle is between the cerebellum and brain stem.
5What condition causes a stroke?
  • A
    inflammation of meninges
  • B
    lumbar puncture
  • C
    infection of cerebrospinal fluid
  • D
    disruption of blood to the brain
A stroke is caused by disruption of blood supply to the brain — either by a clot (ischemic stroke) or a ruptured vessel (hemorrhagic stroke), depriving brain tissue of oxygen and glucose.

The Peripheral Nervous System
The PNS consists of 12 pairs of cranial nerves and 31 pairs of spinal nerves. Spinal nerves reorganize into plexuses that distribute axons to specific body regions.

🔑
The Vagus Nerve — The Wanderer
The vagus nerve (CN X) is the only cranial nerve that does NOT stay in the head/neck region. It "wanders" into the thorax and abdomen, controlling heart rate, lung function, and digestive activity. It is the main nerve of the parasympathetic division.
