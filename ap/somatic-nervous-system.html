Sensory & Motor · Section 14.1
Sensory Perception
Sensation is the activation of sensory receptor cells at the level of the stimulus. Perception is the central processing of sensory stimuli into a meaningful pattern. The two are distinct processes.
⏱ ~30 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 14.1
🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Distinguish between sensation and perception
  • Classify receptors by stimulus type and location

Sensation vs. Perception
tap
Sensation = receptor activation by stimulus. Perception = central processing of stimuli into a meaningful pattern. You can sense without perceiving (background noise).
Nociceptor
tap
Sensory receptor that detects pain — tissue damage or potentially damaging stimuli. Free nerve endings found throughout the body.
Mechanoreceptor
tap
Receptor for mechanical stimuli: pressure, vibration, stretch, sound waves, balance. Includes touch receptors, hair cells of inner ear.
Proprioceptor
tap
Receptor that detects body position and movement. Located in muscles (muscle spindles), tendons (Golgi tendon organs), and joints.

Sensory modalities are divided into general senses (distributed throughout the body — touch, pain, temperature, proprioception) and special senses (dedicated organs — vision, hearing, taste, smell, balance).

Sensory & Motor · Section 14.2
Central Processing
Sensory information ascends through specific pathways to the somatosensory cortex, where the body is mapped onto the brain surface as a distorted "sensory homunculus" — with lips and fingers occupying disproportionately large areas.
⏱ ~25 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 14.2
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Describe the two major ascending sensory pathways
  • Explain the sensory homunculus and cortical mapping
  • Describe visual and auditory processing
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 14.2
Two Major Ascending Pathways
PathwayCarriesCrosses AtDestination
Dorsal ColumnTouch, proprioceptionBrainstem (medulla)Somatosensory cortex (postcentral gyrus)
SpinothalamicPain, temperatureSpinal cord levelSomatosensory cortex (postcentral gyrus)

Both pathways are three-neuron chains that cross the midline (decussate) — meaning the right side of the body is processed by the left cerebral cortex and vice versa. The key difference is where they cross: the dorsal column system crosses in the brainstem, while the spinothalamic tract crosses at the spinal cord level where the sensory neuron enters.

The Sensory Homunculus

The somatosensory cortex (postcentral gyrus) preserves a topographic map of the body surface — the sensory homunculus. However, the map is distorted: body regions with the highest receptor density (lips, fingertips, tongue) receive disproportionately larger cortical representation than less sensitive regions (back, shoulders). This reflects the importance of fine sensory discrimination in those areas.

Visual Processing

Visual information maintains topographic organization: the right visual field is processed in the left visual cortex. Remarkably, the cortex can adapt — subjects wearing inverting prisms initially saw everything upside-down, but after a few days of acclimation, perception normalized.

1Which sensory modality does NOT pass through the ventral posterior thalamus?
  • A
    gustatory
  • B
    proprioception
  • C
    audition
  • D
    nociception
Auditory (audition) information passes through the medial geniculate nucleus of the thalamus, not the ventral posterior nucleus. Touch, pain, taste, and proprioception all relay through the ventral posterior thalamus.
2Which nucleus in the medulla is connected to the inferior colliculus?
  • A
    solitary nucleus
  • B
    vestibular nucleus
  • C
    chief sensory nucleus
  • D
    cochlear nucleus
The cochlear nucleus receives auditory input from the inner ear and projects to the inferior colliculus (midbrain auditory relay) as part of the ascending auditory pathway.
3Visual stimuli in the upper-left visual field will be processed in what region of the primary visual cortex?
  • A
    inferior right
  • B
    inferior left
  • C
    superior right
  • D
    superior left
Visual processing is both contralateral (left field → right cortex) and inverted (upper field → inferior cortex). So upper-left visual field → inferior right visual cortex.
4Which location on the body has the largest region of somatosensory cortex representing it?
  • A
    lips
  • B
    thigh
  • C
    elbow
  • D
    neck
The lips have the largest cortical representation in the sensory homunculus — reflecting their extremely high density of touch receptors and importance in speech, eating, and facial expression.
5Which of the following is a direct target of the vestibular ganglion?
  • A
    superior colliculus
  • B
    cerebellum
  • C
    thalamus
  • D
    optic chiasm
The cerebellum receives direct input from the vestibular ganglion — balance and position information is essential for the cerebellum's role in coordinating movement and maintaining posture.

Sensory & Motor · Section 14.3
Motor Responses
Motor commands originate in the primary motor cortex, descend through the corticospinal tract, and activate lower motor neurons that innervate skeletal muscles. Reflexes bypass higher brain centers for rapid protective responses.
⏱ ~25 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 14.3
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Describe the descending motor pathways
  • Explain the motor homunculus
  • Describe the components of a reflex arc
Key Vocabulary
Click Every Card to Flip It
Corticospinal Tract
tap
Major descending motor pathway from primary motor cortex to spinal cord. Crosses at pyramidal decussation — right cortex controls left body.
Motor Homunculus
tap
Distorted body map on the precentral gyrus. Hands, face, and tongue have disproportionately large representations due to fine motor control demands.
Reflex Arc
tap
A rapid, automatic motor response to a stimulus. Components: receptor → sensory neuron → integration center (spinal cord) → motor neuron → effector (muscle).
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 14.3
Descending Motor Pathways

The primary motor cortex (precentral gyrus of the frontal lobe) generates voluntary motor commands. Like the somatosensory cortex, it is organized as a motor homunculus — with disproportionately large areas devoted to the hands, face, and tongue (areas requiring fine motor control). The prefrontal cortex handles executive functions and working memory. The premotor cortex and supplemental motor area plan and coordinate complex movements.

PathwayOriginControlsCrosses
Corticobulbar tractMotor cortexHead/neck muscles (via cranial motor nuclei)Ipsilateral (same side)
Lateral corticospinalMotor cortex (Betz cells)Limb muscles (appendicular)Pyramidal decussation (medulla) — contralateral
Anterior corticospinalMotor cortexTrunk/axial musclesSpinal cord level
Extrapyramidal Tracts

Additional descending pathways outside the pyramidal system include the tectospinal tract (postural responses to visual stimuli), reticulospinal tract (trunk, posture, muscle tone), and vestibulospinal tract (balance-related posture adjustments). The cerebellum compares motor commands with proprioceptive feedback and sends corrections via the rubrospinal tract.

Reflex Arcs

Reflexes are rapid, automatic motor responses that bypass higher brain centers. The withdrawal reflex protects the body from painful stimuli: a nociceptor detects the stimulus → sensory neuron carries signal to spinal cord → interneuron activates motor neuron → agonist muscle contracts while antagonist is simultaneously inhibited. This reciprocal inhibition ensures smooth, coordinated withdrawal. The entire reflex occurs in less than one second.

Section 14.3 Quiz — Motor Responses

5 questions

1Which region of the frontal lobe initiates movement by directly connecting to cranial and spinal motor neurons?
  • A
    prefrontal cortex
  • B
    supplemental motor area
  • C
    premotor cortex
  • D
    primary motor cortex
The primary motor cortex (precentral gyrus) directly initiates voluntary movement by sending commands through the corticospinal and corticobulbar tracts to motor neurons.
2Which extrapyramidal tract incorporates equilibrium sensations with motor commands for posture?
  • A
    tectospinal tract
  • B
    vestibulospinal tract
  • C
    reticulospinal tract
  • D
    corticospinal tract
The vestibulospinal tract incorporates balance (vestibular) information with motor commands to maintain posture and equilibrium during movement.
3Which region of gray matter in the spinal cord contains motor neurons innervating skeletal muscles?
  • A
    ventral horn
  • B
    dorsal horn
  • C
    lateral horn
  • D
    lateral column
The ventral (anterior) horn contains lower motor neuron cell bodies whose axons exit via ventral roots to innervate skeletal muscles.
4What type of reflex can protect the foot when a painful stimulus is sensed?
  • A
    stretch reflex
  • B
    gag reflex
  • C
    withdrawal reflex
  • D
    corneal reflex
The withdrawal reflex (flexor reflex) rapidly pulls the foot away from a painful stimulus by activating flexor muscles while simultaneously inhibiting extensor muscles.
5What is the name for the topographical representation of sensory input to the somatosensory cortex?
  • A
    homunculus
  • B
    homo sapiens
  • C
    postcentral gyrus
  • D
    primary cortex
The homunculus ("little man") is the distorted body map on the cortical surface — both sensory (postcentral gyrus) and motor (precentral gyrus) homunculi exist.
