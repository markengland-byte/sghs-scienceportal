Functions of the Skeletal System
The skeleton does far more than hold you upright. Its six major functions span mechanics, chemistry, and blood — making bone one of the most metabolically active organs in the body.

Hematopoiesis
tap
The production of blood cells (red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets) in the red bone marrow. Occurs primarily in flat bones (sternum, ribs, skull) and the epiphyses of long bones in adults.
Red Bone Marrow
tap
Highly vascular tissue found in spongy bone that produces blood cells (hematopoiesis). In adults, concentrated in flat bones and epiphyses of long bones. Infants have red marrow throughout most bones.
Yellow Bone Marrow
tap
Fat-storing tissue that fills the medullary cavity of long bone diaphyses in adults. Replaces red marrow during growth. Can convert back to red marrow during severe anemia or blood loss. Energy reserve.
Mineral Homeostasis
tap
The skeleton stores 99% of the body's calcium and 85% of its phosphorus. These minerals are released into or withdrawn from blood as needed, regulated by parathyroid hormone (PTH) and calcitonin. Bone acts as a mineral "bank."
Leverage
tap
Bones act as rigid levers that muscles pull on to produce movement. The joint is the fulcrum. This mechanical system allows muscles to generate large forces with small contractions — essential for locomotion.
Triglycerides (Bone)
tap
Fats stored in yellow bone marrow as an energy reserve (the 6th skeletal function). Can be mobilized during prolonged starvation. The marrow cavity essentially functions as a fat depot within the skeleton.

The Six Functions of Bone

According to OpenStax, "the skeletal system forms the rigid internal framework of the body. It consists of the bones, cartilages, and ligaments." But the skeleton is far more than scaffolding — it performs six distinct functions that span mechanics, chemistry, and blood cell production.

FunctionDescriptionAnatomical Example
1. SupportStructural framework for the body; supports soft tissues and organsVertebral column supports the trunk; femur supports body weight
2. ProtectionSurrounds and shields vital organs and the nervous systemSkull protects brain; rib cage protects heart and lungs; vertebrae protect spinal cord
3. MovementRigid levers pulled by muscles; joints allow articulationFemur + tibia + muscles → walking; radius + ulna → forearm rotation
4. Mineral StorageStores and releases calcium (99%) and phosphorus (85%) to maintain blood homeostasisOsteoclasts dissolve bone to release Ca²⁺ when blood calcium drops
5. Blood Cell ProductionRed bone marrow produces RBCs, WBCs, and platelets (hematopoiesis)Sternum, ribs, hip bones, proximal epiphyses of femur and humerus
6. Energy StorageYellow bone marrow stores triglycerides as an energy reserveMedullary cavity of long bone diaphyses in adults

Mnemonic: "S.P.M.M.H.E." — Some People Make Many Happy Endeavors
Support · Protection · Movement · Mineral storage · Hematopoiesis · Energy storage. The first three are mechanical; the last three are metabolic.

Lesson 1 Quiz — Functions of the Skeletal System

5 questions · includes radar chart interpretation

1Which skeletal function involves the production of red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets?
  • A
    Mineral storage
  • B
    Energy storage in yellow marrow
  • C
    Hematopoiesis — blood cell production in red bone marrow
  • D
    Support and leverage
Hematopoiesis is the production of all blood cells in red bone marrow. In adults, hematopoiesis occurs primarily in flat bones (sternum, ribs, hip bones) and the proximal epiphyses of the femur and humerus. A typical adult produces about 2 million red blood cells per second to replace those worn out after ~120 days of circulation.
2The skeleton stores 99% of the body's calcium. Which cells are responsible for RELEASING calcium from bone into the bloodstream when blood calcium levels fall?
  • A
    Osteoblasts — they build bone and release calcium as a byproduct
  • B
    Osteoclasts — they break down bone matrix, releasing stored calcium ions into the blood
  • C
    Osteocytes — they directly pump calcium out of lacunae
  • D
    Red marrow cells — they filter calcium from blood
Osteoclasts are the bone-resorbing cells that dissolve the mineralized matrix of bone, releasing calcium and phosphate ions into the bloodstream. They are activated by parathyroid hormone (PTH) when blood calcium drops. This is the mineral storage/release function of bone — the skeleton is literally a calcium bank that the body can withdraw from as needed.
3A patient suffers severe blood loss and needs to rapidly produce more red blood cells. Which type of bone marrow is most active, and in which bones is it concentrated in adults?
  • A
    Yellow marrow in the diaphyses of long bones
  • B
    Yellow marrow throughout all bones
  • C
    Red marrow in the diaphyses of long bones
  • D
    Red marrow in flat bones (sternum, ribs, skull) and proximal epiphyses of long bones
Red bone marrow is active in hematopoiesis. In adults it is concentrated in flat bones (sternum for bone marrow biopsies and aspirations, ribs, skull bones) and the proximal epiphyses of the femur and humerus. Yellow marrow (fat) fills the medullary cavity of long bone diaphyses in adults. In emergencies, yellow marrow can convert back to red marrow — this is called myeloid metaplasia.
4Bones function as levers in the musculoskeletal system. What serves as the FULCRUM in this lever system?
  • A
    The joint — it is the pivot point around which the bone (lever) moves when muscles apply force
  • B
    The muscle belly — it generates the force that acts as the fulcrum
  • C
    The periosteum — it transfers force from muscle to bone
  • D
    The diaphysis of the long bone
In the lever system of the musculoskeletal system: the bone is the lever, the joint is the fulcrum, the muscle provides the force (effort), and the load/resistance is whatever is being moved. There are three classes of levers in the body (first, second, third class), with third-class levers being most common — they maximize speed and range of motion at the cost of force.
5📊 Based on the radar chart, which two functions are classified as purely metabolic rather than mechanical?
  • A
    Support and protection
  • B
    Movement and mineral storage
  • C
    Hematopoiesis and energy storage — these involve blood cell production and fat storage, not force/movement
  • D
    Mineral storage and movement
The mechanical functions are: support (structural framework), protection (shields organs), and movement (levers + joints). The metabolic functions are: mineral storage (calcium/phosphorus bank), hematopoiesis (blood cell production), and energy storage (triglycerides in yellow marrow). The distinction matters clinically — bone diseases like osteoporosis primarily affect mechanical functions, while leukemia affects the metabolic (hematopoietic) function.

Bone Classification & Gross Anatomy
Bones come in five shapes, each suited to its function. The anatomy of a long bone reveals a beautifully organized structure — from the hard outer shell to the hollow marrow cavity within.
⏱ ~30 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 6.2
🎯 Lesson Objectives
  • Classify bones into five categories by shape with examples of each.
  • Identify the gross anatomical features of a long bone: diaphysis, epiphysis, metaphysis, articular cartilage, periosteum, endosteum, medullary cavity.
  • Explain the function of periosteum and endosteum.
  • Define bone markings (processes, depressions, openings) and explain their significance.
Key Vocabulary
Click Every Card to Flip It
Diaphysis
tap
The shaft of a long bone — the elongated, cylindrical central region. Composed primarily of compact bone surrounding the medullary cavity. The diaphysis provides strength to resist bending forces during weight-bearing.
Epiphysis
tap
The expanded ends (proximal and distal) of a long bone. Covered by articular cartilage at joint surfaces. Interior is spongy bone with red marrow in adults. Contains the epiphyseal plate (growth plate) in children.
Periosteum
tap
The dense fibrous membrane covering the outer surface of bone (except at joint surfaces). Two layers: outer fibrous (collagen, attachment for ligaments/tendons via Sharpey's fibers) and inner cellular (osteoblasts for growth and repair). Essential for bone healing.
Endosteum
tap
The thin membrane lining the internal surfaces of bone: medullary cavity walls, surfaces of trabeculae, and Haversian canals. Contains osteoblasts and osteoclasts for bone remodeling. Thinner than periosteum.
Medullary Cavity
tap
The hollow cylindrical space within the diaphysis of a long bone. Contains yellow marrow (fat) in adults. This hollow design reduces weight while maintaining strength — like the hollow core of an I-beam in engineering.
Epiphyseal Plate
tap
The "growth plate" — a layer of hyaline cartilage between the epiphysis and metaphysis in growing bones. Chondrocytes here proliferate, allowing bones to lengthen. When growth is complete (~late teens), it ossifies into the epiphyseal line. Fracture here in children can disrupt growth.

Periosteum vs. Endosteum
Periosteum = outer surface. Dense fibrous + osteoblasts. Anchors ligaments/tendons (Sharpey's fibers). Site of appositional (width) growth and fracture repair. Endosteum = inner surface (medullary cavity + trabeculae). Thinner. Also contains osteoblasts and osteoclasts for ongoing remodeling. Both are essential for bone health and repair.

Lesson 2 Quiz — Bone Classification & Gross Anatomy

5 questions

1The patella (kneecap) develops within the quadriceps tendon and protects it from friction. Which bone category does it belong to?
  • A
    Short bone
  • B
    Sesamoid bone — develops within a tendon to protect it and redirect the force of pull
  • C
    Irregular bone
  • D
    Flat bone
The patella is the largest sesamoid bone in the body. Sesamoid bones develop within tendons — the word "sesamoid" comes from the Greek for sesame seed, reflecting their typically small, round shape. The patella protects the quadriceps tendon from friction against the femur and increases the mechanical advantage of the quadriceps muscle by holding the tendon away from the joint axis.
2Which membrane covers the outer surface of bone, contains osteoblasts for growth and repair, and anchors ligaments and tendons via collagen fibers?
  • A
    Endosteum
  • B
    Articular cartilage
  • C
    Synovial membrane
  • D
    Periosteum — its outer fibrous layer anchors tendons/ligaments; its inner osteogenic layer contains osteoblasts for appositional growth and repair
The periosteum has two layers: (1) the outer fibrous layer of dense irregular connective tissue with collagen — attachment site for tendons and ligaments via Sharpey's fibers (perforating fibers that anchor deep into bone); (2) the inner osteogenic (cellular) layer containing osteoblasts that deposit new bone on the outer surface (appositional growth) and are essential for fracture repair. The periosteum is highly innervated — periosteal pain is intense (e.g., "shin splints").
3A child's X-ray shows a radiolucent (dark) band between the epiphysis and diaphysis of the femur. What is this structure, and what does it indicate?
  • A
    The epiphyseal plate (growth plate) — a layer of hyaline cartilage indicating the bone is still growing in length
  • B
    A fracture line running through the bone
  • C
    The endosteum — which appears dark on X-ray
  • D
    The medullary cavity extending into the epiphysis
The dark (radiolucent) band is the epiphyseal plate (growth plate) — composed of hyaline cartilage, which is less dense than bone and doesn't absorb X-rays as well. It appears in growing children and adolescents. Chondrocytes in the plate proliferate and are replaced by bone, pushing the epiphysis farther from the diaphysis (longitudinal growth). It ossifies into the epiphyseal line when growth is complete (~18-25 years). Fractures through the epiphyseal plate (Salter-Harris fractures) in children can disrupt growth and require careful management.
4The hollow medullary cavity design of long bones is often compared to engineering I-beams or hollow cylinders. Why is this design mechanically superior to a completely solid bone of the same mass?
  • A
    Hollow bones have more surface area for muscle attachment
  • B
    Hollow bones are more flexible and resist twisting forces better
  • C
    A hollow cylinder with the same mass as a solid cylinder has greater resistance to bending forces — material at the outer edge provides more mechanical advantage per unit weight; the hollow center saves weight without sacrificing much strength
  • D
    Hollow bones are easier to remodel since osteoclasts can access the interior more easily
Engineering principle: in bending, stress is highest at the outermost surfaces of a structure. A hollow cylinder concentrates material at the outer edge (where it matters most mechanically) and removes it from the center (where it contributes little to bending resistance). This is why steel pipes and bicycle frames are hollow — maximum stiffness for minimum weight. The medullary cavity also serves to house marrow, making the hollow design doubly advantageous biologically.
5📊 Based on the bone shape distribution graph, which category makes up the largest number of bones in the adult skeleton?
  • A
    Flat bones — because the skull has so many protective plates
  • B
    Long bones — the fingers and toes alone have 56 phalanges, and the hands/feet account for more than half of all bones in the body
  • C
    Irregular bones — because vertebrae and pelvic bones are very numerous
  • D
    Short bones — carpals and tarsals are very numerous
Long bones are the most numerous category. The hands and feet alone account for 106 of the 206 adult bones: each hand has 27 bones (8 carpals, 5 metacarpals, 14 phalanges) and each foot has 26 bones. The 14 phalanges per hand (56 total for all fingers and toes) are all long bones. This distribution reflects the importance of fine manipulation and locomotion in human anatomy.

Key Vocabulary
Click Every Card to Flip It
Osteon (Haversian System)
tap
The structural and functional unit of compact bone. Consists of concentric rings (lamellae) of mineralized matrix surrounding a central (Haversian) canal containing blood vessels and nerves. Runs parallel to the long axis of bone.
Osteoblast
tap
Bone-BUILDING cell. Secretes organic matrix (osteoid) and initiates mineralization. Found on bone surfaces (periosteum, endosteum). When trapped in matrix it secretes, it becomes an osteocyte. Think: "Blast = Build."
Osteoclast
tap
Bone-RESORBING cell. Large, multinucleated. Secretes acids and enzymes to dissolve bone matrix, releasing minerals. Derived from monocytes (immune cells). Activated by PTH. Works opposite to osteoblasts. Think: "Clast = Crush."
Osteocyte
tap
Mature bone cell trapped within the mineralized matrix in a lacuna. Communicates with neighboring osteocytes via long cytoplasmic processes in canaliculi. Senses mechanical stress (mechanosensor) and regulates bone remodeling. Most abundant bone cell.
Trabeculae
tap
The thin, interconnected rods and plates of bone matrix that make up spongy bone. Aligned along lines of mechanical stress. The open spaces between trabeculae are filled with red or yellow bone marrow. "Trabecular" comes from Latin for "little beam."
Canaliculi
tap
Tiny channels radiating outward from lacunae that contain the cytoplasmic processes of osteocytes. Allow osteocytes to exchange nutrients and wastes with blood vessels in the central canal. Create a communication network throughout compact bone.

The Osteon — Structural Unit of Compact Bone

According to OpenStax, "the basic structural unit of compact bone is the osteon, or Haversian system — a series of concentric rings, or lamellae, surrounding a central channel." Compact bone makes up the diaphysis of long bones and the outer shell of all bones — it is 70-80% mineralized and provides maximum strength.

Fig. 3.1 — Left: Cross-section of one osteon showing concentric lamellae, central canal, and osteocytes in lacunae. Right: Spongy bone trabeculae with marrow-filled spaces.

🏛️ Compact Bone

  • 80% of skeleton by mass
  • Made of tightly packed osteons
  • Forms diaphysis of long bones
  • Forms outer shell of all bones
  • Very dense — 70-80% mineralized
  • Primary function: strength & support

🕸️ Spongy (Cancellous) Bone

  • 20% of skeleton by mass
  • Made of trabeculae + marrow spaces
  • Found in epiphyses & flat bone interior
  • Lighter — absorbs compressive forces
  • Trabeculae align with stress lines
  • Houses red marrow (hematopoiesis)

Lesson 3 Quiz — Compact & Spongy Bone Tissue

5 questions · includes property comparison

1What is the structural and functional unit of compact bone?
  • A
    The trabecula
  • B
    The lacuna
  • C
    The osteon (Haversian system) — concentric lamellae surrounding a central canal with blood vessels
  • D
    The canaliculus
The osteon (Haversian system) is the basic structural unit of compact bone. It consists of: (1) a central canal containing blood vessels and nerves; (2) concentric lamellae of mineralized matrix around the canal; (3) osteocytes sitting in lacunae between lamellae; (4) canaliculi connecting osteocytes to the central canal and to each other. Multiple osteons run parallel to the long axis of the bone, creating its characteristic grain.
2An osteoblast that becomes completely surrounded by the matrix it secreted is called a(n):
  • A
    Osteocyte — it is now a mature bone cell living in a lacuna within the hardened matrix
  • B
    Osteoclast — bone resorption begins when the cell is surrounded
  • C
    Chondrocyte — cartilage cells replace trapped bone cells
  • D
    Periosteal cell — it migrates to the outer surface
When an osteoblast gets entrapped in the matrix it secretes, it differentiates into an osteocyte — the most abundant bone cell. The osteocyte lives in a small chamber called a lacuna. It maintains long cytoplasmic processes through channels called canaliculi that connect with neighboring osteocytes and with blood vessels in the central canal. Osteocytes are the mechanosensors of bone — they detect strain and orchestrate remodeling responses.
3Spongy bone trabeculae are NOT randomly arranged — they are aligned along specific directions. What determines this alignment?
  • A
    Genetic instructions that determine bone pattern before birth
  • B
    Blood vessel orientation, since trabeculae form around vascular channels
  • C
    Gravity alone — trabeculae always align vertically
  • D
    Lines of mechanical stress — trabeculae align along the directions of compressive and tensile forces experienced by that bone (Wolff's Law)
Wolff's Law: bone adapts its architecture to the mechanical stresses placed on it. Trabeculae align along the principal stress trajectories — the directions of maximum compressive and tensile force. In the femoral head, trabeculae form elegant arcs that direct body weight down the femoral neck. This is why the interior of the femoral head looks like structural engineering. Bones in people who exercise have denser, better-aligned trabeculae; bones in people who are immobilized lose trabecular mass (disuse osteoporosis).
4Which cell is responsible for bone RESORPTION, is multinucleated, and is stimulated by parathyroid hormone?
  • A
    Osteoblast
  • B
    Osteoclast — large multinucleated cell that secretes acid and enzymes to dissolve bone matrix
  • C
    Osteocyte
  • D
    Chondroblast
Osteoclasts are large, multinucleated cells (formed by fusion of monocytes) that resorb bone. They sit in depressions called Howship's lacunae (resorption pits) on the bone surface. They secrete carbonic acid (lowering pH to ~4.5) and proteolytic enzymes to dissolve the mineral and organic components of bone matrix. PTH stimulates osteoclast activity to release calcium when blood calcium drops. They work in opposition to osteoblasts to regulate bone mass.
5📊 Based on the property comparison chart, in which property does spongy bone EXCEED compact bone?
  • A
    Compressive strength — spongy bone handles more weight
  • B
    Mineral density — spongy bone is more heavily mineralized
  • C
    Metabolic activity (hematopoiesis) — spongy bone houses red marrow for blood cell production
  • D
    Resistance to bending — spongy bone is more flexible
Spongy bone exceeds compact bone in metabolic activity because its open spaces house red bone marrow for hematopoiesis. Compact bone is denser (higher mineral density), stronger in compression and bending, and heavier. Spongy bone is lighter, more energy-absorbing, and more metabolically active (blood cell production, calcium exchange surface area). The two tissue types complement each other — compact bone provides the structural shell, spongy bone provides metabolic activity and shock absorption.

Two Pathways to Bone

According to OpenStax, "ossification, or osteogenesis, is the process of bone formation by osteoblasts." All bone forms by one of two methods, depending on the tissue template used.

Intramembranous Ossification

  • Bone forms directly within fibrous connective tissue membrane
  • NO cartilage intermediate step
  • Forms flat bones: skull vault, clavicle, mandible
  • Steps: mesenchyme → osteoblast clusters → osteoid → calcification → spongy bone → compact bone added
  • Fontanelles (soft spots) are membrane not yet ossified
  • Sutures between skull plates close by ~25 years

Endochondral Ossification

  • Bone forms by replacing a hyaline cartilage model
  • Forms most bones: all long bones, most skull base bones
  • Steps: cartilage model → primary ossification center (diaphysis) → secondary centers (epiphyses) → epiphyseal plate remains for growth
  • Responsible for longitudinal (length) growth
  • Epiphyseal plate closes → bone stops lengthening

  • PTH reduces Ca²⁺ excretion in kidney

The Four Stages of Fracture Repair
StageTimelineWhat Happens
1. Hematoma FormationHours–DaysBlood fills fracture gap → clots → fracture hematoma. Blood supply disrupted; bone cells at break die. Hematoma acts as scaffold and releases growth factors (PDGF, TGF-β) to initiate repair.
2. Fibrocartilaginous Callus (Soft Callus)Days–WeeksFibroblasts and chondroblasts from periosteum invade hematoma. Collagen fibers and fibrocartilage replace the clot. Soft callus bridges the fracture gap — bone is stabilized but still weak.
3. Bony Callus (Hard Callus)3–4 WeeksOsteoblasts (from periosteum) replace fibrocartilage with woven bone (bony callus). Visible on X-ray as fluffy new bone around fracture site. Bone is now weight-bearing but wider than original.

🔑
Why the Periosteum Is Critical for Repair
The periosteum is the primary source of osteoblasts and chondroblasts for fracture repair. This is why periosteal stripping (removing periosteum during surgery) significantly impairs healing. Good blood supply (from the periosteum's outer fibrous layer) is also essential — avascular necrosis (bone death from poor blood supply) is a feared complication of certain fractures (e.g. femoral neck, scaphoid).

Anatomy of a Synovial Joint

All synovial joints share six features: (1) Articular cartilage — hyaline cartilage covering bone ends; reduces friction; no blood vessels/nerves. (2) Joint cavity — space containing synovial fluid. (3) Articular capsule — outer fibrous layer + inner synovial membrane. (4) Synovial membrane — secretes synovial fluid (lubricates + nourishes cartilage). (5) Reinforcing ligaments — attach bone to bone; stabilize joint. (6) Nerves and blood vessels (to capsule — not cartilage).
