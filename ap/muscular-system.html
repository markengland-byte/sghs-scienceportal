Principles · Section 11.1
Muscle Interactions, Fascicle Arrangement & Lever Systems
Skeletal muscles rarely work alone. They function in coordinated groups — agonists produce movement, antagonists oppose it, and synergists assist. Fascicle arrangement determines force and range of motion.
⏱ ~25 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 11.1
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Describe the roles of agonists, antagonists, synergists, and fixators
  • Compare the different fascicle arrangements
  • Explain the three classes of lever systems
Key Vocabulary

Agonist (Prime Mover)
tap
The principal muscle responsible for producing a specific movement. Example: biceps brachii during forearm flexion.
Antagonist
tap
Muscle that opposes the action of the agonist. Controls movement speed and provides braking force. Example: triceps during flexion.
Synergist
tap
Muscle that assists the agonist. A fixator is a synergist that stabilizes the bone of origin so the agonist can act more efficiently.
Origin & Insertion
tap
Origin: less moveable attachment (usually proximal). Insertion: more moveable attachment (usually distal). The muscle contracts to pull the insertion toward the origin.
Pennate
tap
Feather-like fascicle arrangement where fibers attach at an angle to a central tendon. Generates more force but less range of motion. Types: uni-, bi-, multipennate.
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 11.1
Muscle Roles in Movement

The agonist (prime mover) is the principal muscle involved in producing a specific movement. A synergist assists the prime mover — it can also act as a fixator, stabilizing the bone where the prime mover originates so the agonist can act more efficiently. The antagonist performs the opposite action and serves two critical roles: maintaining body position and controlling rapid movements by providing a braking force.

Lever Systems

Bones serve as rigid levers, joints as fulcrums, muscles as the effort force, and the object being moved as the load. Most body lever systems are third-class levers (effort between fulcrum and load) — they sacrifice mechanical advantage for speed and range of motion.

Principles · Section 11.2
Naming Skeletal Muscles
Skeletal muscles are named based on multiple criteria including size, shape, fiber direction, location, number of origins, and action. Understanding these conventions makes learning muscle names logical rather than arbitrary.
⏱ ~15 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 11.2
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Describe the criteria used to name skeletal muscles
  • Apply naming conventions to identify muscle characteristics
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 11.2

Naming CriterionLatin/Greek TermsExample
Sizemaximus (largest), medius, minimus (smallest), longus (long), brevis (short)Gluteus maximus, adductor longus

Attachment sitesOrigin-to-insertion namingSternocleidomastoid (sternum + clavicle → mastoid)
Actionflexor, extensor, abductor, adductor, levator, depressorFlexor carpi radialis, extensor digitorum
🧠
Decoding Muscle Names
Most muscle names combine multiple criteria. For example, extensor digitorum longus tells you: action (extensor = extends), body part (digitorum = fingers/toes), and size (longus = long). Once you learn the Latin roots, muscle names become self-describing.

Axial Muscles · Section 11.3
Axial Muscles of the Head, Neck & Back
The muscles of facial expression are unique — they insert into the skin rather than bone. Muscles of mastication move the jaw, while the sternocleidomastoid and erector spinae control head and trunk movement.
⏱ ~30 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 11.3
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Identify the muscles of facial expression and their unique insertion
  • Describe the muscles of mastication, the tongue, and eye movement
  • Identify the muscles of the neck and back
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 11.3
Muscles of Facial Expression

The muscles of facial expression are unique because they insert into the skin rather than bone. When they contract, the skin moves to create facial expressions. Key muscles include the orbicularis oris (lips — kissing, whistling), orbicularis oculi (eyes — blinking, squinting), occipitofrontalis (raises eyebrows — connected by the epicranial aponeurosis), buccinator (compresses cheeks — chewing, blowing), and corrugator supercilii (frowning).

Muscles of Mastication

The masseter is the prime mover for jaw elevation (closing the mouth). It is one of the strongest muscles in the body relative to its size. The temporalis assists in elevation and retracts the jaw. The medial and lateral pterygoid muscles produce side-to-side grinding motions.

Neck and Back Muscles

The sternocleidomastoid is a prime mover in head flexion — when both sides contract, the head flexes forward; when one side contracts, the head rotates to the opposite side. The erector spinae group (iliocostalis, longissimus, spinalis) provides postural support and extends the vertebral column.

Axial Muscles · Section 11.4
Axial Muscles of the Abdominal Wall & Thorax
The four-layered abdominal wall compresses and supports the abdominal organs. The diaphragm — a dome-shaped sheet of skeletal muscle — is the primary muscle of respiration.
⏱ ~25 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 11.4
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Identify the muscles of the anterior abdominal wall
  • Describe the diaphragm and intercostal muscles
  • Describe the muscles of the pelvic floor
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 11.4
Abdominal Wall Muscles

The anterior and lateral abdominal wall contains four paired muscles arranged in layers from superficial to deep: the external obliques (fibers run inferomedially — "hands in pockets" direction), internal obliques (fibers run perpendicular to external obliques), transversus abdominis (fibers run transversely — "like the front of a belt"), and rectus abdominis (paired vertical muscles segmented by tendinous intersections). The linea alba is where the bilateral rectus sheaths join at the midline.

Thoracic Muscles

The diaphragm separates the thoracic and abdominal cavities and is dome-shaped at rest. It is the primary muscle of respiration — when it contracts, it flattens, increasing thoracic volume for inhalation. The external intercostals aid inspiration by elevating the rib cage, while the internal intercostals assist in forced expiration by pulling ribs together.

Pelvic Floor

The levator ani (pubococcygeus and iliococcygeus) is the most important muscle of the pelvic floor, supporting the pelvic viscera. The perineum is divided into urogenital and anal triangles.

Clinical: The Rotator Cuff
The rotator cuff consists of four muscles — supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, and subscapularis (SITS) — whose tendons fuse with the shoulder joint capsule. They are the primary stabilizers of the shoulder. Rotator cuff tears are common in throwing athletes and overhead workers, causing pain and weakness with arm elevation.
