The Endocrine System
The endocrine system uses chemical messengers — hormones — transported through the bloodstream to regulate growth, metabolism, reproduction, and homeostasis. It works alongside the nervous system as the body's two long-distance communication systems.

Foundations · Section 17.1
An Overview of the Endocrine System
The endocrine system uses ductless glands to secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream. Unlike the fast, targeted nervous system, endocrine signaling is slower but produces longer-lasting, widespread effects.
⏱ ~15 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 17.1
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Distinguish between endocrine and exocrine glands
  • Compare endocrine and nervous system signaling
  • Define autocrine and paracrine signaling

Endocrine Gland
tap
Ductless gland that secretes hormones directly into the bloodstream for transport to distant target cells.
Exocrine Gland
tap
Gland that releases secretions through ducts to body surfaces or cavities (e.g., sweat glands, salivary glands). NOT part of the endocrine system.
Paracrine Signaling
tap

The major endocrine glands include the pituitary, thyroid, parathyroid, adrenal, and pineal glands. Additionally, the hypothalamus, pancreas, thymus, heart, kidneys, and reproductive organs contain endocrine cells. Even adipose tissue and bone have been shown to produce hormones.

Endocrine glands are ductless glands that secrete hormones directly into surrounding fluid for bloodstream transport. This contrasts with exocrine glands (like sweat and salivary glands) that release secretions through ducts. Beyond endocrine signaling, autocrine signaling affects the same cell that produced the signal, and paracrine signaling affects nearby cells.

Foundations · Section 17.2
Hormones
Hormones are classified by their chemical structure into amino acid-derived, peptide, and lipid-derived (steroid) types. Their structure determines how they travel in blood, how they signal target cells, and how long their effects last.
⏱ ~25 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 17.2
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Classify hormones by chemical structure
  • Distinguish between intracellular and membrane receptor mechanisms
  • Explain the cAMP second messenger system
  • Describe negative feedback regulation
Key Vocabulary
Click Every Card to Flip It
Steroid Hormone
tap
Lipid-derived hormone from cholesterol. Hydrophobic — diffuses through cell membrane to bind intracellular receptors. Long half-life (60–90 min for cortisol).
Second Messenger
tap
Intracellular signaling molecule (e.g., cAMP) activated when a water-soluble hormone binds a membrane receptor. Amplifies the hormonal signal inside the cell.
Negative Feedback
tap
Primary mechanism controlling hormone secretion. Rising hormone levels inhibit further release, preventing excess. Example: high cortisol inhibits ACTH release.
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 17.2
Three Chemical Classes of Hormones
ClassSourceSolubilityReceptor LocationExamples
Amino acid-derivedTryptophan or tyrosineWater-soluble (most)Cell membraneMelatonin, epinephrine, thyroid hormones
Peptide/ProteinAmino acid chainsWater-solubleCell membraneInsulin, GH, ADH, oxytocin
Lipid-derived (Steroid)CholesterolLipid-solubleIntracellular (cytosol/nucleus)Cortisol, aldosterone, testosterone, estrogen
Two Receptor Mechanisms

Lipid-soluble hormones (steroids) diffuse through the cell membrane and bind intracellular receptors in the cytosol or nucleus. The hormone-receptor complex binds DNA directly and initiates gene transcription — producing new proteins. This is slow but produces long-lasting effects.

Water-soluble hormones cannot cross the membrane. They bind cell surface receptors, activating intracellular second messenger systems. The most common is the cAMP pathway: hormone binds receptor → G protein activates → adenylyl cyclase converts ATP to cAMP → protein kinases activate → phosphorylation cascade → cellular response. This is fast but short-lived.

🔑
Negative Feedback — The Master Regulator
Almost all hormone secretion is controlled by negative feedback. As hormone levels rise, the hypothalamus and/or pituitary reduce their signaling, preventing overproduction. Example: rising cortisol inhibits both CRH (hypothalamus) and ACTH (anterior pituitary).

Major Glands · Section 17.3
The Pituitary Gland & Hypothalamus
The hypothalamus-pituitary complex is the command center of the endocrine system. The anterior pituitary produces six major hormones; the posterior pituitary stores and releases two hormones made by the hypothalamus.
⏱ ~35 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 17.3
🎯 Learning Objectives

tap
Manufactures 6 hormones (GH, TSH, ACTH, FSH, LH, PRL) under hypothalamic control via the hypophyseal portal system.
Posterior Pituitary
tap
Neural tissue that stores and releases 2 hormones (ADH, oxytocin) made by hypothalamic neurons. Does NOT manufacture hormones.
Growth Hormone (GH)

🔑
The Posterior Pituitary Produces ZERO Hormones
A common misconception: the posterior pituitary does not manufacture hormones. ADH and oxytocin are synthesized by hypothalamic neurons and transported down axons to the posterior pituitary for storage and release. The posterior lobe is neural tissue, not glandular.

Major Glands · Section 17.4
The Thyroid & Parathyroid Glands
The butterfly-shaped thyroid gland controls metabolism via T3 and T4, while the tiny parathyroid glands regulate blood calcium via PTH. Together with calcitonin, they maintain calcium homeostasis.
⏱ ~25 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 17.4–17.5
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Describe thyroid hormone synthesis and regulation
  • Explain the role of PTH and calcitonin in calcium homeostasis
  • Describe thyroid and parathyroid disorders
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 17.4–17.5
Thyroid Hormones

The thyroid gland is a butterfly-shaped organ located anterior to the trachea, just inferior to the larynx. Its functional units are thyroid follicles containing colloid. Thyroid hormones (T3 — triiodothyronine, and T4 — thyroxine) are formed when iodine attaches to thyroglobulin protein. T3 is more potent than T4. Thyroid hormones regulate basal metabolic rate, increase nutrient breakdown and ATP production, and support nervous system development. Calcitonin is produced by parafollicular cells and decreases blood calcium by inhibiting osteoclasts.

Parathyroid Hormone (PTH)

The four tiny parathyroid glands are embedded in the posterior surface of the thyroid. Chief cells secrete PTH, the major hormone regulating blood calcium. When blood calcium drops, PTH stimulates osteoclasts (bone breakdown → calcium release), increases kidney calcium reabsorption, and activates vitamin D (calcitriol) for intestinal calcium absorption.

ConditionCauseSymptoms
HypothyroidismLow T3/T4Weight gain, fatigue, cold intolerance, constipation
HyperthyroidismExcess T3/T4 (Graves' disease)Weight loss, heat intolerance, tremors, rapid heart rate
GoiterIodine deficiency → thyroid enlargementVisible neck swelling
HyperparathyroidismExcess PTHBone loss, fractures, kidney stones

Major Glands · Section 17.5
The Adrenal Glands
Each adrenal gland has two distinct regions: the cortex (steroid hormones — aldosterone, cortisol, androgens) and the medulla (catecholamines — epinephrine, norepinephrine). They sit atop the kidneys and receive one of the highest rates of blood flow in the body.
⏱ ~25 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 17.6
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Describe the three zones of the adrenal cortex and their hormones
  • Explain the function of the adrenal medulla
  • Describe Cushing's disease and Addison's disease
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 17.6
Adrenal Cortex — Three Zones
ZoneLocationHormone ClassKey HormoneFunction
Zona GlomerulosaOuterMineralocorticoidsAldosteroneNa+ retention, K+ excretion; RAAS system; blood pressure
Zona FasciculataMiddleGlucocorticoidsCortisolGlucose mobilization, anti-inflammatory, stress response; HPA axis

Adrenal Medulla

The adrenal medulla contains chromaffin cells that release epinephrine and norepinephrine in response to sympathetic nervous system stimulation. These catecholamines trigger glycogen-to-glucose conversion, increase heart rate and blood pressure, and dilate airways — the acute fight-or-flight response via the SAM pathway (sympathomedullary).

⚠️
Clinical: Cushing's vs. Addison's
Cushing's disease = cortisol hypersecretion → high blood glucose, fat deposits (moon face, buffalo hump), muscle wasting, thin skin. Addison's disease = corticosteroid hyposecretion → low blood glucose, low sodium, dehydration, hyperpigmentation, fatigue.

Other Glands · Section 17.6
Pineal Gland, Gonads & Placental Hormones
The pineal gland regulates circadian rhythm via melatonin. The gonads produce steroid sex hormones. During pregnancy, the placenta becomes a temporary endocrine organ producing hCG, relaxin, and other hormones.
⏱ ~20 min
📖 OpenStax A&P 2e, 17.7–17.8
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Describe melatonin and its role in circadian rhythm
  • List the gonadal hormones and their functions
  • Describe the hormones of the placenta
Core Content — OpenStax A&P 2e, 17.7–17.8
Pineal Gland

The pineal gland is a tiny endocrine gland located inferior and posterior to the thalamus. Pinealocytes produce and secrete melatonin, derived from serotonin. Melatonin secretion increases in darkness and decreases with bright light exposure, regulated via the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus. Melatonin influences circadian rhythms — the dark-light fluctuations affecting sleepiness, appetite, and body temperature.

Gonadal Hormones
HormoneSourceFunctions
TestosteroneTestesMale reproductive development, sperm maturation, secondary sex characteristics (voice, body hair, muscle mass)
EstrogensOvariesFemale reproductive development, menstrual cycle regulation, secondary sex characteristics, pregnancy maintenance
ProgesteroneOvaries (corpus luteum)Prepares uterus for pregnancy, maintains pregnancy, menstrual cycle regulation
InhibinGonads (both sexes)Suppresses FSH secretion from anterior pituitary
Placental Hormones

Human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) promotes progesterone synthesis and is the hormone detected by pregnancy tests. Human placental lactogen (hPL) prepares breasts for lactation. Relaxin helps soften and widen the pubic symphysis in preparation for childbirth.

The pancreatic islets regulate blood glucose via insulin (lowers) and glucagon (raises). Several other organs — heart, kidneys, GI tract, thymus, and adipose tissue — also have secondary endocrine functions.

Other Glands · Section 17.8
Development & Aging of the Endocrine System
Endocrine glands originate from all three embryonic germ layers. With aging, many glands decrease output — leading to reduced metabolism, bone loss, decreased fertility, and impaired glucose tolerance.

  • B
    decreased basal metabolic rate
  • C

Decreased thyroid function lowers the basal metabolic rate, leading to weight gain and increased body fat. It would cause decreased cold tolerance (not increased) and increased fatigue.

EPO (erythropoietin) stimulates red blood cell production in bone marrow. More RBCs = more oxygen-carrying capacity = improved endurance. This is why EPO doping is banned in sports.
