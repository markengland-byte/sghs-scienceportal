Overview of the Neurological Exam
The neurological exam is a clinical assessment tool used to quickly evaluate neurological function and determine if specific parts of the nervous system have been affected by damage or disease. It can be completed in as little as 5 minutes in an emergency setting.

The Five Major Subtests

The neurological exam is a series of subtests that assess the function of specific regions of the nervous system. The examination consists of five major subtests: the mental status exam (assessing higher cognitive functions), the cranial nerve exam (testing the 12 cranial nerves), the sensory exam (testing sensory functions of spinal nerves), the motor exam (testing motor functions of spinal nerves), and the coordination exam (assessing complex, coordinated movements). The gait exam, often considered a sixth component, specifically evaluates walking mechanics.

Neuroanatomy and the Neurological Exam

Localization of function is the concept that circumscribed locations are responsible for specific functions. The five major examination sections correspond to CNS regions: mental status relates to cerebrum function; cranial nerve testing assesses diencephalon and brain stem connections; coordination and gait exams primarily evaluate cerebellar function; motor and sensory exams assess spinal cord and spinal nerve connections.

Figure 16.1 — The different regions of the CNS relate to the major sections of the neurological exam: the mental status exam, cranial nerve exam, sensory exam, motor exam, and coordination exam (including the gait exam).
Causes of Neurological Deficits

Neurons deteriorate within 1–2 minutes without oxygen, with permanent damage potentially occurring within hours. Blood flow loss to brain regions constitutes a stroke or cerebrovascular accident (CVA).

An ischemic stroke is the loss of blood flow to an area because vessels are blocked or narrowed. Causes include embolus (blood clots or fat deposits), vessel wall thickening, or hypovolemia. A transient ischemic attack (TIA) presents similarly but resolves within 24 hours; strokes produce effects lasting 24+ hours.

A hemorrhagic stroke is bleeding into the brain because of a damaged blood vessel. Accumulated blood exerts physical pressure on brain tissue, compresses local arteries, and may cause edema as the blood-brain barrier breaks down.

Figure 16.2 — (a) A hemorrhage into the tissue of the cerebrum results in a large accumulation of blood with additional edema. (b) A CT scan shows an intraparenchymal hemorrhage within the parietal lobe.

Other causes of neurological deficits include: infectious diseases that trigger astrocyte and microglia reactions throughout the CNS; blunt force trauma that physically damages nervous tissue; and neurodegenerative diseases (Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, Huntington's, ALS, multiple sclerosis) that cause progressive nervous tissue degeneration.

🔑
Ischemic vs. Hemorrhagic Stroke
An ischemic stroke results from blocked blood flow (clot, embolus, narrowing). A hemorrhagic stroke results from bleeding into the brain. Both produce neurological deficits, but hemorrhagic strokes often cause more severe damage due to physical pressure on brain tissue and edema from blood-brain barrier breakdown.

The Mental Status Exam
The mental status exam assesses cognitive functions of the cerebrum — orientation and memory, language and speech, sensorium, and judgment and abstract reasoning. It reveals the relationship of the brain to the body through behavioral assessment.

Functions of the Cerebral Cortex

The cerebral cortex is the thin layer of gray matter on the outside of the cerebrum. On average, it is approximately 2.55 mm thick and highly folded to fit within the limited space of the cranial vault. The German neurologist Korbinian Brodmann described approximately 50 regions of the cortex that differed enough from each other to be considered separate areas based on cytoarchitecture.

Figure 16.3 — On the basis of cytoarchitecture, Brodmann described the extensive array of cortical regions. Subsequent investigations found that these areas corresponded very well to functional differences in the cerebral cortex.

Areas 1, 2, 3, 4, 17, and 22 are each described as primary cortical areas. The adjoining regions are each referred to as association areas. Integration areas are found in the spaces between the domains for particular sensory or motor functions, and they integrate multisensory information or process sensory or motor information in more complex ways.

Figure 16.4 — The cerebral cortex contains three types of processing regions: primary, association, and integration areas.
Orientation and Memory

Orientation is the patient's awareness of their immediate circumstances — awareness of time, place, and personal identity. Memory is largely a function of the temporal lobe, along with structures beneath the cerebral cortex such as the hippocampus and the amygdala.

Henry Molaison (patient HM) had both medial temporal lobes removed to treat intractable epilepsy. This resulted in anterograde amnesia — the inability to form new episodic memories — while retaining short-term memory and procedural memory. This demonstrated that the hippocampus is essential for consolidating short-term memories (in the prefrontal lobe) into long-term memory (in the temporal lobe).

Language and Speech

Broca's area (frontal lobe) is associated with speech production — damage causes expressive aphasia (non-fluent, broken speech). Wernicke's area (posterior temporal lobe) is associated with language comprehension — damage causes receptive aphasia (inability to understand language). Conduction aphasia results from damage to the white matter tracts connecting these two regions.

Figure 16.5 — Two important integration areas associated with language function are Broca's and Wernicke's areas. They are connected through deep white matter running from the posterior temporal lobe to the frontal lobe.
Sensorium — Praxis and Gnosis

Praxis is a practical exercise in which the patient performs a task completely on the basis of verbal description without any demonstration. Gnosis involves two tasks: stereognosis (naming objects by touch alone with eyes closed) and graphesthesia (recognizing numbers or letters traced on the palm).

Judgment and Abstract Reasoning

The prefrontal cortex is responsible for planning and making decisions. Testing includes problem solving ("If you see a house on fire, what would you do?"), interpreting proverbs ("Don't look a gift horse in the mouth"), and comparing word pairs for similarities (apple and orange).

🧠
Three Types of Memory
Short-term (working) memory — prefrontal lobe, holds info briefly. Episodic memory — autobiographical long-term memories stored in temporal lobe. Procedural memory — motor skills ("how to"), preserved even when episodic memory is lost (as in patient HM).

The Cranial Nerve Exam
The twelve cranial nerves serve the head and neck, controlling special senses, gaze, facial expression, swallowing, and parasympathetic functions. Tests of these nerves provide insight into damage to specific regions of the brain stem.

Functional Groupings of Cranial Nerves

Three of the nerves are strictly responsible for special senses (I olfactory, II optic, VIII vestibulocochlear). Three nerves control the extraocular muscles for gaze (III oculomotor, IV trochlear, VI abducens). Four nerves connect to muscles of the face, oral cavity, and pharynx (V trigeminal, VII facial, IX glossopharyngeal, XII hypoglossal). Four nerves make up the cranial component of the parasympathetic nervous system (III, VII, IX, X).

Sensory Nerves

Testing smell (CN I) is straightforward — common smells are presented to one nostril at a time. Loss of smell is called anosmia. Testing vision (CN II) uses the Snellen chart for visual acuity. The optic nerves meet at the optic chiasm where fibers sort so that the two halves of the visual field are processed by the opposite sides of the brain.

Figure 16.6 — The pituitary gland is immediately inferior to the optic chiasm. A pituitary tumor can press against the crossing fibers, causing "tunnel vision" — loss of peripheral visual field.

The vestibulocochlear nerve (CN VIII) carries both equilibrium and auditory sensations. The Rinne test distinguishes conductive from sensorineural hearing loss using a tuning fork on the mastoid process then next to the ear. The Weber test places the tuning fork on top of the skull to test laterality of hearing loss.

Gaze Control

The medial longitudinal fasciculus (MLF) allows for conjugate gaze by connecting the oculomotor, trochlear, and abducens nuclei. Failure of one eye to abduct while the other adducts is called internuclear ophthalmoplegia. The accommodation–convergence reflex coordinates medial rectus contraction (convergence) with ciliary body adjustment (focusing) when viewing near objects.

Figure 16.7 — If the head is turned in one direction, the VOR coordinates vestibular sense with eye movement nuclei through the MLF to stabilize the visual field.
🔑
The Trigeminal System
The trigeminal nerve (CN V) is the head's equivalent of the spinal sensory pathways. The chief nucleus (pons) receives light touch — analogous to the dorsal column pathway. The spinal trigeminal nucleus (medulla) receives pain and temperature — analogous to the spinothalamic pathway.

The Sensory & Motor Exams
Connections between the body and the CNS occur through the spinal cord. The sensory and motor exams test dermatomes, ascending tracts, muscle strength, reflexes, and the distinction between upper and lower motor neuron disease.

Sensory Testing

The sensory exam tests dermatomes — topographically organized skin regions innervated by specific spinal nerves. A cotton-tipped applicator tests light touch (relayed by the dorsal column pathway), while the broken pointed end tests pain (relayed by the spinothalamic pathway). Vibration is tested with a tuning fork on bone prominences; proprioception is tested by moving fingers or toes.

Figure 16.8 — The surface of the skin can be divided into topographic regions (dermatomes) that relate to the location of sensory endings based on the spinal nerve that contains those fibers.

The dorsal column pathway conveys fine touch, vibration, and proprioceptive information — ascending ipsilateral to the stimulus and decussating in the medulla. The spinothalamic pathway primarily conveys pain and temperature — decussating at the level of entry and ascending contralateral to the source. This segregation means different sensory deficits localize to different spinal cord injuries.

Motor Testing — UMN vs. LMN

The corticospinal tract represents the upper motor neuron (UMN) projecting from the precentral gyrus through the brain stem to the spinal cord. The ventral horn motor neuron is the lower motor neuron (LMN) projecting to skeletal muscle. Voluntary movements require both cells to be active.

Spinal Cord Hemisection

A hemisection damages tracts on one side. On the paralyzed leg: the patient acknowledges painful stimuli but not fine touch or proprioception (dorsal column damaged ipsilateral, but spinothalamic fibers from that side crossed below the injury). On the functional leg: the opposite is true — fine touch and proprioception are intact but pain sensation is lost (contralateral spinothalamic fibers are cut).

⚠️
Clinical: The Babinski Sign
The plantar reflex normally causes toe curling (negative Babinski) in adults due to corticospinal tract modulation. In infants (whose corticospinal tract is immature), the response is dorsiflexion with toe splaying (positive Babinski). In adults, a positive Babinski sign indicates a UMN lesion.

Location and Connections of the Cerebellum

The cerebellum is located in apposition to the dorsal surface of the brain stem, centered on the pons. Three cerebellar peduncles connect it to the brain stem:

PeduncleConnects ToFunction
Inferior (ICP)Medulla, inferior oliveSensory input — proprioceptive feedback from spinocerebellar tract
Middle (MCP)PonsMotor commands from cerebral cortex via cortico-ponto-cerebellar pathway
Superior (SCP)Midbrain, thalamusMajor output — to red nucleus and back to motor cortex

Cerebellar Regions

The vermis (spinocerebellum) receives proprioceptive input from the spinal cord and controls axial musculature (balance, posture, gait). The flocculonodular lobe (vestibulocerebellum) receives vestibular input for balance. The lateral hemispheres (cerebrocerebellum) receive cerebral cortex input and control appendicular musculature (limb movements, motor planning).

Figure 16.10 — The cerebellum divided into midline (vermis + flocculonodular lobe) and hemispheres (lateral regions).
Coordination Tests

The finger-to-nose test has the patient touch their finger to the examiner's finger and then to their nose, back and forth. This tests the cerebrocerebellum's ability to coordinate flexion and extension around joints using visual and proprioceptive feedback. Rapid alternating movements — touching each finger to the thumb, or patting the hand back and forth — test the ability to switch between antagonistic muscle groups.

Posture and Gait

Station begins with the patient standing in normal position (eyes open) to check balance. Tandem gait is walking heel-to-toe in a straight line. The Romberg test has the patient stand with feet together and eyes closed — without visual feedback, the patient must rely on proprioception and vestibular input alone.

Ataxia

Ataxia presents as a loss of coordination in voluntary movements. Causes include: exposure to exogenous substances (alcohol, ketamine, mercury), focal lesions (strokes, tumors, trauma, MS), and genetic disorders (hereditary cerebellar degeneration, Wilson's disease).

🧠
The Field Sobriety Test — Neurological Exam in Action
The FST is a variation of the coordination exam! Walking a straight line tests spinocerebellar function. The finger-to-nose test (eyes closed) removes visual feedback and reveals cerebellar compromise. The cerebellum is very sensitive to ethanol — alcohol disrupts the coordination circuit between motor cortex, cerebellum, and descending pathways.
