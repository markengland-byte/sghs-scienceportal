BIO.8a — Populations & Carrying Capacity
Growth Curves, Limiting Factors & Population Interactions
🎯
Carrying Capacity = Where the Graph Levels Off
The SOL WILL show you a population growth graph and ask you to identify carrying capacity. It's the point where the population PLATEAUS (levels off), not the highest point on the graph. If the population overshoots and then drops back, carrying capacity is the level it stabilizes at.
Populations, Communities & Ecosystems

A population is a group of organisms of the SAME species living in the same area at the same time. A community is all the populations of DIFFERENT species interacting in an area. An ecosystem includes the community PLUS all the nonliving (abiotic) factors in the environment.

Biotic vs. Abiotic Factors
Biotic (Living)Abiotic (Nonliving)
Predators, prey, competitorsTemperature, sunlight
Food sources, parasitesWater, moisture, rainfall
Disease-causing organismsSoil nutrients, pH
Mates, symbiotic partnersSalinity, air composition
Population Growth Curves

When a population enters a new environment with abundant resources, it grows exponentially (J-shaped curve). But resources are finite. As the population grows, competition increases, food becomes scarce, and disease spreads. Growth slows and eventually the population levels off at the carrying capacity (S-shaped or logistic curve).

Carrying capacity (K) is the maximum number of organisms an environment can support long-term with available resources. On a graph, it's where the population line plateaus.

🧠
Memory: J vs. S Curves
J-curve = exponential growth (unlimited resources, no limits yet). S-curve = logistic growth (real world, limited resources, levels off at carrying capacity). The SOL shows S-curves and asks you to find K — look for the plateau.
Limiting Factors

Limiting factors are resources or conditions that restrict population growth. When a limiting factor runs out, the population can't grow further. Examples: food, water, shelter, space, mates, sunlight (for plants). Disease and predation also limit populations.

Population Interactions
InteractionDescriptionExample
CompetitionTwo species compete for the same resourceLions and hyenas competing for prey
PredationOne organism kills and eats anotherHawk eating a mouse
MutualismBoth species benefitMycorrhizal fungi + plant roots (fungi get sugars, plant gets minerals)
CommensalismOne benefits, other is unaffectedBarnacles on a whale
ParasitismOne benefits, other is harmedTick feeding on a dog

1

3

BIO.8b — Energy Flow & Nutrient Cycles
Food Chains, Energy Pyramids & Biogeochemical Cycles
🎯
The 10% Rule Is Critical for Energy Pyramids
Only about 10% of energy at one trophic level is transferred to the next. The rest is lost as heat through metabolism. This means producers always have the MOST energy and biomass, and top predators have the LEAST. If a question asks about energy flow, remember: energy DECREASES going up the pyramid.
Food Chains and Food Webs

A food chain is a linear pathway showing energy flow: Producer → Primary Consumer → Secondary Consumer → Tertiary Consumer. A food web shows multiple interconnected food chains in an ecosystem, representing the complex feeding relationships among all organisms.

Energy enters most ecosystems as sunlight, which producers (plants, algae) convert to chemical energy through photosynthesis. Consumers obtain energy by eating other organisms. Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) break down dead organisms and recycle nutrients back into the ecosystem.

Trophic Levels and Energy Transfer
Trophic LevelTypeEnergyExample
1st (base)ProducersMost energy (100%)Grass, algae, trees
2ndPrimary consumers (herbivores)~10% of producer energyGrasshoppers, rabbits, zooplankton
3rdSecondary consumers~1% of originalFrogs, small fish, snakes
4thTertiary consumers (top predators)~0.1% of originalHawks, sharks, lions
⚠️
SOL Trap: Energy vs. Nutrients
Energy FLOWS through an ecosystem in ONE direction (sun → producers → consumers → lost as heat). It does NOT cycle. Nutrients CYCLE through an ecosystem (carbon, nitrogen, water move between organisms, atmosphere, and earth repeatedly). The SOL tests whether you know the difference.
The Carbon Cycle

Carbon moves through the ecosystem via four main processes:

ProcessWhat Happens
PhotosynthesisPlants absorb CO₂ from atmosphere and convert it to glucose (organic carbon)
Cellular respirationAll organisms release CO₂ back to atmosphere by breaking down glucose
CombustionBurning fossil fuels releases stored carbon as CO₂ into atmosphere
DecompositionDecomposers break down dead organisms, releasing CO₂ and returning carbon to soil
The Nitrogen Cycle

Nitrogen is essential for building proteins and DNA, but most organisms can't use atmospheric N₂ directly. The nitrogen cycle converts nitrogen between usable and unusable forms:

ProcessWhat Happens
Nitrogen fixationBacteria convert N₂ gas → ammonia (NH₃) that plants can absorb
NitrificationBacteria convert ammonia → nitrates (NO₃⁻) that plants prefer
AssimilationPlants absorb nitrates; animals get nitrogen by eating plants
DecompositionDecomposers break down dead organisms, releasing nitrogen as ammonia
DenitrificationBacteria convert nitrates back to N₂ gas, returning it to atmosphere

BIO.8c — Ecological Succession
Primary & Secondary Succession, Climax Communities
🎯
Primary vs. Secondary Succession Is Directly Tested
The SOL asks you to distinguish primary (starts from BARE ROCK, no soil) from secondary (starts after a DISTURBANCE where soil remains). Also know: Virginia's climax community is a deciduous oak-hickory forest. These are high-frequency questions.
What Is Ecological Succession?

Ecological succession is the predictable, gradual change in the species that make up a community over time. It follows a pattern: simple, hardy organisms colonize first, then are gradually replaced by more complex species until a stable climax community is reached.

Primary Succession

Primary succession occurs on surfaces where NO soil exists — bare rock, new volcanic islands, areas exposed by retreating glaciers. It is very slow (can take hundreds to thousands of years) because soil must be created from scratch.

StageWhat Happens
1. Pioneer speciesLichens (fungus + algae) and mosses colonize bare rock. They break down rock into tiny particles, beginning soil formation.
2. Soil buildsAs pioneers die and decompose, organic matter mixes with rock particles to form thin soil.
3. Grasses & small plantsSoil is deep enough for grasses, ferns, and wildflowers. Their roots hold soil, prevent erosion.
4. Shrubs & small treesShade-tolerant shrubs and fast-growing trees establish. They outcompete grasses for light.
5. Climax communityMature forest develops. In Virginia, this is a deciduous oak-hickory (hardwood) forest.
Secondary Succession

Secondary succession occurs after a disturbance (fire, flood, farming, logging) destroys the existing community but SOIL REMAINS. Because soil is already present (with seeds, roots, and nutrients), secondary succession is much FASTER than primary succession.

Example: An abandoned farm field in Virginia → grasses and weeds grow first → then shrubs → then pine trees → eventually hardwood trees (oak and hickory) take over as the climax community.

🧠
Memory: Primary = Primitive (no soil), Secondary = Soil Stays
Primary succession starts from scratch (bare rock, NO soil) — think "primitive" conditions. Secondary succession starts with existing soil after a disturbance — the "second time around" is faster because soil already exists.
Climax Community

A climax community is the final, stable stage of succession. It remains relatively unchanged unless a major disturbance occurs. The VDOE framework specifically states: "The climax community in most of Virginia is a deciduous oak-hickory (hardwood) forest."

Aquatic Succession

Succession also occurs in aquatic environments. A pond may gradually fill in with sediment and organic matter: open water → submerged plants → floating plants → marsh/wetland → eventually fills in completely to become dry land with terrestrial succession continuing. The VDOE requires you to "describe the patterns of succession found in aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems of Virginia."

BIO.8d — Human Impact & Virginia Ecosystems
Habitat Destruction, Pollution, Invasive Species & Virginia Watersheds
🎯
Human Impact Questions Are Virginia-Specific
The SOL asks about threats to Virginia ecosystems specifically: Chesapeake Bay watershed pollution, eutrophication from agricultural runoff, invasive species in Virginia waterways, and habitat loss from development. Know the cause-and-effect chains (fertilizer → algal bloom → oxygen depletion → fish kill).
Five Major Threats to Ecosystems
ThreatDescriptionExample
Habitat destructionClearing land for farming, development, loggingDeforestation removes habitat for wildlife; draining wetlands destroys aquatic ecosystems
PollutionChemicals, waste, and excess nutrients contaminate air, water, and soilFertilizer runoff causes eutrophication in Chesapeake Bay
Invasive speciesNon-native organisms that outcompete, prey on, or infect native speciesKudzu vine smothering native plants in Virginia; zebra mussels clogging water systems
OverexploitationHarvesting organisms faster than they can reproduceOverfishing depletes fish populations; overharvesting mushrooms
Climate changeRising temperatures and changing weather patterns disrupt ecosystemsCoral bleaching, shifting migration patterns, sea level rise
Eutrophication: The Fertilizer → Fish Kill Chain

Eutrophication is one of the most-tested BIO.8d concepts. Here's the chain of events:

Excess fertilizer runoff → Nutrients enter water → Algal bloom (rapid algae growth) → Algae die → Bacteria decompose algae → Bacteria consume dissolved oxygen → Oxygen levels drop → Fish suffocate and die

This is a major problem in the Chesapeake Bay watershed, which receives agricultural runoff from farms across Virginia, Maryland, and Pennsylvania.

Virginia-Specific Ecosystems

The VDOE framework requires you to "design, evaluate, and refine a solution for reducing the negative effects of human activity on a Virginia watershed or ecosystem." Key Virginia ecosystems include:

  • Chesapeake Bay — Largest estuary in the US; threatened by nutrient pollution, sediment runoff, and overfishing
  • Blue Ridge Mountains — Appalachian forests; threatened by acid rain, invasive insects (hemlock woolly adelgid)
  • Coastal wetlands — Filter pollutants, prevent flooding; threatened by development and sea level rise
  • Shenandoah Valley — Agricultural region; runoff from farms affects local waterways
Solutions for Reducing Human Impact

The SOL may ask you to evaluate solutions:

  • Buffer zones: Plant trees/grass along waterways to filter runoff before it enters streams
  • Reduced fertilizer use: Apply only the amount of fertilizer crops need; use organic alternatives
  • Wetland restoration: Rebuild wetlands that naturally filter pollutants and absorb floodwater
  • Invasive species control: Monitor and remove invasive plants/animals before they spread
  • Conservation easements: Protect land from development to preserve habitat
⚠️
SOL Trap: "Least Specialized" Animals Survive Change Best
The SOL may ask: "Which animals have the best chance of surviving a sudden environmental change?" The answer is always the LEAST specialized (generalists). Organisms that can eat many types of food, live in many habitats, and tolerate a range of conditions are more likely to survive when their environment changes drastically.
🔍 Worked SOL Example — Human Impact

(2004-14) An oak-hickory forest is cleared and replanted with pines. Which species would be most threatened?

1
Think about habitat specificity: Which species depends specifically on oak-hickory forest features?
2
Evaluate each species: The hooded warbler nests in the dense understory of hardwood forests. Pines have a different understory structure.
3
Answer: C — Hooded warbler. The most specialized species (dependent on specific habitat features) is most threatened by habitat change.

With only 3,000 left, any factor that reduces reproduction would be devastating. A disease reducing fertility = fewer births = population can't recover = extinction. More range or non-competing species wouldn't threaten them.

  • B

  • C

  • D

Generalists (least specialized) can eat different foods, live in different habitats, and tolerate a range of conditions. When the environment changes, they can adjust. Specialists (highly specialized) depend on specific resources that may disappear.

BIO.8 One-Page Cheat Sheet

Print for quick reference.

Populations (BIO.8a)

Carrying capacity (K) = where population graph levels off. Limiting factors: food, water, space, disease, predation. J-curve = exponential (unlimited). S-curve = logistic (reaches K). Interactions: competition, predation, mutualism (both +), commensalism (one +, one 0), parasitism (one +, one −).

Energy & Cycles (BIO.8b)

10% rule: only ~10% energy passes up each trophic level. Producers = most energy/biomass. Energy FLOWS (one direction, lost as heat). Nutrients CYCLE (carbon, nitrogen, water). Carbon cycle: photosynthesis, respiration, combustion, decomposition. Nitrogen cycle: fixation, nitrification, assimilation, decomposition, denitrification.

Human Impact (BIO.8d)

5 threats: habitat destruction, pollution, invasive species, overexploitation, climate change. Eutrophication: fertilizer → algal bloom → O₂ depletion → fish kill. Virginia: Chesapeake Bay watershed, oak-hickory forests, coastal wetlands. Solutions: buffer zones, reduced fertilizer, wetland restoration, invasive species control.

Released Virginia SOL questions —

1 (2001-16) A symbiotic relationship where one organism benefits while the other is harmed is — BIO.8a
  • A
    antagonism
  • B
    commensalism
  • C
    parasitism
  • D
    mutualism
Parasitism: parasite benefits, host is harmed (tick on dog). Mutualism: both benefit. Commensalism: one benefits, other unaffected.
2 (2003-49) In which biome do evaporation rates exceed precipitation rates? BIO.8a
  • A
    Hardwood forest
  • B
    Desert
  • C
    Grassland
  • D
    Tropical jungle
Deserts are defined by having more water lost through evaporation than gained through precipitation. This extreme aridity is the defining characteristic.
3 (2002-48) On a population graph, the deer population at carrying capacity is where the line — BIO.8a
  • A
    starts increasing
  • B
    reaches its steepest slope
  • C
    begins to decline
  • D
    levels off and stabilizes
Carrying capacity = where the population LEVELS OFF (plateaus). Not the peak, not the slope — the stable plateau.
4 (2004-8) Which organism has the greatest risk of population loss from the death of only a few individuals? BIO.8a
  • A
    Bacteria
  • B
    Flies
  • C
    Elephants
  • D
    Rats
Elephants produce few offspring with long gestation. A small population can't absorb losses. Bacteria, flies, and rats reproduce rapidly and can recover quickly.
5 (2006-43) Mycorrhizal fungi absorb water/minerals for plants and receive carbohydrates in return. This is — BIO.8a
  • A
    mutualism
  • B
    competition
  • C
    predation
  • D
    parasitism
Both benefit: fungi get carbs, plant gets water/minerals = mutualism.
6 (2003-34) Which is required by ALL living things? BIO.8b
  • A
    Oxygen
  • B
    Energy
  • C
    Mobility
  • D
    Carbon dioxide
ALL living things need energy. Not all need oxygen (anaerobic bacteria), not all move, not all use CO₂ directly.
7 (2006-48) In a food web diagram, which organism provides nutrients for the LARGEST number of other organisms? BIO.8b
  • A
    Bluefish
  • B
    Seal
  • C
    Snapper
  • D
    Herring
In a food web, the organism eaten by the most other organisms provides nutrients to the largest number. Herring is typically a key prey species eaten by many predators.
8 (2004-20) Which organism is correctly paired with its metabolism? BIO.8b
  • A
    Human — heterotroph
  • B
    Fish — autotroph
  • C
    Grass — heterotroph
  • D
    Mushroom — autotroph
Humans eat = heterotroph. Fish eat = heterotroph. Grass photosynthesizes = autotroph. Mushrooms decompose = heterotroph.
9 In a carbon cycle diagram, which process removes CO₂ from the atmosphere? BIO.8b
  • A
    Combustion
  • B
    Respiration
  • C
    Photosynthesis
  • D
    Decomposition
Photosynthesis absorbs CO₂ from the atmosphere and converts it to glucose. Respiration, combustion, and decomposition all RELEASE CO₂.
10 Energy is transferred from one organism to another in an ecosystem by — BIO.8b
  • A
    decomposition
  • B
    food consumption
  • C
    sunlight absorption
  • D
    water evaporation
Energy flows through food chains by EATING. Each organism obtains energy by consuming the one below it in the food chain.
11 (2006-14) In an aquatic habitat, a pioneer community example would be — BIO.8c
  • A
    grass
  • B
    water lilies
  • C
    water reeds
  • D
    algae
Algae are pioneers in aquatic succession — first to colonize, require least established conditions.
12 Virginia's climax community is a — BIO.8c
  • A
    pine forest
  • B
    grassland
  • C
    deciduous oak-hickory forest
  • D
    marsh
VDOE states Virginia's climax = deciduous oak-hickory (hardwood) forest.
13 After a volcanic eruption creates new land, the first organisms to appear would be — BIO.8c
  • A
    lichens and mosses
  • B
    tall trees
  • C
    large mammals
  • D
    flowering plants
Primary succession on bare volcanic rock: lichens and mosses are pioneer species. They break down rock to begin soil formation.
14 A forest destroyed by fire regrows faster than bare volcanic rock because the burned area has — BIO.8c
  • A
    more sunlight
  • B
    existing soil with seeds and nutrients
  • C
    fewer predators
  • D
    more rainfall
Secondary succession (after fire) is faster because SOIL already exists with seeds, roots, and nutrients. Primary succession (bare rock) must create soil from scratch.
15 (2002-33) Many Virginia forests have been cleared for farmland. Which factor would LEAST affect how long it takes to return a farm to forest? BIO.8c
  • A
    The ability of the farmland to retain moisture
  • B
    The number of viable tree seeds on the land
  • C
    The density of the original forest on the farm
  • D
    The amount of available nutrients in the soil
The density of the ORIGINAL forest (before it was cleared) doesn't affect how quickly new forest grows back. What matters NOW is moisture, seeds, and soil nutrients. This is secondary succession — the land already has soil.
16 (2004-14) An oak-hickory forest cleared and replanted with pines would most threaten the — BIO.8d
  • A
    cardinal
  • B
    summer tanager
  • C
    hooded warbler
  • D
    field sparrow
The hooded warbler depends on dense hardwood understory. Replacing hardwoods with pines destroys this specific habitat. More specialized species are most threatened by habitat change.
17 (2004-46) Which caterpillar control method would be most dangerous to honeybees? BIO.8d
  • A
    Moth scent traps
  • B
    Caterpillar parasites
  • C
    Moth-repelling plants
  • D
    Spraying insecticides
Insecticides kill ALL insects indiscriminately, including beneficial pollinators like honeybees. The other methods target only the pest species.
18 (2005-33) Of causes of wetland loss, people have LEAST control over — BIO.8d
  • A
    droughts
  • B
    draining and filling
  • C
    pollutant discharge
  • D
    overgrazing
Droughts are natural weather events; humans can't prevent them. The others are direct human activities that can be regulated.
19 (2006-15) Least specialized animals survive sudden environmental changes best because they — BIO.8d
  • A
    adapt to different conditions
  • B
    move faster
  • C
    mutate rapidly
  • D
    reproduce abundantly
Generalists tolerate a wide range of conditions. When the environment changes, they can adjust. Specialists depend on specific resources that may disappear.
20 (2003-24) With fewer than 3,000 manatees left, which would most likely cause extinction? BIO.8d
  • A
    Increased range
  • B
    More non-competing species
  • C
    Disease reducing fertility
  • D
    A freeze in northern range
With only 3,000 left, reduced fertility = fewer births = population can't recover = extinction. More range or non-competing species wouldn't threaten them.
