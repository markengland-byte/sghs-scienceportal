When you see an experiment scenario on the SOL, ask yourself: "What did the scientist CHANGE?" = Independent Variable. "What did the scientist MEASURE?" = Dependent Variable. "What stayed the same in all groups?" = Constants. This 3-question routine solves nearly every BIO.1a question.
BIO.1a — Experimental Design
Variables, Controls & Hypotheses
🎯
This Is Worth ~2 Questions on the SOL
Nearly every BIO.1 question starts with a scenario. You must be able to quickly identify the IV, DV, constants, and control group from a short paragraph. Drill the routine below until it's automatic.
The Experiment Routine: 4 Questions to Ask Every Time
Question to AskWhat It IdentifiesSOL Example Answer
What did the scientist CHANGE?Independent Variable (IV)"Amount of fertilizer" — the scientist used 5g, 10g, and 15g in different groups
What did the scientist MEASURE?Dependent Variable (DV)"Height of the plants after 2 weeks" — what was observed as a result
What STAYED THE SAME in all groups?Constants (controlled variables)"Type of plant, amount of water, amount of sunlight, type of soil" — all identical
Which group received NO treatment?Control Group"Group receiving 0g fertilizer" — the baseline comparison group
The Hypothesis: If…Then…Because

A valid hypothesis must be testable (you can design an experiment) and falsifiable (it could be proven wrong). The standard SOL format is:

"If [independent variable] is [changed in this way],
then [dependent variable] will [predicted result],
because [scientific reasoning]."
⚠️
Common Mistake: Confusing IV and DV
The IV is the CAUSE — it's what you set up BEFORE the experiment. The DV is the EFFECT — it changes BECAUSE of the IV and is measured at the END. Ask: "Could this variable be set up before I start?" = IV. "Do I have to wait to measure this?" = DV.
Why the Control Group Matters

Without a control group, you cannot determine whether your treatment caused the results. If plants with fertilizer grow taller than plants without fertilizer, the fertilizer likely caused the growth. Without the unfertilized control group, you'd have no comparison.

🧠
Memory: "Control = Compare"
The control group is what you COMPARE the experimental groups against. It typically receives: no treatment (placebo), OR the standard/normal treatment. Everything else stays identical. "If there's no control, there's no comparison — and no valid conclusion."
BIO.1b — Data & Measurement
Qualitative vs. Quantitative · Accuracy vs. Precision · Sample Size
Qualitative vs. Quantitative Data
TypeDefinitionExamplesSOL Tip
QualitativeDescriptive observations using words — no numbers"The liquid turned blue." "The plant is wilting." "The colony smells sour."Any time you see a word like "appears," "looks," "smells," or "feels" = qualitative
QuantitativeNumerical observations using measurements"The plant grew 12.5 cm." "3 out of 10 mice survived." "pH = 4.2"Any time you see a NUMBER or UNIT = quantitative
🧠
Memory: "Quantity = Number"
Quantitative has the word "quantity" in it → numbers. Qualitative has "quality" → descriptions. If it has a unit (cm, g, °C, mL), it is always quantitative.
Accuracy vs. Precision — The Bull's-Eye Model
TermDefinitionBull's-Eye AnalogyExample
AccuracyHow close the measurement is to the TRUE (accepted) valueShots cluster near the center (bull's-eye)Measuring boiling water: 99°C, 100°C, 101°C (accurate — near 100°C)
PrecisionHow CONSISTENT/REPEATABLE the measurements areShots cluster tightly together (even if far from center)Measuring boiling water: 95°C, 95°C, 95°C (precise — consistent, but inaccurate)
🎯
The 4-Case SOL Question — Know All Four
Accurate AND Precise: Results close to true value AND close together (95°C, 100°C, 99°C → no, these spread too much... 99°C, 100°C, 100°C ✓)
Precise but NOT Accurate: Results close together but far from true value (e.g., 93°C, 93°C, 93°C — consistent but wrong)
Accurate but NOT Precise: Results scattered around the true value (95°C, 100°C, 106°C — average near 100°C but spread)
Neither: Results scattered AND far from true value (75°C, 95°C, 110°C)
Sample Size & Replication
TermDefinitionWhy It Matters
Sample SizeThe number of subjects, trials, or observations in an experimentSmall sample size = unreliable results; a sample of 3 mice could vary wildly by chance. Large samples average out individual variation.
ReplicationRepeating the entire experiment (not just multiple subjects)If independent researchers get the same result, confidence increases that the finding is real, not a fluke of one lab
⚠️
SOL Trap: "Only 3 subjects were tested"
When a question asks WHY a study is unreliable or invalid, "small sample size" is almost always the correct answer. On the SOL, if they tell you an experiment used 3, 4, or 5 subjects, the answer is almost always that the sample size is too small.
BIO.1c — Graph Interpretation
Reading & Interpreting Scientific Graphs
🎯
Graph Questions Appear on EVERY SOL Test
At least 1-2 questions on every Biology SOL involve reading a graph. The question is never just "read the number" — it asks you to draw a conclusion, identify a trend, or predict what happens beyond the graph. The most common miss: students read the right number but pick the wrong conclusion from the options.
Graph Types & When to Use Each
Graph TypeBest Used ForWhat to Look For
Bar GraphComparing groups or categories (different soils, different treatments)Tallest bar = highest value; compare heights across groups
Line GraphShowing change over time or a continuous variable (temperature vs. rate)Slope direction (rising/falling), peak/trough, where curves cross
Scatter PlotShowing the relationship between two variables (study time vs. grade)Trend direction: positive (both increase), negative (one increases, other decreases), no correlation (scattered)
Pie ChartShowing parts of a whole (% composition)Largest slice = largest proportion
Practice: Enzyme Activity vs. Temperature
Enzyme Reaction Rate at Different Temperatures
A typical bell-curve enzyme activity graph — this type appears frequently on the Virginia Biology SOL
📊 Based on this graph, at which temperature is the enzyme MOST active?
A) 10°C — enzymes work best in cold conditions
B) 37°C — this is the peak/optimum temperature on the graph
C) 50°C — high temperature increases reaction rate
D) 70°C — the enzyme is completely denatured here
✅ Correct! The peak of the curve is at 37°C — this is the enzyme's optimal temperature. Above 37°C the enzyme begins to denature (unfold) and activity drops sharply to zero by ~60°C.
❌ Look at the peak of the line — it is highest at 37°C. That peak is the OPTIMUM temperature. High temperatures (above ~45°C) cause the enzyme to denature, dropping activity to zero.
Practice: Population Growth
Population Growth: Species A vs. Species B
Line graphs comparing two populations — another common SOL format
📊 In what year did Species A's population FIRST exceed Species B's?
A) Year 2
B) Year 3
C) Year 4 — this is where the two lines cross
D) Year 6
✅ Year 4 is where the lines cross — Species A rises above Species B. Before year 4, Species B had more individuals; after year 4, Species A has more.
❌ Find where the two lines intersect (cross) — that's where A first equals then exceeds B. The intersection is at Year 4.
5 Rules for SOL Graph Questions
#Rule
1Read the axes first. What is on the x-axis? What is on the y-axis? What are the units? Do this before looking at the data.
2Identify the trend. Is it increasing? Decreasing? Leveling off? Peaking? Crossing?
3Only conclude what the graph shows. Don't add outside knowledge. The correct answer is always directly supported by the graph.
4Beware of cause-and-effect answer choices. A graph shows correlation — it cannot prove causation. Wrong choices often say "causes" when the data only shows a relationship.
5Watch for "best describes" vs. "proves." A graph can "show" or "suggest" a trend — it rarely "proves" causation.
BIO.1d — Scientific Reasoning
Correlation vs. Causation · Source Reliability · Experimental Errors
Correlation vs. Causation — The Most Misunderstood Concept in Biology
TermMeaningSOL Example
CorrelationTwo variables change together — but one does NOT necessarily CAUSE the other. A graph, scatter plot, or data table can show a correlation."Cities with more hospitals have more deaths." Both are higher in bigger cities. Hospitals don't cause death — city size causes both.
CausationOne variable DIRECTLY causes the change in another. Requires a controlled experiment to establish."Smoking increases risk of lung cancer" — proven through controlled studies showing the biological mechanism.
🎯
SOL Trap: "A student CONCLUDES that X CAUSES Y from the graph"
On the SOL, they will show you data with two correlated variables and one answer choice will say something "causes" the other. That is almost always WRONG — data alone cannot prove causation. The correct answer will say "there is a relationship between" or "as X increases, Y also increases" (correlation language). Watch for the word "causes" in answer choices — it's usually the distractor.
Source Reliability — Which Sources Can You Trust?
Source TypeReliabilityReason
Peer-reviewed journal article🟢 HighestReviewed by expert scientists before publication; most rigorous standard
Government agency website (.gov)🟢 HighCDC, NIH, VDOE — accountable, evidence-based
University/research institution (.edu)🟡 Usually goodAcademically accountable but not all content peer-reviewed
News article (major outlet)🟡 MediumMay summarize studies but can oversimplify; check primary source
Personal blog, social media🔴 LowNo review process; anyone can post anything
Advertisement / product website🔴 LowestFinancial interest in a conclusion; not objective
Types of Experimental Errors
Error TypeDefinitionExample
Procedural ErrorMistake in how the experiment was conductedStudent forgot to add the reagent; timing was off
Measurement ErrorInaccurate reading of an instrument or recordingReading a graduated cylinder from the wrong angle
Sampling ErrorSample was too small or not representativeTested only 3 patients; tested only male rats
BiasExperimenter's expectation influences the resultsUnconsciously rounding measurements toward the expected value
🔑
Inference vs. Observation
Observation: What you directly see, hear, measure — a fact recorded at the time. "The solution turned red." "The mouse ran 14 laps." Inference: A conclusion drawn FROM observations based on prior knowledge. "The solution is acidic." (inferred from color change) "The mouse appears stressed." (inferred from behavior). The SOL sometimes asks which statement is an inference vs. an observation — look for statements that go beyond the raw data.

1A student investigates how the amount of fertilizer affects the height of bean plants. Three groups receive 5g, 10g, or 15g of fertilizer. All other conditions are identical. What is the independent variable?BIO.1a
  • A
    The height of the plants after 2 weeks
  • B
    The amount of fertilizer added to each group
  • C
    The type of soil used
  • D
    The amount of water given to each plant
The independent variable is what the scientist deliberately changes — here, the fertilizer amount (5g/10g/15g) is different in each group. Plant height is the DV (measured result). Soil and water are constants (kept the same).
2In the same experiment, what is the dependent variable?BIO.1a
  • A
    The amount of fertilizer
  • B
    The type of bean plant
  • C
    The amount of sunlight
  • D
    The height of the plants after 2 weeks
The dependent variable is what you MEASURE — what depends on the IV. Here, plant height is observed and recorded after 2 weeks as a result of the fertilizer treatment.

  • B
    Independent variable
  • C

    Dependent variable
The control group receives no experimental treatment (or the standard treatment). It is the baseline — without it, you cannot know whether the treatment caused the results. Group A (with fertilizer) is the experimental group.

5A student measures boiling water five times: 95°C, 95°C, 94°C, 95°C, 95°C. The accepted boiling point is 100°C. These results are BEST described as:BIO.1b
  • A
    Accurate and precise
  • B

  • C
    Accurate but not precise
  • D

Precise = consistent, repeatable results (94-95°C, tightly clustered). Accurate = close to the true value (100°C). These readings are consistent but 5-6°C from the true value → precise but NOT accurate.
6A scientist tests a new drug on 4 mice. A peer reviewer says the results are unreliable. What is the MOST likely reason?BIO.1b
  • A
    Mice are poor models for human drug testing
  • B
    The sample size of 4 is too small — results could be due to individual variation rather than the drug's effect
  • C
    The experiment was not replicated in a different lab
  • D
    The drug was not tested on humans first
When a SOL question asks why a study is unreliable and mentions a very small number of subjects, the answer is almost always SAMPLE SIZE. Four mice is far too small — results could happen by chance with that few subjects.
7A student writes: "If the amount of light increases, then the rate of photosynthesis will increase." This statement is a valid hypothesis because it is:BIO.1a
  • A
    A proven scientific fact
  • B
    Testable and makes a specific, falsifiable prediction about the relationship between IV (light) and DV (photosynthesis rate)
  • C
    Based on a peer-reviewed study
  • D
    Written in question format
A valid hypothesis is testable (you can design an experiment) and falsifiable (it could be proven wrong). This hypothesis clearly states the IV and DV and predicts a relationship. It is NOT a fact — it is a prediction awaiting testing.
8📊 A bar graph shows plant heights: Sandy soil = 8 cm, Clay = 5 cm, Loam = 14 cm, Silt = 11 cm. Which conclusion is BEST supported by this data?BIO.1c

9Data shows that cities with more fire stations also have more fires per year. A student concludes that fire stations CAUSE fires. What is WRONG with this conclusion?BIO.1d
  • A
    The data clearly shows fire stations cause fires
  • B
    Fire stations should reduce fires, not cause them
  • C

  • D

Classic correlation ≠ causation trap. Larger cities have both more fires AND more fire stations — city size explains both. The data shows a correlation; it does NOT prove fire stations cause fires. A graph or data set alone can NEVER prove causation.
10A scientist repeats the same experiment 10 times and gets consistent results each time. This demonstrates that the results are:BIO.1b
  • A
    Accurate — the results must be close to the true value
  • B
    Biased — the scientist expected the same result each time
  • C
    Reliable due to replication — consistent results across repeated trials increase confidence in the conclusion
  • D
    Based on too large a sample size
Replication = repeating the experiment multiple times. Consistent results across many replications = high reliability. Note: replication improves reliability, not necessarily accuracy — the consistent result could still be wrong if there was a systematic error in all trials.
11Which source would be MOST reliable for a report on the effects of climate change on coral reefs?BIO.1d

12Why is a control group necessary in an experiment?BIO.1a

13📊 A scatter plot shows data points trending upward from left to right (as x increases, y increases). What type of relationship does this show?BIO.1c

Upward trend on a scatter plot = positive correlation (both variables increase together). This is a correlation — NOT causation. A scatter plot cannot prove that one variable causes the other; only a controlled experiment can establish causation.
14A student tests whether temperature affects seed germination. Seeds are planted at 10°C, 20°C, 30°C, and 40°C. After 7 days, she counts the number of seeds that sprouted. What is the dependent variable?BIO.1a

15A student observes that when litmus paper turns red, it indicates an acid is present. Which statement is an INFERENCE based on this observation?BIO.1d

  • D

An observation is what you directly see. An inference is a conclusion drawn FROM an observation using prior knowledge. "The paper turned red" = observation. "The solution is acidic" = inference (we know red litmus = acid, so we infer the solution's nature).
