BIO.1a — Asking Questions & Defining Problems
Hypotheses, Variables & Experimental Design
🎯
This Is Worth ~2 Questions on Every SOL
Nearly every BIO.1 question on the SOL starts with a short experiment scenario. You read it, and then you must identify the IV, DV, constants, or control group. The good news: these questions follow the exact same pattern every single year. Learn the routine below, and these become free points.
Start with a Real-World Scenario

Imagine you want to know if playing music helps plants grow faster. You set up two groups of identical bean plants in the same room, with the same soil, the same amount of water, and the same amount of sunlight. The only difference? One group gets 8 hours of classical music per day, and the other group sits in silence. After 3 weeks, you measure the height of every plant in both groups.

This is a simple experiment, but it contains every piece the SOL will ask you about. Let's break it down using the 4-Question Routine — the single most useful tool for BIO.1a questions.

The 4-Question Routine: Ask These Every Time
Question to AskWhat It IdentifiesMusic & Plants Answer
What did the scientist CHANGE?Independent Variable (IV)Music vs. no music — the only thing that differs between the two groups
What did the scientist MEASURE?Dependent Variable (DV)Plant height after 3 weeks — what was recorded as data
What STAYED THE SAME in all groups?Constants (controlled variables)Soil type, water amount, sunlight, room temperature, pot size, plant species — all identical
Which group got NO treatment?Control GroupThe silent group — no music applied; this is the baseline for comparison

This routine works on every experiment the SOL will show you. The IV is always what was deliberately changed before the experiment started. The DV is always what was measured after the experiment ran. Constants are everything else — the stuff you keep the same so the test is fair. And the control group is the group that receives no treatment, so you have something to compare against.

🔑
The Core Rule: IV = Cause, DV = Effect
Think of the IV as the cause (what you do) and the DV as the effect (what happens as a result). The IV can be set up BEFORE the experiment begins. The DV can only be determined AFTER data is collected. If you're unsure which is which, ask: "Could the scientist have decided this BEFORE starting?" If yes, it's the IV.
Writing a Valid Hypothesis

A hypothesis is not a guess. It is a testable, falsifiable prediction that connects the independent variable to the dependent variable and includes a reason. The standard format the SOL expects is:

"If [independent variable] is [changed in this way],
then [dependent variable] will [predicted result],
because [scientific reasoning]."

Let's look at three examples — some good, some bad — so you can see what the SOL is looking for.

HypothesisValid?Why?
"If bean plants are exposed to classical music for 8 hours daily, then they will grow taller than plants in silence, because sound vibrations may stimulate cell growth."YESLinks IV (music) to DV (height), includes a testable prediction and a reason. You could design an experiment to test this.
"Plants like music."NONot testable — how do you measure "liking"? No IV, no DV, no prediction. This is an opinion, not a hypothesis.
"If I water plants with soda instead of water, then the plants will grow shorter, because the sugar and acid in soda may damage root cells."YESClear IV (soda vs. water), clear DV (height), clear prediction (shorter), and reasoning (sugar/acid damage). Testable and falsifiable.
⚠️
Common SOL Traps on This Standard
Trap 1: Confusing IV and DV. Students often pick the DV when asked for the IV. Remember: the IV is what the scientist SET UP before starting. The DV is the RESULT that was measured afterward.

Trap 2: Untestable hypotheses. Statements like "the plant is happy" or "the reaction will be interesting" are not hypotheses because you cannot measure "happy" or "interesting." A hypothesis must predict a measurable outcome.

Trap 3: Forgetting the control group. When a question asks "why are the results invalid?", check if the experiment has a control group. No control = no valid comparison = no valid conclusion.

BIO.1b — Planning & Carrying Out Investigations
Sample Size, Replication, Tools & Experimental Validity
🎯
Why This Standard Matters on the SOL
BIO.1b questions ask you to evaluate whether an experiment is set up correctly. The most common question type: "Why are these results NOT reliable?" The answer is almost always small sample size or no control group. If you understand what makes an experiment valid, these questions become straightforward.
What Makes a Valid Experiment?

A valid experiment is one that actually tests what it claims to test. For an experiment to be valid, it needs five things: (1) a clearly defined independent variable that is deliberately changed, (2) a measurable dependent variable, (3) a control group for comparison, (4) all other variables held constant, and (5) a large enough sample size to rule out chance. If any of these five elements is missing, the results cannot be trusted. The SOL will give you experiment descriptions with one of these elements missing and ask you to identify the flaw.

Think of it this way: if your friend told you, "I gave one plant fertilizer and it grew taller, so fertilizer works!" you should immediately see the problems. One plant is not enough (sample size too small). There is no comparison plant without fertilizer (no control group). Maybe the plant grew taller because of sunlight, not fertilizer (constants not controlled). A valid experiment would use dozens of plants, with half receiving fertilizer and half receiving none, all in identical conditions.

Sample Size: Why It Matters More Than Anything

Sample size is the number of subjects, organisms, or trials in your experiment. It is arguably the most important factor in determining whether your results are reliable. Here is why, explained with a simple analogy.

Imagine flipping a coin 3 times. You get heads all 3 times. Does that prove the coin is rigged? Of course not — getting 3 heads in a row happens about 12.5% of the time with a perfectly fair coin. Now flip that same coin 100 times. If you get heads 95 out of 100 times, something is clearly wrong with the coin. The difference is sample size. With 3 flips, random chance can easily produce misleading results. With 100 flips, the randomness averages out, and the true pattern emerges.

The same principle applies to biology experiments. If you test a drug on 3 mice and all 3 survive, that could be coincidence. If you test it on 300 mice and 290 survive, you have strong evidence. On the SOL, whenever a question asks "why are these results unreliable?" and the experiment used only 3-5 subjects, the answer is almost always "the sample size is too small."

⚠️
SOL Trap: Small Sample Size Questions
The SOL loves to describe an experiment with 3 or 4 test subjects and ask why the results are not valid. Students often pick "no hypothesis" or "wrong tools," but the answer is almost always "the sample size is too small to draw reliable conclusions." Look for the number of subjects first — if it's tiny, that is your answer.
Replication vs. Repeated Trials

These two terms sound similar but mean different things, and the SOL may test whether you know the difference. Repeated trials means running the same experiment multiple times within a single study — for example, measuring plant height on Day 7, Day 14, and Day 21, or running three rounds of the same test. This helps reduce random error within your own experiment. Replication means that independent researchers in a different lab repeat your entire experiment and get similar results. Replication is what gives the scientific community confidence that a finding is real and not just a fluke from one particular lab or researcher.

Control Groups: Why They Are Essential

A control group is the group that receives NO treatment — it provides the baseline for comparison. Without it, you cannot determine whether your treatment actually caused the observed effect. Imagine testing whether a new fertilizer makes tomatoes grow bigger. You apply the fertilizer to 20 plants and they all produce large tomatoes. Great result, right? Not necessarily. Without a control group of 20 plants grown WITHOUT fertilizer, you have no way of knowing whether the tomatoes would have been just as big on their own. Maybe it was a great growing season. Maybe the soil was naturally rich. The control group answers: "What would have happened without the treatment?"

Safety and Ethics

The Virginia SOL curriculum framework specifically states that students must "plan and conduct investigations in a safe and ethical manner including considerations of environmental, social, and personal effects." This means: wearing safety goggles and gloves when handling chemicals, properly disposing of biological waste, following teacher instructions for equipment use, and considering whether an experiment could harm organisms or the environment. While the SOL rarely asks standalone safety questions, safety considerations may appear as part of experiment-design scenarios.

🧠
Constants Checklist: How to Identify Them
Constants are everything that stays the SAME across all groups. When you read an experiment scenario, look for phrases like "same type of," "identical," "all received," "kept constant," or "uniform." If the scenario says "the same amount of water was given to all plants," then water amount is a constant. Constants ensure a fair test — if something other than the IV differs between groups, you cannot know what caused the results.

BIO.1c — Interpreting, Analyzing & Evaluating Data
Data Tables, Graphs & Drawing Conclusions from Data
🎯
Graph Questions Appear on EVERY SOL Test
At least 1-2 questions on every Biology SOL involve reading a graph or data table. The question is never just "read the number" — it asks you to draw a conclusion, identify a trend, or predict what happens next. The most common mistake: students read the correct data point but pick the wrong conclusion from the answer choices.
How to Read a Data Table

Data tables are the foundation of every experiment. Before you can interpret a graph, you need to understand the table it came from. Here is how to read one systematically.

In a properly constructed data table, the independent variable (IV) goes in the left column — this is what the scientist changed. The dependent variable (DV) goes in the right column(s) — this is what was measured. If the experiment includes repeated trials, each trial gets its own column, and the final column shows the mean (average). Every column must have a header that includes the variable name AND the unit of measurement.

Here is an example of a well-constructed data table for our music-and-plants experiment:

Treatment (IV)Trial 1 Height (cm)Trial 2 Height (cm)Trial 3 Height (cm)Mean Height (cm)
Classical music (8 hr/day)14.215.113.814.4
No music (control)11.512.311.911.9

Notice the structure: IV on the left (music vs. no music), DV on the right (height in cm), three trials for reliability, and a mean calculated from the trials. The SOL will sometimes ask you to identify the IV or DV from a data table — always look at column headers and ask "which column was the scientist's choice?" (IV) vs. "which column was measured as a result?" (DV).

If the x-axis shows categories (Fertilizer A, B, C), use a bar graph. If the x-axis shows time (Day 1, Day 2, Day 3) or a continuous variable, use a line graph. If you are looking for a relationship between two measured variables, use a scatter plot. If you are showing parts of a whole, use a pie chart.

Making Predictions from Graphs

Prediction questions ask you to extend a trend beyond the data shown. The key: identify the pattern first, then apply it. If a line graph shows a population increasing by 20 each week (20, 40, 60, 80), the prediction for the next week is 100 — you simply continue the pattern. If a line graph shows exponential growth (doubling each period), then the next value is double the last. The SOL will NOT ask you to predict something from a completely random scatter — there will always be a clear trend to extend.

Qualitative vs. Quantitative Data

Every piece of data you collect in a science experiment falls into one of two categories: qualitative or quantitative. Understanding the difference is not just vocabulary — the SOL will hand you a list of observations and ask you to classify them, or it will ask which type of data a particular tool collects. Getting this wrong costs you an easy point.

Qualitative data describes a quality — a characteristic you observe with your senses but do not measure with a number. Think of the word "quality" hiding inside "qualitative." When you say "the leaf turned brown," you are describing a color change. When you say "the solution smelled like vinegar," you are describing an odor. When you note "the bacteria formed smooth, round colonies," you are describing shape and texture. None of these involve a number or a unit — they describe what something is like.

Quantitative data involves a quantity — a measurement expressed with a number and a unit. Think of the word "quantity" hiding inside "quantitative." When you record "the leaf lost 3.2 g of mass," that is quantitative because it has a number (3.2) and a unit (grams). "The plant grew 4.7 cm in 5 days" is quantitative. "The solution had a pH of 2.3" is quantitative. The key signature is always: number + unit.

Here is the critical distinction the SOL tests: a statement can include a number and still be qualitative if the number is not a measurement. "There are five petals on the flower" is a count used to describe a feature — some test-writers classify this as qualitative (describing structure) while others call it quantitative (it uses a number). On the SOL, the safest rule is: if it involves a measuring instrument and a unit, it is quantitative; if it describes an appearance, color, smell, texture, or behavior, it is qualitative.

💡
Practice — Classify Each Statement
1. "The frog's skin was bright green with black spots." → Qualitative (describes color/pattern)
2. "The tadpole measured 2.4 cm in length." → Quantitative (number + unit)
3. "The bacterial culture had a sour odor." → Qualitative (describes smell)
4. "The reaction produced 15.8 mL of gas." → Quantitative (number + unit)
5. "The solution turned cloudy after heating." → Qualitative (describes appearance)
6. "The plant's mass increased by 0.7 g over 10 days." → Quantitative (number + unit)
Accuracy vs. Precision — The Bull's-Eye Analogy

These two words sound similar but mean completely different things in science, and the SOL loves to test whether you can tell them apart.

Accuracy is how close your measurement is to the true value — the accepted, known answer. If water boils at 100°C and your thermometer reads 99.8°C, your measurement is accurate. Accuracy answers the question: "Did you hit the bull's-eye?"

Precision is how close your repeated measurements are to each other. If you measure the boiling point three times and get 94.2°C, 94.3°C, and 94.1°C, those values are very precise — they are tightly clustered. But they are NOT accurate, because the true value is 100°C, and you are consistently 6 degrees off. Precision answers the question: "Are your shots grouped tightly together?"

Picture a dartboard with four possible outcomes:

ScenarioAccuracyPrecisionWhat It Looks Like
1. Accurate + PreciseHigh ✓High ✓All darts clustered tightly in the bull's-eye. Your thermometer reads 99.9, 100.0, 100.1. This is the GOAL of good measurement.
2. Precise but NOT AccurateLow ✗High ✓All darts clustered tightly — but in the upper left corner, far from center. Your thermometer reads 94.2, 94.3, 94.1. Consistent but WRONG. This suggests a systematic error — the instrument may be miscalibrated.
3. Accurate but NOT PreciseHigh ✓Low ✗Darts scattered around the board, but they average out to center. Your thermometer reads 96.0, 104.0, 100.5. The mean is close to 100, but individual readings vary wildly. This suggests random error.
4. Neither Accurate NOR PreciseLow ✗Low ✗Darts scattered everywhere, nowhere near the center. Readings of 88.0, 105.0, 72.0. Nothing is consistent and nothing is close to the true value.
⚠️
Why This Matters in Real Experiments
If your data is precise but not accurate, your instrument is probably miscalibrated — recalibrate it. If your data is accurate but not precise, you have too much random error — improve your technique or increase sample size. The SOL may describe a lab scenario and ask you to identify the problem. Knowing which type of error is at play tells you the solution.
Observation vs. Inference — A Critical Distinction

An observation is something you directly detect with your senses or measure with an instrument. It is a fact recorded at the time of the event. There is no guessing, no interpreting, no reasoning involved — just recording what happened.

An inference is a conclusion you draw from one or more observations, combined with your prior knowledge. It goes beyond what you directly saw. Inferences can be wrong — observations (if recorded honestly) cannot.

Walk through these examples carefully:

StatementTypeWhy
"The solution turned red when indicator was added."ObservationYou directly saw the color change. That is a recorded fact.
"The solution is acidic."InferenceYou concluded it was acidic because the indicator turned red. That conclusion requires prior knowledge about what red means.
"The owl pellet contained three mouse skulls."ObservationYou directly counted the skulls. Factual recording.
"The owl ate at least three mice."InferenceYou concluded how many mice the owl consumed based on the skull count. Reasonable, but still a conclusion drawn from evidence.

Correlation vs. Causation — MOST COMMONLY MISSED on the SOL

This is the single most frequently missed concept on the Virginia Biology SOL. Students see data showing two things happening together and assume one causes the other. The SOL is designed to punish that assumption.

Correlation means two variables change together — when one goes up, the other goes up (positive correlation), or when one goes up, the other goes down (negative correlation). A graph can show correlation. A data table can show correlation. But correlation alone does NOT prove that one variable caused the change in the other.

The classic example: As ice cream sales increase, drowning deaths also increase. The data shows a clear positive correlation. Does ice cream cause drowning? Of course not. A third variable — hot weather — causes both. People buy more ice cream in summer AND swim more in summer. The two variables are correlated but one does not cause the other.

Causation means one variable directly produces a change in another. To establish causation, you need a controlled experiment — one where you manipulate the independent variable, hold all other variables constant, include a control group, and measure the dependent variable. Only this kind of experiment can show that X causes Y.

Here is what the SOL does: it shows you a graph or data table where two variables are correlated, and one of the answer choices says "X causes Y." That answer is almost always WRONG. The correct answer will use correlation language: "as X increases, Y also increases," "there is a relationship between X and Y," or "X is associated with Y." Watch for these signal words:

Correlation Language (Usually Correct)Causation Language (Usually the Trap)

The One-Sentence Rule
If the question describes a graph, survey, or observational study — the answer can ONLY describe a correlation. If the question describes a controlled experiment — the answer MAY describe a cause-and-effect relationship. No graph alone can prove causation. Only a controlled experiment can.
Hypothesis vs. Theory vs. Law — VDOE Explicitly Requires This

The Virginia SOL requires students to distinguish between these three levels of scientific knowledge. This is one of the most commonly misunderstood topics because everyday language uses these words differently from how science uses them. On the SOL, only the scientific definitions matter.

Hypothesis: A testable prediction that can be supported or rejected by a single experiment. A hypothesis is a starting point — it has not yet been extensively tested. It usually follows an "if...then" format: "If fertilizer is added to the soil, then plant height will increase." A hypothesis can be rejected — and that is perfectly fine. Rejection is still a valid scientific result.

Theory: A well-substantiated explanation of natural phenomena, supported by a large body of evidence from many independent experiments, observations, and analyses over time. A theory is NOT a guess. It is one of the strongest forms of scientific knowledge. Theories explain WHY something happens — the mechanism behind it. Examples: Cell Theory, Theory of Evolution by Natural Selection, Germ Theory of Disease. Each of these is backed by thousands of independent studies.

Law: A description of an observed pattern in nature, often expressed mathematically. A law tells you WHAT happens, but not WHY. The Law of Conservation of Energy says energy cannot be created or destroyed — but it does not explain the mechanism. Mendel's Law of Segregation says alleles separate during meiosis — it describes the pattern but does not explain the molecular machinery that makes it happen.

⚠️
A Theory Does NOT Become a Law — They Serve Different Purposes
This is the biggest misconception students bring to the SOL. Many students believe: hypothesis → theory → law, as if a theory "graduates" into a law with enough evidence. That is completely wrong. A theory explains WHY (mechanism). A law describes WHAT (pattern). They answer different questions. The Theory of Evolution will never become the "Law of Evolution" — it already explains the mechanism of natural selection. The Law of Gravity describes the pattern (objects attract); the theory of gravity explains why they attract.
🎯
SOL Trap: "Evolution is JUST a theory"
If an answer choice implies that a scientific theory is "just a guess," "unproven," or "less reliable than a law," it is WRONG. In science, a theory has been tested repeatedly, supported by enormous bodies of evidence, and accepted by the scientific community. The word "theory" in science means the opposite of what it means in casual conversation. The SOL specifically tests whether students understand this distinction.

Types, Uses, & Limitations of Scientific Models
🔑
Why This Standard Matters

Model questions appear on the SOL regularly — often as part of a question about cell structure, DNA, or ecology that also tests whether you understand what models can and cannot do.
What Is a Scientific Model?

A scientific model is a representation of something that is too large, too small, too complex, too slow, too fast, or too dangerous to study directly. Models are tools scientists use to simplify reality so they can understand, explain, predict, and communicate about natural phenomena. Every model is, by definition, a simplification — and that is both its strength and its limitation.

Think about why we use models in biology. You cannot watch DNA replicate inside a living cell in real time with your naked eye — it is too small and too fast. You cannot observe evolution over millions of years — it is too slow. You cannot release a deadly virus into a population to study its spread — it is too dangerous. So instead, scientists build models: physical replicas, mathematical equations, computer simulations, and diagrams that capture the essential features of the real thing while leaving out unnecessary details.

The key insight for the SOL: a model is not the real thing. It is a simplified representation. Every model has strengths (what it shows well) and limitations (what it leaves out or gets wrong). The SOL will test whether you understand both.

Why Models Are Useful

Models serve four primary purposes in science, and the SOL may ask about any of them:

1. Predict: Models allow scientists to make predictions about what will happen under new conditions. A mathematical population model can predict whether a species will go extinct under different conservation strategies. A computer model of climate can predict temperature changes over the next 50 years.

2. Explain: Models help explain complex processes by breaking them into simpler visual or mathematical parts. A diagram of cellular respiration shows where each step occurs (glycolysis in cytoplasm, Krebs cycle in mitochondrial matrix, electron transport chain on inner membrane) in a way that a paragraph of text cannot.

3. Communicate: Models allow scientists to share ideas clearly with other scientists and with the public. Watson and Crick's physical model of DNA communicated the double-helix structure more effectively than words alone ever could.

4. Test ideas safely: Models allow engineers and scientists to test solutions before building or implementing them in the real world. A virtual model of a new drug binding to a receptor can be tested millions of times in a computer before a single dose is given to a patient. This connects to the engineering design process — models are essential for testing designs before construction.

Limitations of Models — What the SOL Actually Tests

This is the part that shows up most often on the SOL. The test loves to ask: "A student builds a model of X. What is a limitation of this model?" You need to know the general categories of limitation:

Simplified: Every model leaves out details. A 3D cell model made of clay cannot show the thousands of chemical reactions happening inside the cell every second. A food web diagram cannot show every single species in an ecosystem — only the major ones.

Not to scale: Physical models often cannot accurately represent the true sizes or distances involved. A model of the solar system in a classroom cannot show the actual distances between planets. A cell model cannot show the true size ratio between organelles.

Static vs. dynamic: Physical models and diagrams are usually static — they show a single moment frozen in time. But biological processes are dynamic — they involve movement, change, and interaction over time. A clay DNA model cannot show replication happening. A cell diagram cannot show vesicles moving from the ER to the Golgi.

Must be updated: Models are based on current knowledge. When new evidence emerges, models must be revised. The model of the atom has been revised many times (Dalton → Thomson → Rutherford → Bohr → quantum). The cell theory was updated when microscopes improved. DNA structure was revised as X-ray crystallography data became available. A model that cannot be updated with new evidence is not a good scientific model.

🎯
SOL Pattern: "What is a limitation of this model?"
When you see this question, the answer is almost always one of: (1) the model is simplified and does not show all details, (2) the model cannot show processes in action, (3) the model is not to scale, or (4) the model does not capture all variables. Pick the answer that describes something the model cannot do that the real thing can do.

Science is a self-correcting process. As new tools and techniques produce new evidence, existing models are revised or replaced. This is not a weakness — it is a strength of the scientific process. The SOL may ask why a model changed or what caused it to be updated. The answer is always: new evidence.

BIO.1f — Evaluating Sources & Communication
Source Reliability · Peer Review · Communicating Scientific Information
Why Source Evaluation Matters in Science

Science depends on reliable information. Every conclusion, every treatment, every policy recommendation is only as good as the evidence it is built on. If the evidence comes from a biased, unreliable, or unreviewed source, the conclusion built on that evidence is worthless — or worse, dangerous. The Virginia SOL tests whether you can distinguish between sources that have been rigorously reviewed and sources that have not.

This is not an abstract skill. In the real world, unreliable sources have led people to reject vaccines, take harmful supplements, and misunderstand diseases. In biology class and on the SOL, you need to know which sources have been through a quality-control process and which have not. The SOL will typically present four source options and ask which is "most reliable" or "best" for researching a specific topic. The answer follows a clear hierarchy.

What Is Peer Review? — Step by Step

Peer review is the quality-control process that separates scientific journals from every other type of publication. Understanding this process is essential because the SOL may ask why peer-reviewed sources are more reliable, or what happens during the peer review process.

Here is how it works, step by step:

StepWhat HappensWhy It Matters
1. SubmissionA scientist or team writes a paper describing their hypothesis, methods, data, analysis, and conclusions. They submit it to a scientific journal.The paper must be complete and follow the journal's formatting and ethical standards before it is even considered.
2. Editorial screeningThe journal's editor reads the paper to determine if it is within the journal's scope and meets basic quality standards.Papers that are off-topic, poorly written, or obviously flawed are rejected immediately — they never reach reviewers.
3. Expert reviewThe editor sends the paper to 2-4 independent scientists who are experts in the same field. These reviewers do NOT know who wrote the paper (in double-blind review). They examine the methods, check the data analysis, look for errors, and evaluate whether the conclusions are supported by the evidence.This is the critical step. Independent experts catch mistakes, flawed reasoning, missing controls, and unsupported claims that the original authors may have missed.
4. DecisionReviewers send their feedback to the editor. The editor makes one of three decisions: accept (rare on first submission), revise and resubmit (most common — authors must address all reviewer concerns), or reject (the paper is fundamentally flawed or not novel enough).Most papers go through multiple rounds of revision before being accepted. This back-and-forth process strengthens the final paper.
5. PublicationOnly after passing review and any required revisions is the paper published in the journal, where other scientists and the public can read it.By the time you read a peer-reviewed paper, it has been scrutinized by the authors, the editor, and multiple independent experts. This does not guarantee it is perfect — but it is far more reliable than information that has NOT been through this process.
🔑
Why Peer Review Matters
Peer review catches errors before they reach the public. It prevents fraud — if you fabricate data, expert reviewers familiar with the field are likely to notice inconsistencies. It maintains standards — journals with rigorous peer review have higher credibility. It is the reason peer-reviewed journals sit at the top of the reliability hierarchy. No blog, news article, or social media post goes through this process.
Communicating Scientific Information

Science is only useful if it is communicated effectively. The VDOE framework requires students to "communicate scientific and/or technical information about phenomena in multiple formats." This means scientists (and students) must be able to present findings using:

Written reports: Lab reports follow a standard structure — purpose, hypothesis, materials, procedure, data, analysis, conclusion. Each section serves a specific communication purpose. The conclusion must be supported by the data presented, not by opinion or outside sources not cited.

Data displays: Tables organize raw data. Graphs visualize trends and relationships. The choice of graph type matters — bar graphs for comparing categories, line graphs for showing change over time, scatter plots for showing relationships between two continuous variables. A well-chosen graph communicates a pattern instantly that would take paragraphs to describe in words.

Oral presentations and posters: Scientists present findings at conferences. Students present findings in class. In both cases, the claim must be supported by evidence, and the evidence must come from reliable sources or original data.

Evaluating Competing Arguments

Sometimes two scientists or two studies reach different conclusions about the same topic. The SOL may ask how to evaluate which explanation is better. The answer is always: look at the evidence. The explanation supported by more evidence, better-designed experiments, larger sample sizes, and peer-reviewed publications is the stronger one. Personal opinion, popularity, or the reputation of the scientist does NOT determine which explanation is correct — only the evidence does.

When comparing two arguments, ask these questions: (1) Is the evidence from a controlled experiment or just an observation? (2) Was the study peer-reviewed? (3) Has the result been replicated by other scientists? (4) Is the sample size large enough to be meaningful? (5) Does the conclusion go beyond what the data actually shows? The argument that better answers these questions is the more scientifically supported one.

⚠️
SOL Warning: "Most Reliable" vs. "Most Current"
The SOL sometimes asks for the "most current AND reliable" source. A textbook may be reliable but outdated. A scientific journal provides the most current peer-reviewed research. If the question emphasizes current information (recent discoveries, new treatments), a scientific journal is always the best answer — because journals publish new research continuously, while textbooks are updated only every few years.
