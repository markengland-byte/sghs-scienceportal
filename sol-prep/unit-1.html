BIO.1a — Experimental Design
Variables, Controls & Hypotheses
🎯
This Is Worth ~2 Questions on the SOL
Nearly every BIO.1 question starts with a scenario. You must be able to quickly identify the IV, DV, constants, and control group from a short paragraph. Drill the routine below until it's automatic.
The Experiment Routine: 4 Questions to Ask Every Time
Question to AskWhat It IdentifiesSOL Example Answer
What did the scientist CHANGE?Independent Variable (IV)"Amount of fertilizer" — the scientist used 5g, 10g, and 15g in different groups
What did the scientist MEASURE?Dependent Variable (DV)"Height of the plants after 2 weeks" — what was observed as a result
What STAYED THE SAME in all groups?Constants (controlled variables)"Type of plant, amount of water, amount of sunlight, type of soil" — all identical
Which group received NO treatment?Control Group"Group receiving 0g fertilizer" — the baseline comparison group
The Hypothesis: If…Then…Because

A valid hypothesis must be testable (you can design an experiment) and falsifiable (it could be proven wrong). The standard SOL format is:

"If [independent variable] is [changed in this way],
then [dependent variable] will [predicted result],
because [scientific reasoning]."
⚠️
Common Mistake: Confusing IV and DV
The IV is the CAUSE — it's what you set up BEFORE the experiment. The DV is the EFFECT — it changes BECAUSE of the IV and is measured at the END. Ask: "Could this variable be set up before I start?" = IV. "Do I have to wait to measure this?" = DV.
Why the Control Group Matters

Without a control group, you cannot determine whether your treatment caused the results. If plants with fertilizer grow taller than plants without fertilizer, the fertilizer likely caused the growth. Without the unfertilized control group, you'd have no comparison.

🧠
Memory: "Control = Compare"
The control group is what you COMPARE the experimental groups against. It typically receives: no treatment (placebo), OR the standard/normal treatment. Everything else stays identical. "If there's no control, there's no comparison — and no valid conclusion."
Quick Check — Can You Identify the Variables?
? (2006-42) A scientist designed an experiment to test the effect of temperature on bacterial growth. He grew three different cultures of the bacterium E. coli under three heat lamps at different temperatures. What was the independent variable in this experiment?
  • A
    Length of the experiment
  • B
    Number of bacteria
  • C
    Reproduction rate
  • D
    Temperature
The scientist deliberately CHANGED the temperature (three different temperatures). Temperature is what was manipulated → Independent Variable. Number of bacteria and reproduction rate are results (DV).
? (2001-14) Two plant species found in a dry region of the western United States exhibit vastly different abilities to survive. Species A has very slow stem growth and few leaves but is very abundant. Species B has rapid stem growth and many leaves but is very rare. Which hypothesis is most likely supported by this information?
  • A
    Flower size and color may give species B an advantage over species A.
  • B
    Reduced root growth may give species A an advantage over species B.
  • C
    Leaf shape may give species B an advantage over species A.
  • D
    Reduced stem growth may give species A an advantage over species B.
In a DRY environment, slow stem growth and few leaves means LESS water loss through transpiration. Species A is abundant because its traits conserve water — a survival advantage. The hypothesis must connect the observable trait (reduced stem growth) to the survival outcome. This is the kind of "which hypothesis is supported?" question the SOL loves.

BIO.1b — Data & Measurement
Qualitative vs. Quantitative · Accuracy vs. Precision · Sample Size
Qualitative vs. Quantitative Data
TypeDefinitionExamplesSOL Tip
QualitativeDescriptive observations using words — no numbers"The liquid turned blue." "The plant is wilting." "The colony smells sour."Any time you see a word like "appears," "looks," "smells," or "feels" = qualitative
QuantitativeNumerical observations using measurements"The plant grew 12.5 cm." "3 out of 10 mice survived." "pH = 4.2"Any time you see a NUMBER or UNIT = quantitative
🧠
Memory: "Quantity = Number"
Quantitative has the word "quantity" in it → numbers. Qualitative has "quality" → descriptions. If it has a unit (cm, g, °C, mL), it is always quantitative.
Accuracy vs. Precision — The Bull's-Eye Model
TermDefinitionBull's-Eye AnalogyExample
AccuracyHow close the measurement is to the TRUE (accepted) valueShots cluster near the center (bull's-eye)Measuring boiling water: 99°C, 100°C, 101°C (accurate — near 100°C)
PrecisionHow CONSISTENT/REPEATABLE the measurements areShots cluster tightly together (even if far from center)Measuring boiling water: 95°C, 95°C, 95°C (precise — consistent, but inaccurate)
🎯
The 4-Case SOL Question — Know All Four
Accurate AND Precise: Results close to true value AND close together (99°C, 100°C, 100°C ✓)
Precise but NOT Accurate: Results close together but far from true value (e.g., 93°C, 93°C, 93°C — consistent but wrong)
Accurate but NOT Precise: Results scattered around the true value (95°C, 100°C, 106°C — average near 100°C but spread)
Neither: Results scattered AND far from true value (75°C, 95°C, 110°C)
Sample Size & Replication
TermDefinitionWhy It Matters
Sample SizeThe number of subjects, trials, or observations in an experimentSmall sample size = unreliable results; a sample of 3 mice could vary wildly by chance. Large samples average out individual variation.
ReplicationRepeating the entire experiment (not just multiple subjects)If independent researchers get the same result, confidence increases that the finding is real, not a fluke of one lab
⚠️
SOL Trap: "Only 3 subjects were tested"
When a question asks WHY a study is unreliable or invalid, "small sample size" is almost always the correct answer. On the SOL, if they tell you an experiment used 3, 4, or 5 subjects, the answer is almost always that the sample size is too small.
Quick Check — Accuracy, Precision & Data Types
? (2002-46) Jan consistently read the volume of liquids at the top of the meniscus instead of the bottom. How would this practice impact her work?
  • A
    Her measurements would lack precision.
  • B
    Her measurements would be too high.
  • C
    Her measurements would be very accurate.
  • D
    Her measurements would be too low for less dense liquids.
Reading the meniscus at the top instead of the bottom consistently gives a HIGHER reading than the true volume. The measurements are precise (consistent — she makes the same error every time) but NOT accurate (systematically too high). This is a classic measurement error question.
? (2006-8) In an experiment, the height of several plants was recorded daily in millimeters. Which tool would be the most accurate and appropriate for this measurement?
  • A
    Yardstick
  • B
    Graduated cylinder
  • C
    Digital scale
  • D
    Metric ruler
Plant height is a LENGTH measurement in MILLIMETERS. A metric ruler measures length in mm. A graduated cylinder measures liquid volume, a digital scale measures mass, and a yardstick uses inches/feet (not metric). Always match the TOOL to the TYPE of measurement and UNIT required.

BIO.1c — Graph Interpretation
Reading & Interpreting Scientific Graphs
🎯
Graph Questions Appear on EVERY SOL Test
At least 1-2 questions on every Biology SOL involve reading a graph. The question is never just "read the number" — it asks you to draw a conclusion, identify a trend, or predict what happens beyond the graph. The most common miss: students read the right number but pick the wrong conclusion from the options.
Graph Types & When to Use Each
Graph TypeBest Used ForWhat to Look For
Bar GraphComparing groups or categories (different soils, different treatments)Tallest bar = highest value; compare heights across groups
Line GraphShowing change over time or a continuous variable (temperature vs. rate)Slope direction (rising/falling), peak/trough, where curves cross
Scatter PlotShowing the relationship between two variables (study time vs. grade)Trend direction: positive (both increase), negative (one increases, other decreases), no correlation (scattered)
Pie ChartShowing parts of a whole (% composition)Largest slice = largest proportion
Practice: Enzyme Activity vs. Temperature
Enzyme Reaction Rate at Different Temperatures
A typical bell-curve enzyme activity graph — this type appears frequently on the Virginia Biology SOL
📊 Based on this graph, at which temperature is the enzyme MOST active?
A) 10°C — enzymes work best in cold conditions
B) 37°C — this is the peak/optimum temperature on the graph
C) 50°C — high temperature increases reaction rate
D) 70°C — the enzyme is completely denatured here
✅ Correct! The peak of the curve is at 37°C — this is the enzyme's optimal temperature. Above 37°C the enzyme begins to denature (unfold) and activity drops sharply to zero by ~60°C.
❌ Look at the peak of the line — it is highest at 37°C. That peak is the OPTIMUM temperature. High temperatures (above ~45°C) cause the enzyme to denature, dropping activity to zero.
Practice: Population Growth
Population Growth: Species A vs. Species B
Line graphs comparing two populations — another common SOL format
📊 In what year did Species A's population FIRST exceed Species B's?
A) Year 2
B) Year 3
C) Year 4 — this is where the two lines cross
D) Year 6
✅ Year 4 is where the lines cross — Species A rises above Species B. Before year 4, Species B had more individuals; after year 4, Species A has more.
❌ Find where the two lines intersect (cross) — that's where A first equals then exceeds B. The intersection is at Year 4.
5 Rules for SOL Graph Questions
#Rule
1Read the axes first. What is on the x-axis? What is on the y-axis? What are the units? Do this before looking at the data.
2Identify the trend. Is it increasing? Decreasing? Leveling off? Peaking? Crossing?
3Only conclude what the graph shows. Don't add outside knowledge. The correct answer is always directly supported by the graph.
4Beware of cause-and-effect answer choices. A graph shows correlation — it cannot prove causation. Wrong choices often say "causes" when the data only shows a relationship.
5Watch for "best describes" vs. "proves." A graph can "show" or "suggest" a trend — it rarely "proves" causation.
Quick Check — Graph Interpretation
? (2005-7) A biologist tracked a bluegill fish population in a lake over several years. The data showed the population was 200 in 1990, rose to 350 by 1993, held steady at 350 through 1996, then declined to 180 by 1999. During which time period did the overall bluegill population decline?
  • A
    1990–1993
  • B
    1996–1999
  • C
    1993–1996
  • D
    1990–1996
The population DECLINED from 350 to 180 during 1996–1999. From 1990–1993, it was INCREASING. From 1993–1996, it was STABLE (no change). On graph questions, always look at the DIRECTION of the line during the specific time period asked about.
? (2003-32) A graph shows the number of mice born each week: Week 1 = 20, Week 2 = 40, Week 3 = 60, Week 4 = 80. If the trend continues, how many mice will be born in Week 5?
  • A
    90
  • B
    100
  • C
    140
  • D
    160
The pattern increases by +20 each week: 20, 40, 60, 80… the next value is 100. This is a PREDICTION question — you extend the existing trend. Always identify the pattern first (here: constant increase of 20/week), then apply it to the next data point.

BIO.1d — Scientific Reasoning
Correlation vs. Causation · Source Reliability · Experimental Errors
Correlation vs. Causation — The Most Misunderstood Concept in Biology
TermMeaningSOL Example
CorrelationTwo variables change together — but one does NOT necessarily CAUSE the other. A graph, scatter plot, or data table can show a correlation."Cities with more hospitals have more deaths." Both are higher in bigger cities. Hospitals don't cause death — city size causes both.
CausationOne variable DIRECTLY causes the change in another. Requires a controlled experiment to establish."Smoking increases risk of lung cancer" — proven through controlled studies showing the biological mechanism.
🎯
SOL Trap: "A student CONCLUDES that X CAUSES Y from the graph"
On the SOL, they will show you data with two correlated variables and one answer choice will say something "causes" the other. That is almost always WRONG — data alone cannot prove causation. The correct answer will say "there is a relationship between" or "as X increases, Y also increases" (correlation language). Watch for the word "causes" in answer choices — it's usually the distractor.
Source Reliability — Which Sources Can You Trust?
Source TypeReliabilityReason
Peer-reviewed journal article🟢 HighestReviewed by expert scientists before publication; most rigorous standard
Government agency website (.gov)🟢 HighCDC, NIH, VDOE — accountable, evidence-based
University/research institution (.edu)🟡 Usually goodAcademically accountable but not all content peer-reviewed
News article (major outlet)🟡 MediumMay summarize studies but can oversimplify; check primary source
Personal blog, social media🔴 LowNo review process; anyone can post anything
Advertisement / product website🔴 LowestFinancial interest in a conclusion; not objective
Types of Experimental Errors
Error TypeDefinitionExample
Procedural ErrorMistake in how the experiment was conductedStudent forgot to add the reagent; timing was off
Measurement ErrorInaccurate reading of an instrument or recordingReading a graduated cylinder from the wrong angle
Sampling ErrorSample was too small or not representativeTested only 3 patients; tested only male rats
BiasExperimenter's expectation influences the resultsUnconsciously rounding measurements toward the expected value
🔑
Inference vs. Observation
Observation: What you directly see, hear, measure — a fact recorded at the time. "The solution turned red." "The mouse ran 14 laps." Inference: A conclusion drawn FROM observations based on prior knowledge. "The solution is acidic." (inferred from color change) "The mouse appears stressed." (inferred from behavior). The SOL sometimes asks which statement is an inference vs. an observation — look for statements that go beyond the raw data.
Hypothesis vs. Theory vs. Law

The SOL requires you to distinguish between these three levels of scientific knowledge. They are NOT a hierarchy — a theory does NOT "graduate" into a law.

TermDefinitionKey PointExample
HypothesisA testable, falsifiable predictionCan be supported or rejected by a single experiment. A starting point."If fertilizer is added, then plant height will increase."
TheoryA well-substantiated explanation supported by a large body of evidence, tested repeatedlyNOT "just a guess." One of the strongest forms of scientific knowledge. Explains WHY something happens.Cell Theory, Theory of Evolution, Germ Theory of Disease
LawA description of an observed pattern in nature, often mathematicalDescribes WHAT happens, not WHY. Does not explain the mechanism.Law of Conservation of Energy, Law of Segregation
⚠️
Key Distinction: A theory does NOT become a law
They serve different purposes. A theory explains WHY something happens (mechanism). A law describes WHAT happens (pattern). The Theory of Evolution will never become the "Law of Evolution" — it already explains the mechanism. The Law of Gravity describes what happens (objects attract), while the theory of gravity explains why.
🎯
SOL Trap: "Evolution is JUST a theory"
On the SOL, a theory is NOT a guess. A scientific theory is one of the strongest forms of scientific knowledge. It has been tested repeatedly, supported by mountains of evidence, and accepted by the scientific community. If an answer choice implies a theory is "just a guess" or "unproven," it is WRONG. The SOL specifically tests whether students understand the scientific meaning of "theory."
Quick Check — Scientific Reasoning
? (2006-22) Data about the climate in an ecosystem were collected for 30 years. Which hypothesis about a population of eagles could be made based on the climate data collected?
  • A
    Eagle chick survival is directly related to annual rainfall amounts.
  • B
    An outbreak of disease in 1987 killed 82% of the eagle population.
  • C
    Eagles remain with the same mate throughout their lifetime.
  • D
    Mortality of eagles from pesticides after 1992 was less than 5%.
The data collected was about CLIMATE — so any valid hypothesis must RELATE to climate variables. Only choice A connects climate data (rainfall) to an eagle population outcome (chick survival). Choices B, C, and D involve disease, mating behavior, and pesticides — none of these can be hypothesized from climate data alone. A hypothesis must be based on the available data.
? (2004-38) Richard was observing black swallowtail butterflies in the field. Which one of these is an observation he made about a black swallowtail butterfly?
  • A
    The black swallowtail belongs to the class Insecta.
  • B
    The black swallowtail laid its eggs on a parsley plant.
  • C
    The black swallowtail likes to fly more than anything else.
  • D
    The black swallowtail is closely related to the spicebush swallowtail.
An OBSERVATION is something you directly SEE happen. Richard could directly see the butterfly lay eggs on parsley (B). Choice A is a classification (requires prior knowledge, not field observation). Choice C is an inference about preference ("likes to fly"). Choice D is an inference about evolutionary relationships. Only B describes something directly witnessed in the field.

KEY: Data/graphs show correlation. Only controlled experiments prove causation. Watch for "causes" in answer choices — usually WRONG.
Source Reliability Ranking
● Peer-reviewed journal (HIGHEST)
● Government agency (.gov)
● University / .edu website
● Major news outlet
● Blog / social media (LOW)
● Ads / product websites (LOWEST)
Top 5 SOL Tips for BIO.1
  1. IV = what they CHANGE. DV = what they MEASURE.
  2. No control group = no valid conclusion.
  3. Small sample size = unreliable results.
  4. Graphs show correlation, NOT causation.
  5. Peer-reviewed journal = most reliable source.

1(2006-42) A scientist designed an experiment to test the effect of temperature on bacterial growth. He grew three different cultures of E. coli under three heat lamps at different temperatures. What was the independent variable in this experiment?BIO.1a
  • A
    Length of the experiment
  • B
    Number of bacteria
  • C
    Reproduction rate
  • D
    Temperature
The independent variable is what the scientist deliberately CHANGES. Here, the scientist set different temperatures for each culture group — that is the IV. Number of bacteria and reproduction rate are the measured results (DV).
2(2003-41) A student wanted to look at plant growth in five different soil samples. He planted the same type of seeds in identical containers and left them together in full sunlight. He gave each plant the same amount of water and charted the growth of each plant stem. What is the independent variable in this experiment?BIO.1a
  • A
    Seeds
  • B
    Soil
  • C
    Light
  • D
    Container
The student changed the SOIL between groups — that's the IV. Seeds, containers, sunlight, and water were all kept the same (constants). Plant stem growth is the DV (what was measured).
3(2004-33) Orchids were studied to determine if the amount of humidity affected the flowering of these plants. Which of these was the independent variable in this study?BIO.1a
  • A
    The amount watered
  • B
    The percentage of humidity
  • C
    The length of time required for flowering
  • D
    The number of flowers on each plant
The experiment tests whether humidity affects flowering. Humidity is what was CHANGED (IV). Flowering time and number of flowers are what was MEASURED (DV).
4(2001-45) Which sentence best states the importance of using control groups?BIO.1a
  • A
    Control groups provide a method by which statistical variability can be reduced.
  • B
    Control groups allow comparison between subjects receiving a treatment and those receiving no treatment.
  • C
    Control groups eliminate the need for statistical tests and simplify calculations.
  • D
    Control groups eliminate the need for large sample sizes, reducing the number of measurements needed.
The whole point of a control group is COMPARISON. Without it, you have no baseline to determine whether the treatment caused the observed effect. "Control = Compare."
5(2004-23) A student is trying to determine the effect of pollutants on the growth of three groups of seeds. The results will not be valid because the experiment has no —BIO.1a
  • A
    Variable
  • B
    Hypothesis
  • C
    Control
  • D
    Conclusion
Without a control group (seeds grown WITHOUT pollutants), there is no baseline for comparison. You cannot determine if the pollutants caused any effect. No control = no valid conclusion.
6(2006-22) Data about the climate in an ecosystem were collected for 30 years. Which hypothesis about a population of eagles could be made based on the climate data collected?BIO.1a
  • A
    Eagle chick survival is directly related to annual rainfall amounts.
  • B
    An outbreak of disease in 1987 killed 82% of the eagle population.
  • C
    Eagles remain with the same mate throughout their lifetime.
  • D
    Mortality of eagles from pesticides after 1992 was less than 5%.
A hypothesis must be BASED on the data collected. Since CLIMATE data (including rainfall) was collected, only a hypothesis relating eagle survival to climate factors (like rainfall) makes sense. Disease, mating, and pesticides are not climate data.
7(2006-8) In an experiment, the height of several plants was recorded daily in millimeters. Which tool would be the most accurate and appropriate for this measurement?BIO.1b
  • A
    Yardstick
  • B
    Graduated cylinder
  • C
    Digital scale
  • D
    Metric ruler
You need to measure HEIGHT in MILLIMETERS. A metric ruler measures length in mm. A yardstick uses inches, a graduated cylinder measures liquid volume, and a scale measures mass — none are appropriate for mm height measurement.
8(2005-8) A biology class of 24 students decides to measure the height of each student and then calculate the average height. Which of these is a possible source of error in this activity?BIO.1b
  • A
    The number of males and females in the class
  • B
    The difference in the ages of the students in the class
  • C
    The total number of students in the class
  • D
    The accuracy of making and recording measurements
Measurement error comes from HOW you take and record measurements. Inaccurate readings (wrong position, rounding errors, misreading the tool) are the most common experimental error source.
9(2003-10) An experiment tested the effectiveness of four fertilizers on plant growth. Two grams of each fertilizer were diluted in 9 mL of water. Which measuring device would introduce the least error into the measurement of the 9 mL of water?BIO.1b
  • A
    20 mL graduated cylinder
  • B
    100 mL graduated cylinder
  • C
    10 mL graduated cylinder
  • D
    50 mL graduated cylinder
Use the graduated cylinder closest in size to what you are measuring. 9 mL is closest to 10 mL capacity. Smaller cylinders have finer graduations and introduce less measurement error than larger ones for the same volume.
10(2002-50) A student wants to view cells under the compound microscope at a total magnification of 400X. If the eyepiece is 10X, which of the following objective lenses should be used?BIO.1b
  • A
    10X
  • B
    4X
  • C
    100X
  • D
    40X
Total magnification = eyepiece x objective lens. So 400X = 10X x 40X. The student needs the 40X objective lens.
11(2002-40) A student wanted to study the effect of temperature on algae levels in a local stream. Which items are most useful for gathering data and most appropriate for communicating the results?BIO.1b
  • A
    pH strips and written observations of stream water
  • B
    Test tubes, thermometers, and graphs of results
  • C
    Research on the Internet and videotapes of water samples
  • D
    Microscopes and written descriptions of weather patterns
To study temperature vs. algae, you need to MEASURE temperature (thermometer), collect samples (test tubes), and COMMUNICATE results (graphs). This combination best matches data gathering AND presentation.
12(2003-32) According to a graph of mouse births over time, how many mice will be born in week 5 if the trend continues?BIO.1c
  • A
    90
  • B
    100
  • C
    140
  • D
    160
To predict a trend, extend the pattern. If births are increasing by a consistent amount each week, continue that pattern to week 5. Exponential growth in mouse populations leads to 160 by week 5.
13(2004-29) A graph shows lynx and hare populations between 1845 and 1940. If a predator of the lynx enters the food chain, you might expect the number of —BIO.1c
  • A
    Lynx and hares to become equal
  • B
    Lynx to increase
  • C
    Hares to increase
  • D
    Hares and lynx to decrease
If a new predator eats lynx, the lynx population DECREASES. Fewer lynx means fewer hares are eaten, so the hare population INCREASES. This is a predator-prey cascade.
14(2003-19) A graph shows the activity rates of two enzymes (pepsin and trypsin) at different pH levels. This graph shows that —BIO.1c
  • A
    More enzymes are present at a higher pH
  • B
    pH affects the activity rate of enzymes
  • C
    Pepsin is less effective at low pH than trypsin
  • D
    Pepsin is less sensitive to pH than trypsin
The graph shows enzyme activity CHANGES with pH — each enzyme has a different optimum pH. This directly supports that pH affects enzyme activity rates. The graph does not show enzyme amounts or compare sensitivities.
15(2005-37) A graph of sheep population in Tasmania from 1840 to 1920 suggests the carrying capacity was approximately —BIO.1c
  • A
    0.75 million
  • B
    1.00 million
  • C
    1.75 million
  • D
    2.25 million
Carrying capacity is the maximum population an environment can sustain — shown on a graph where the line LEVELS OFF (plateaus). The sheep population leveled off at approximately 1.75 million.
16(2004-38) Richard was observing black swallowtail butterflies in the field. Which one of these is an observation he made about a black swallowtail butterfly?BIO.1d
  • A
    The black swallowtail belongs to the class Insecta.
  • B
    The black swallowtail laid its eggs on a parsley plant.
  • C
    The black swallowtail likes to fly more than anything else.
  • D
    The black swallowtail is closely related to the spicebush swallowtail.
An OBSERVATION is what you directly see. Richard could directly SEE the butterfly laying eggs on parsley. Classification (A), preferences (C), and relationships (D) are all inferences — they go beyond what was directly seen.
17(2003-2) Which of these would be the best resource to research the symptoms and treatment of hypoglycemia?BIO.1d
  • A
    Daily television news
  • B
    New England Journal of Medicine
  • C
    Journal of Zoology
  • D
    Natural History Magazine
Hypoglycemia is a MEDICAL condition. The New England Journal of Medicine is a peer-reviewed MEDICAL journal — the most reliable and relevant source. Zoology studies animals, not human medical conditions.
18(2005-42) In 1869, DNA was discovered within the nuclei of cells. By the 1940s, scientists knew chromosomes were made of DNA and protein. In the 1950s, scientists demonstrated DNA is the hereditary material. In 1953, using information from other scientists, an American biologist and an English physicist built a model of DNA. These discoveries best illustrate the importance of —BIO.1d
  • A
    Replication of results
  • B
    Collaborative efforts among scientists
  • C
    Recent improvements in the scientific method
  • D
    Independent research
Each discovery BUILT on previous scientists' work — from DNA discovery (1869) to the double helix model (1953). Multiple scientists across decades worked together. This is collaborative effort, not independent work.
19(2004-25) Which of the following best explains why a student researching genetics should use the most recent textbooks available?BIO.1d
  • A
    No technologies from more than ten years ago are still in use.
  • B
    Research in Mendelian genetics began very recently.
  • C
    New discoveries frequently add to older knowledge in genetics.
  • D
    Older textbooks are more difficult to understand.
Genetics is a rapidly advancing field. New discoveries (gene editing, genomics, CRISPR) are constantly expanding our knowledge. Recent textbooks include these advances — older ones do not. Science builds on previous knowledge.
20(2006-17) A student's experiment showed that earthworms move away from light. This statement should be classified as —BIO.1d
  • A
    An inference
  • B
    A prediction
  • C
    A hypothesis
  • D
    A conclusion
The experiment was DONE — the student observed the result. A statement summarizing what the experiment SHOWED is a conclusion. A hypothesis comes BEFORE the experiment. A prediction forecasts what WILL happen. An inference goes beyond the data.
