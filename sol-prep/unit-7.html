Study Tip for BIO.7
The key concept: natural selection acts on EXISTING variation. Organisms don't "decide" to evolve. If you understand this 4-step process, you can answer any natural selection question.

BIO.7a — Fossil & DNA Evidence
How We Know Populations Change Over Time
🎯
Relative vs. Absolute Dating Is Directly Tested
The SOL will ask you to distinguish between relative dating (position in rock layers) and absolute dating (radioactive decay). You also need to know how DNA comparisons reveal evolutionary relationships — more similar DNA = more closely related.
The Fossil Record

A fossil is any preserved evidence of an organism that lived in the past — bones, shells, imprints, footprints, or even preserved organisms in amber or ice. The fossil record is the collection of all known fossils, and it provides a time-ordered history of life on Earth spanning ~3.5 billion years.

By studying the fossil record, scientists can: track how species have changed over time, identify patterns of diversity and extinction, compare extinct organisms with living ones, and infer evolutionary relationships.

Relative Dating vs. Absolute Dating
FeatureRelative DatingAbsolute Dating
What it tells youWhich fossil is OLDER or YOUNGER relative to othersThe EXACT age of a fossil (in years)
MethodPosition in rock layers — deeper layers = olderRadioactive decay of isotopes (e.g., carbon-14, potassium-40)
PrecisionLess precise — "older than" or "younger than"Very precise — "approximately 65 million years old"
Key principleLaw of superposition: bottom layers deposited firstHalf-life: time for half of a radioactive isotope to decay
🧠
Memory: Relative = Layers, Absolute = Numbers
Relative dating tells you "this fossil is older THAN that one" (position in rock). Absolute dating tells you "this fossil is 65 million years old" (exact number from radioactive decay). Relative = relative position. Absolute = absolute number.
DNA Evidence for Evolution

Comparative genomics compares DNA sequences across species. The more similar the DNA, the more recently two species shared a common ancestor. For example, humans and chimpanzees share ~98% of their DNA, indicating a very recent common ancestor. Humans and bananas share ~60%, indicating a much more distant relationship.

DNA analysis has become the most powerful tool for determining evolutionary relationships — more accurate than physical appearance alone, because organisms can look similar due to similar environments (convergent evolution) even if they're not closely related.

Homologous Structures

Homologous structures are body parts that share a similar underlying structure because they come from a common ancestor, even though they may serve different functions today. The classic example: the forelimbs of humans (arm), whales (flipper), bats (wing), and cats (leg) all have the same bones (humerus, radius, ulna) arranged in the same pattern. Different functions, same origin = evidence of common ancestry.

BIO.7b/c — Genetic Variation & Natural Selection
How Variation Arises & How Nature Selects
🎯
Natural Selection ≠ Lamarck's Acquired Characteristics
The SOL tests whether you understand the CORRECT mechanism of evolution (natural selection) vs. the INCORRECT one (Lamarck). Organisms do NOT evolve because they "want to" or "need to." Evolution happens because EXISTING genetic variation is selected by the environment. Individuals don't change — populations change over generations.
Sources of Genetic Variation

Natural selection can only work if there is variation in a population. Without differences, there's nothing to select. The VDOE framework identifies three main sources:

SourceHow It Creates Variation
MutationsRandom changes in DNA sequence. Most are neutral, some are harmful, a few are beneficial. The raw material for evolution.
Gene flowMovement of genes between populations through migration. Introduces new alleles into a population.
Sexual reproductionCrossing over, independent assortment, and random fertilization shuffle alleles into unique combinations each generation.
Natural Selection: The 4-Step Process

Darwin's theory of natural selection explains HOW populations evolve. It works through four steps:

StepWhat HappensExample
1. OverproductionSpecies produce MORE offspring than can surviveA frog lays 1,000 eggs, but only 10-20 survive to adulthood
2. VariationIndividuals in a population have DIFFERENT inherited traitsSome frogs are green, some are brown, some are spotted
3. Competition & SelectionOrganisms with traits better suited to the environment survive more oftenGreen frogs hiding in green vegetation avoid predators better than brown frogs
4. ReproductionSurvivors pass their favorable traits to offspring; over time, the trait becomes more commonAfter many generations, most frogs in that habitat are green
⚠️
SOL Trap: Lamarck vs. Darwin
Lamarck (WRONG): Organisms change during their lifetime because they "need" to, and pass those changes to offspring. Example: "Giraffes stretched their necks to reach high leaves, so their necks got longer." Darwin (CORRECT): Some giraffes were born with naturally longer necks (genetic variation). Those giraffes could reach more food, survived better, and passed the long-neck gene to more offspring. Over generations, the population shifted toward longer necks. Individuals don't evolve; populations do.
Environmental Pressures

Selective pressures are environmental factors that favor certain traits: predators, climate, disease, food availability, competition. When the environment changes, the traits that are "favorable" may change too. Organisms that can't adapt (don't have the right genetic variation) may go extinct.

The VDOE framework states: "Populations produce more offspring than the environment can support. Organisms with certain genetic variations will be favored to survive and pass their variations on to the next generation."

Reproductive Strategies

The VDOE framework requires you to "describe the effect of reproductive strategies and rates on a population's survival." Different species use different strategies:

StrategyDescriptionExamples
Many offspring, little careProduce thousands of eggs/young; most die, but sheer numbers ensure some surviveSea turtles (100+ eggs), frogs (1000+ eggs), fish (millions of eggs)
Few offspring, high careProduce few young; invest heavily in parental care to maximize each one's survivalHumans (1 baby), elephants (1 calf), eagles (1-2 chicks)

Both strategies are successful in different environments. The key insight: reproductive strategy affects population survival by balancing quantity vs. quality of offspring.

🔍 Worked SOL Example — Natural Selection

(2005-30) Antibiotic-resistant bacteria have developed in a short time because —

1
Apply natural selection: Antibiotics kill susceptible bacteria. A few bacteria have random mutations making them resistant.
2
Selection pressure: The antibiotic is the selective pressure. Resistant bacteria survive; susceptible ones die.
3
Why so fast? Bacteria reproduce RAPIDLY (every 20 minutes), so resistant populations can dominate within weeks.
4
Answer: bacteria reproduce rapidly.

BIO.7c/d — Adaptations & Speciation
Types of Adaptations, Speciation & Extinction
🎯
Adaptation Identification Is the Most Common BIO.7 Question
The SOL shows you a physical feature (foot shape, tooth type, body form, eye size) and asks what it tells you about the organism. Flat teeth = herbivore. Grasping feet = tree-dweller. Streamlined body = aquatic. Large eyes = nocturnal. Know the feature-to-function connections.
Types of Adaptations

An adaptation is an inherited trait that increases an organism's fitness (ability to survive and reproduce) in its environment. There are three types:

TypeDefinitionExamples
Anatomical (Structural)Physical body featuresThick fur in cold climates, webbed feet for swimming, sharp claws for climbing
BehavioralActions that increase survivalMigration, hibernation, nocturnal activity, courtship displays
PhysiologicalInternal body processesEndothermy (warm-blooded), venom production, ability to digest cellulose
Common SOL Adaptation Questions
FeatureAdaptation ForExample
Flat teethGrinding plants (herbivore)Cows, horses, hadrosaurus
Sharp pointed teethTearing meat (carnivore)Wolves, T. rex
Grasping curved feetPerching on branches (tree-dweller)Songbirds, owls
Talons (large curved claws)Catching and killing prey (predator)Hawks, eagles
Webbed feetSwimmingDucks, frogs
Wide flat feetWalking on sand/snow (weight distribution)Camels, snowshoe hares
Large eyesSeeing in low light (nocturnal)Owls, tarsiers
Large digging clawsBurrowing undergroundMoles, badgers
Streamlined bodyMoving through water efficientlyDolphins, sharks, penguins
Warning colorsSignaling danger to predatorsPoison dart frogs, coral snakes
Mimicry and Camouflage

Mimicry occurs when a harmless species evolves to resemble a dangerous one. The nonpoisonous eastern scarlet snake has colored bands resembling the poisonous coral snake — predators avoid both. Camouflage is when an organism blends in with its environment to avoid detection (e.g., a green insect on a green leaf).

Speciation

Speciation is the formation of a new species. It occurs when a population is split into groups that can no longer interbreed and produce fertile offspring. The most common cause is geographic isolation — a physical barrier (mountain, river, ocean) separates a population. Over time, the separated groups accumulate different mutations and adaptations, eventually becoming so different they can no longer interbreed.

Extinction

Extinction occurs when a species cannot adapt to changing environmental conditions and dies out completely. If a population lacks the genetic variation needed to survive a new selective pressure (disease, climate change, habitat loss, new predator), it goes extinct. The fossil record shows that ~99% of all species that ever lived are now extinct.

🔑
Adaptation vs. Extinction
If a population HAS genetic variation that helps some individuals survive a new pressure → natural selection acts → population adapts → species survives. If a population LACKS the right variation → no individuals survive → species goes extinct. Genetic diversity = survival insurance.

BIO.7d — Scientific Evidence for Evolution
Homologous Structures, Gradualism vs. Punctuated Equilibrium
🎯
Multiple Lines of Evidence Support Evolution
The SOL tests whether you understand that evolution is supported by evidence from MANY disciplines: paleontology (fossils), genetics (DNA), anatomy (homologous structures), embryology (similar development), and biochemistry (protein comparisons). No single piece of evidence stands alone — they all converge on the same conclusion.
Homologous vs. Analogous vs. Vestigial Structures
TypeDefinitionWhat It ShowsExample
HomologousSimilar structure, DIFFERENT function; from common ancestorCommon ancestry (divergent evolution)Human arm, whale flipper, bat wing — same bones, different uses
AnalogousSimilar FUNCTION, different structure; from different ancestorsConvergent evolution (similar environments)Bird wing vs. insect wing — both fly, completely different structure
VestigialReduced structure with no current functionAncestral traits from a common ancestorHuman appendix, whale hip bones, wingless beetle wing covers
🧠
Memory: Homo = Same Origin, Ana = Same Job
Homologous = same (homo) origin/structure, different function. Evidence of COMMON ANCESTRY. Analogous = same job/function, different origin. Evidence of CONVERGENT EVOLUTION (similar environments, not related). Vestigial = a "vestige" (leftover) from an ancestor — no longer used.
Gradualism vs. Punctuated Equilibrium

Scientists debate the PACE of evolutionary change:

ModelDescriptionEvidence
GradualismSlow, continuous change over millions of years; many intermediate forms in fossil recordGradual transitions seen in some fossil sequences (e.g., horse evolution)
Punctuated EquilibriumLong periods of stability (no change) interrupted by short bursts of rapid changeMany species appear suddenly in fossil record with few intermediates; rapid change after environmental upheaval

Both models are supported by evidence in different lineages. The SOL may ask you to compare them or identify which model a given fossil pattern supports.

Evidence from Multiple Disciplines

The VDOE framework states that "biological evolution is supported by scientific evidence from many disciplines":

  • Paleontology: Fossil record shows change over time, transitional forms, extinction patterns
  • Genetics/Biochemistry: DNA and protein sequence comparisons reveal relationships; universal genetic code
  • Anatomy: Homologous, analogous, and vestigial structures
  • Embryology: Similar embryonic stages across species suggest common ancestry
  • Biogeography: Distribution of species on islands and continents matches evolutionary predictions
  • Direct observation: Antibiotic resistance in bacteria, pesticide resistance in insects
🔑
Embryology Example
Fish, chicken, pig, and human embryos all develop gill slits, tails, and similar body segments in early development — even though only fish keep gills as adults. These shared early features are controlled by the same developmental genes inherited from a distant common ancestor. The more similar two species' embryonic stages, the more closely related they are.
🔑
Biogeography Example: Darwin's Finches
On the Galapagos Islands, Darwin found finches with very different beak shapes on different islands — some had thick beaks for cracking seeds, others had thin beaks for catching insects, others had curved beaks for eating cactus. All descended from a single ancestor that colonized the islands. Geographic isolation + different food sources + natural selection = different beak shapes = eventually different species. This is adaptive radiation driven by biogeography.
🔍 Worked SOL Example — Structural Comparison

(2002-35) Comparing a bat's pectoral girdle with a pterosaur's — which bone corresponds to the humerus?

1
Identify the concept: Comparing bone structures between two species = homologous structures.
2
Approach: Find the bone in the pterosaur that occupies the same position and has the same relative size/shape as the humerus in the bat.
3
Key insight: Even though bats and pterosaurs are not closely related (convergent evolution for flight), their forelimb bones can still be compared because all tetrapod forelimbs share the same basic bone pattern from a distant common ancestor.

Print for quick reference.

Evidence for Evolution (BIO.7a)

Fossils: preserved evidence of past life. Relative dating = rock layers (deeper = older). Absolute dating = radioactive decay (exact age). DNA comparison: more similar DNA = more closely related. Homologous structures: same bones, different function = common ancestor.

Natural Selection (BIO.7b/c)

4 steps: (1) Overproduction of offspring. (2) Variation exists. (3) Competition — favorable traits survive. (4) Those traits are passed on → population changes. Variation sources: mutations, gene flow, sexual reproduction. NOT Lamarck (organisms don't choose to evolve). Individuals don't evolve; populations do.

Gradualism = slow continuous change. Punctuated equilibrium = long stability + rapid bursts. Speciation = new species when populations can't interbreed. Extinction = can't adapt to change.

Absolute dating uses radioactive isotope decay to determine the exact age in years. Relative dating only tells you which is older/younger.

Highly similar DNA = recent common ancestor. 98% is very high (like humans and chimps) but they're still different species.

Similar embryonic development across very different species is strong evidence of shared common ancestry. The same developmental genes are active early, then diverge as the embryo matures. This is embryology evidence for evolution.

16 Darwin's finches on different Galapagos islands evolved different beak shapes. This is evidence that — BIO.7d

  • D

Different islands had different food sources (seeds, insects, cacti). Finches with beak shapes best suited to the available food survived and reproduced. Over time, isolated populations diverged into separate species. This is biogeography + natural selection evidence.
17 A species of fish produces 10,000 eggs per year, but only about 50 survive to adulthood. This is an example of — BIO.7b
  • A
    overproduction of offspring leading to competition
  • B
    genetic engineering in fish populations
  • C
    Lamarck's theory of acquired characteristics
  • D
    punctuated equilibrium
Overproduction = species produce far more offspring than can survive. This creates competition, where individuals with favorable traits are more likely to survive and reproduce. This is a key step in natural selection.
18 The human appendix and whale hip bones are examples of — BIO.7d
  • A
    analogous structures
  • B
    vestigial structures
  • C
    homologous structures
  • D
    adaptive structures
Vestigial structures are reduced body parts with no current function. The human appendix and whale hip bones are leftovers from ancestors who used them — evidence that these organisms evolved from ancestors with different body plans.
19 Compared to mammals, sea turtles produce hundreds of eggs at once but provide no parental care. This reproductive strategy — BIO.7b
  • A
    guarantees all offspring will survive
  • B
    means sea turtles are more evolved than mammals
  • C
    eliminates the need for genetic variation
  • D
    increases the chance that some offspring will survive to reproduce
Producing many offspring with little parental care is a reproductive strategy: even though most die, the sheer number means SOME will survive. This contrasts with mammals (few offspring, high parental care). Both strategies maximize population survival in different environments.
20 (2003-36) Which type of reproduction provides the most protection to developing offspring of land-dwelling animals? BIO.7b

Internal development (viviparity, like mammals) provides the MOST protection — the developing offspring is inside the mother's body, protected from predators, temperature changes, and dehydration.
