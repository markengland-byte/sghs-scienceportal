BIO.2a — Water Chemistry
Polarity, Hydrogen Bonding & Properties of Water
🎯
Water Questions Appear on Most Biology SOLs
At least one question on nearly every Biology SOL tests water's unique properties. The key is knowing WHY water behaves the way it does — it all starts with polarity and hydrogen bonds. Master those two concepts and every water question becomes easy.
Why Water Is Special: Polarity & Hydrogen Bonding

Water (H₂O) is a polar molecule — the oxygen end has a slight negative charge and the hydrogen ends have a slight positive charge. This unequal charge distribution means water molecules are attracted to each other: the positive hydrogen of one molecule is drawn to the negative oxygen of another. This attraction is called a hydrogen bond.

Hydrogen bonds are individually weak, but millions of them acting together give water extraordinary properties that make life possible.

Water Properties & Biological Importance
PropertyDefinitionBiological Example
CohesionWater molecules attract OTHER water molecules (stick together)Water moves up a plant stem in one continuous column — each molecule pulls the next
AdhesionWater molecules attract OTHER substances (stick to surfaces)Water climbs up the inside of a narrow glass tube (capillary action) or clings to plant xylem walls
Surface TensionThe "skin" on water's surface caused by cohesionInsects walk on water; a needle floats if placed gently on the surface
Universal SolventWater dissolves more substances than any other liquidBlood plasma (mostly water) carries dissolved nutrients, gases, and wastes throughout the body
High Specific HeatWater absorbs/releases large amounts of heat with little temperature changeOceans moderate coastal climates; your body temperature stays stable despite hot/cold environments
Thermal RegulationEvaporation of water cools surfaces (evaporative cooling)Sweating cools the body; transpiration cools plant leaves
Ice FloatsSolid water (ice) is LESS dense than liquid waterIce forms on top of lakes, insulating the water below and preventing lakes from freezing solid — aquatic life survives winter
🧠
Memory: Cohesion vs. Adhesion
Cohesion = water sticking to water (co = together, same). Adhesion = water sticking to a different surface. On the SOL, if the question is about water climbing up a tube or moving through a plant — BOTH cohesion and adhesion are involved. If it's about surface tension — that's cohesion only.
⚠️
Common Confusion: Specific Heat vs. Evaporative Cooling
High specific heat = water resists temperature change (the ocean doesn't boil or freeze easily). Evaporative cooling = when water evaporates off your skin, it takes heat WITH it, cooling you down. They are related but different concepts — the SOL can test either one.
Quick Check — Water Properties

  • D

Water is called the "universal solvent" because it dissolves more substances than any other liquid. Option B is wrong — ice is LESS dense than liquid water (that's why ice floats). Option D is wrong — water has a HIGH heat capacity (high specific heat).

Because ice is less dense than liquid water, it floats on top. This ice layer insulates the liquid water below, preventing the entire lake from freezing solid. Fish and other aquatic organisms survive winter in the liquid water beneath the ice.

Perspiration (sweating) uses evaporative cooling. When sweat evaporates from your skin, it takes heat energy with it, lowering your body temperature. This is a direct application of water's thermal regulation properties.

BIO.2b — Macromolecules
The Four Categories of Biological Macromolecules
🎯
A Macromolecule Comparison Question Appears on Every SOL
You WILL be asked to identify a macromolecule by its function, monomer, or example. The most common format: "Which macromolecule does [function]?" If you can fill in the table below from memory, you can answer every one of these questions.
Monomers & Polymers

A monomer is a single small building block. A polymer is a large molecule made by linking many monomers together. Think of monomers as individual LEGO bricks and polymers as the completed structure.

The Big Four — Macromolecule Comparison Table
MacromoleculeMonomerPrimary FunctionExamples
CarbohydratesMonosaccharides (simple sugars: glucose, fructose)Quick energy; structural support (cellulose in plants)Glucose, starch, glycogen, cellulose, chitin
LipidsGlycerol + fatty acids (NOT a true polymer)Long-term energy storage; insulation; cell membrane structureFats, oils, waxes, phospholipids, steroids (cholesterol)
ProteinsAmino acids (20 types)Most diverse: enzymes, structural support, transport, defense, movementEnzymes, hemoglobin, antibodies, collagen, keratin, actin
Nucleic AcidsNucleotidesStore and transmit genetic informationDNA, RNA, ATP
🔑
Key Distinction: Quick Energy vs. Long-Term Energy
Carbohydrates = quick/immediate energy (your body burns glucose first). Lipids = long-term energy storage (your body stores fat for later). On the SOL, if the question says "immediate energy" or "quick energy" → carbohydrates. If it says "long-term storage" or "insulation" → lipids. This is the #1 macromolecule confusion.
🧠
Memory: Elements in Each Macromolecule
All four macromolecules contain C, H, and O (carbon, hydrogen, oxygen). Proteins also contain nitrogen (N) and sometimes sulfur. Nucleic acids also contain nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P). SOL trick: "Which macromolecule contains nitrogen?" → Proteins AND nucleic acids.
⚠️
Common Mistake: Lipids Are NOT True Polymers
Lipids are made of glycerol and fatty acids but are NOT built by linking identical monomers in a chain. The SOL rarely tests this directly, but knowing it helps you understand why lipids are the "odd one out" in the macromolecule group. Carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids are all true polymers.
Quick Check — Macromolecules
? Both lipids and carbohydrates are important in animal cells because both —
  • A
    provide insulation
  • B
    store energy
  • C
    form cell walls
  • D
    contain nitrogen
Both lipids AND carbohydrates store energy — that's what they have in common. Carbohydrates provide quick energy; lipids provide long-term energy. Only lipids provide insulation (not carbohydrates). Neither contains nitrogen. Cell walls are made of cellulose (a carbohydrate), but lipids don't form cell walls.
? Proteins are formed from monomers (subunits) called —
  • A
    nucleic acids
  • B
    fatty acids
  • C
    nucleotides
  • D
    amino acids
Proteins are polymers of amino acids (there are 20 different types). Fatty acids are part of lipids. Nucleotides are monomers of nucleic acids. Don't confuse nucleic acids (a macromolecule category) with nucleotides (the monomer).
? Which of the following macromolecules are a prominent part of animal tissues that function in insulation, helping animals conserve heat?
  • A
    Carbohydrates
  • B
    Lipids
  • C
    Proteins
  • D
    Nucleic acids
Lipids (fats) form insulating layers beneath the skin of animals, helping conserve body heat. This is one of lipids' key functions alongside long-term energy storage and forming cell membranes.

BIO.2c — Enzymes
Enzymes as Catalysts & the Lock-and-Key Model
🎯
Enzyme Graph Interpretation Is a Common SOL Question
You will likely see a graph showing enzyme activity vs. temperature or pH. The curve rises to a peak (the optimum) and then drops sharply. The drop = denaturation. Know what the peak means and why the enzyme stops working at extremes.
What Is an Enzyme?

An enzyme is a special protein that acts as a biological catalyst — it speeds up chemical reactions in living cells without being used up or permanently changed. Without enzymes, most biochemical reactions would happen too slowly to sustain life.

The Lock-and-Key Model

Each enzyme has a uniquely shaped region called the active site. Only a specific molecule — the substrate — fits into the active site, just like a key fits into a specific lock. This explains why enzymes are specific: amylase breaks down starch but cannot break down proteins, because the shapes don't match.

TermDefinitionAnalogy
EnzymeProtein catalyst; speeds up reactionsThe lock
Active SiteRegion on the enzyme where substrate bindsThe keyhole
SubstrateThe molecule the enzyme acts onThe key
ProductWhat is produced after the reactionThe door opening
🔑
Key Concept: Enzymes Are NOT Used Up
After an enzyme catalyzes a reaction, it is released unchanged and ready to bind another substrate molecule. One enzyme molecule can catalyze thousands of reactions. The enzyme is not consumed and not changed by the reaction — the SOL tests this.
Factors That Affect Enzyme Activity
FactorEffect on EnzymeSOL Detail
TemperatureActivity increases as temperature rises to the optimum, then drops sharply above itMost human enzymes: optimum ~37°C. Above ~45°C, the enzyme denatures (unfolds). Below optimum, reactions slow but the enzyme is not destroyed.
pHEach enzyme has an optimum pH; activity drops if pH is too high or too lowPepsin (stomach): optimum pH ~2 (acidic). Trypsin (intestine): optimum pH ~8 (basic). If pH shifts too far, the enzyme denatures.
Substrate ConcentrationMore substrate = faster reactions, up to a point (all active sites occupied = plateau)When every enzyme is "busy," adding more substrate doesn't help — you've reached the maximum rate.
⚠️
Common Mistake: "The enzyme is destroyed"
When an enzyme denatures, its shape changes so the substrate no longer fits the active site. The enzyme is NOT destroyed or broken apart — it has lost its shape and therefore its function. Think of it like bending a key — the metal is still there, but it won't fit the lock anymore.
🧠
Memory: "Denature = De-shape"
Denaturation = the enzyme loses its natural shape. No shape = no function. Causes: extreme heat, extreme pH, or extreme salt concentration. The enzyme's chemical composition hasn't changed, but its 3D structure has.
Quick Check — Enzymes
? What is the function of enzymes in biological systems?
  • A
    Enzymes act as products to create new chemical reactions.
  • B
    Enzymes act as substrates when the necessary proteins are unavailable.
  • C
    Enzymes bond with substrates to create the new reaction products.
  • D
    Enzymes act as catalysts to drive chemical reactions forward.
Enzymes are biological catalysts — they speed up chemical reactions without being used up. They are NOT products and they are NOT substrates. Option C is tricky because enzymes do bind substrates, but they don't permanently bond — they release the products and go back to work.
? Peroxidase is an enzyme that breaks down hydrogen peroxide in cells. It accomplishes this because of its structure. What part of the enzyme is involved in catalytic activity?
  • A
    Quaternary structure
  • B
    Active site
  • C
    Binding pocket
  • D
    Pleated sheet
The active site is the specific region on the enzyme where the substrate (hydrogen peroxide) binds and the reaction occurs. This is the part of the enzyme directly involved in catalytic activity — it's where the "lock-and-key" interaction happens.
? This graph shows that —
  • A
    more enzymes are present at a higher pH
  • B
    pepsin is less sensitive to pH than trypsin
  • C
    pepsin is less effective at low pH than trypsin
  • D
    pH affects the activity rate of enzymes
The graph shows different enzymes (pepsin and trypsin) have different optimum pH levels, and their activity rates change as pH changes. The key conclusion: pH affects enzyme activity. Pepsin works best at low pH (~2, in the stomach) while trypsin works best at higher pH (~8, in the intestine). The correct answer describes what the graph SHOWS, not what it implies.

BIO.2d — Protein Synthesis
From DNA to Protein: Transcription & Translation
🎯
You WILL Be Asked to Use a Codon Chart on the SOL
The SOL provides a codon chart. You must be able to: (1) read an mRNA sequence, (2) break it into three-letter codons, and (3) use the chart to find the matching amino acid. Practice this skill until it's automatic.
The Central Dogma: DNA → RNA → Protein

Protein synthesis is a two-step process that uses the instructions stored in DNA to build proteins. The information flows in one direction: DNA → mRNA → Protein. This is called the central dogma of molecular biology.

Step 1: Transcription (DNA → mRNA)
DetailWhat Happens
WhereIn the nucleus
WhatOne strand of DNA is used as a template to build a complementary mRNA strand
Key EnzymeRNA polymerase
Base PairingA→U, T→A, C→G, G→C (note: RNA uses Uracil instead of Thymine)
ResultA single-stranded mRNA molecule that carries the genetic message out of the nucleus
🧠
Memory: "Transcription = Transcribe = Copy"
Think of transcription as copying a message. A secretary transcribes notes — copies them from one format to another. DNA is "transcribed" (copied) into mRNA. Both are nucleic acids — it's just a format change. Transcription happens in the nucleus because that's where the DNA is.
Step 2: Translation (mRNA → Protein)
DetailWhat Happens
WhereAt the ribosome (in the cytoplasm)
WhatThe mRNA sequence is "read" in groups of three bases (codons). Each codon specifies one amino acid.
tRNA RoletRNA molecules carry amino acids to the ribosome. Each tRNA has an anticodon that matches an mRNA codon.
ResultA chain of amino acids linked by peptide bonds = a protein
🧠
Memory: "Translation = Translate = Change Language"
Translation means converting from one language to another. The mRNA message (written in nucleotide language) is translated into protein (written in amino acid language). Translation happens at the ribosome.
How to Use a Codon Chart

A codon chart shows which amino acid each three-letter mRNA codon codes for. To use it:

StepWhat to DoExample: AUG
1Find the first letter of the codon on the LEFT side of the chartFirst letter = A
2Find the second letter across the TOP of the chartSecond letter = U
3Find the third letter on the RIGHT side of the chartThird letter = G
4Read the amino acid at the intersectionAUG = Methionine (Met) — also the START codon
🔑
Key Facts: Start and Stop Codons
AUG = START codon (also codes for methionine — every protein begins with this). UAA, UAG, UGA = STOP codons (they don't code for any amino acid — they signal the ribosome to stop building the protein). The SOL may ask you to identify when translation begins (AUG) or ends (stop codon).
⚠️
Common Mistake: Reading DNA Instead of mRNA
The codon chart uses mRNA codons, not DNA. If the question gives you a DNA sequence, you must first transcribe it to mRNA (A→U, T→A, C→G, G→C) before using the chart. If the question gives you mRNA directly, go straight to the chart. Read the question carefully!
Quick Check — Protein Synthesis
? Amino acids link together by peptide bonds to form proteins. In which cellular organelle would this process occur?
  • A
    Mitochondrion
  • B
    Ribosome
  • C
    Lysosome
  • D
    Golgi body
Translation (the linking of amino acids into proteins) occurs at the ribosome. The ribosome reads the mRNA and assembles amino acids into a protein chain. Mitochondria make ATP (energy). Lysosomes break things down. The Golgi body packages and ships proteins after they're made.
? During protein synthesis, where does transcription take place?
  • A
    At the ribosome in the cytoplasm
  • B
    In the mitochondria
  • C
    In the nucleus
  • D
    On the cell membrane
Transcription (DNA → mRNA) occurs in the nucleus, where the DNA is located. The mRNA then leaves the nucleus and travels to a ribosome in the cytoplasm for translation (mRNA → protein). Remember: transcription = nucleus, translation = ribosome.
? A segment of mRNA reads: AUG-GCA-UUU. How many amino acids will be coded for by this sequence?
  • A
    3 amino acids
  • B
    6 amino acids
  • C
    9 amino acids
  • D
    1 amino acid
Each codon (group of 3 mRNA bases) codes for one amino acid. AUG = 1 amino acid, GCA = 1 amino acid, UUU = 1 amino acid. Three codons = three amino acids. Always count the number of three-letter groups to find the number of amino acids.

BIO.2e — Photosynthesis & Respiration
Energy Capture & Energy Release in Living Systems
🎯
Know the Equations and WHERE Each Process Occurs
The SOL will ask you to identify reactants, products, AND the organelle where each process takes place. You do NOT need to know the complex multistep processes (Calvin cycle, Krebs cycle, etc.) — just the overall equations and locations.
Photosynthesis — Building Food from Sunlight
6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
carbon dioxide + water + sunlight → glucose + oxygen
DetailPhotosynthesis
LocationChloroplasts (in plant cells and some protists)
Reactants (inputs)CO₂ (carbon dioxide) + H₂O (water) + light energy
Products (outputs)C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose) + O₂ (oxygen)
EnergyLight energy is CONVERTED into chemical energy (stored in glucose)
Key PigmentChlorophyll (absorbs sunlight)
Who Does It?Autotrophs: plants, algae, some bacteria
Cellular Respiration — Releasing Energy from Food
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP (energy)
glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water + energy
DetailCellular Respiration
LocationMitochondria (in ALL eukaryotic cells — plants AND animals)
Reactants (inputs)C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose) + O₂ (oxygen)
Products (outputs)CO₂ (carbon dioxide) + H₂O (water) + ATP (energy)
EnergyChemical energy in glucose is RELEASED and captured in ATP
Who Does It?ALL living organisms (both plants and animals)
Side-by-Side Comparison
FeaturePhotosynthesisCellular Respiration
LocationChloroplastsMitochondria
ReactantsCO₂ + H₂O + lightGlucose + O₂
ProductsGlucose + O₂CO₂ + H₂O + ATP
EnergyAbsorbs/stores energyReleases/uses energy
OrganismsAutotrophs only (plants, algae)ALL organisms
When?Requires lightOccurs all the time (24/7)
🔑
Key Concept: The Two Processes Are a Cycle
The products of photosynthesis (glucose + O₂) are the reactants of cellular respiration. The products of cellular respiration (CO₂ + H₂O) are the reactants of photosynthesis. They are complementary processes — each one produces what the other needs.
🔑
ATP — The Energy Currency
ATP (adenosine triphosphate) is the molecule cells use to power all life processes. Cellular respiration produces ATP from glucose. When a cell needs energy, it breaks an ATP bond to release it. Think of ATP as cash — glucose is like a paycheck that must be "cashed in" (through respiration) to be spent.
⚠️
Common Mistake: "Only animals do cellular respiration"
WRONG. Plants do BOTH photosynthesis AND cellular respiration. Plants make glucose through photosynthesis, then break it down through respiration to get ATP for their own energy needs. Animals only do respiration (they eat food to get glucose). This is a common SOL trap.
🧠
Memory: "Chloroplast = Kitchen, Mitochondria = Power Plant"
The chloroplast is the kitchen — it MAKES the food (glucose) from raw ingredients (CO₂, water, light). The mitochondrion is the power plant — it BURNS the food (glucose) to generate energy (ATP). Plants have BOTH organelles. Animals only have mitochondria.
💡
VDOE Note
Students are NOT expected to know the complex multistep processes of photosynthesis (light reactions, Calvin cycle) or respiration (glycolysis, Krebs cycle, electron transport chain). Focus on the overall equations, locations, and the relationship between the two processes.
Quick Check — Photosynthesis & Respiration

  • A

  • B

  • C

  • D

Photosynthesis produces glucose and oxygen, which are the reactants for cellular respiration. Cellular respiration produces CO₂ and water, which are the reactants for photosynthesis. The output of one process is the input of the other — forming a cycle. Option A is wrong because plants do BOTH processes.
? The energy-transferring molecule used by cells is —
  • A
    CO₂
  • B
    RNA
  • C
    ATP
  • D
    DNA
ATP (adenosine triphosphate) is the cell's main energy-carrying molecule. Cellular respiration converts the chemical energy in glucose into ATP, which cells then use to power life processes. CO₂ is a waste product. RNA and DNA store genetic information, not energy for cell processes.
? Which of these processes is carried out in the same way in both plants and animals?
  • A
    Excretion of metabolic waste
  • B
    Circulation of body fluids
  • C
    Asexual reproduction
  • D
    Cellular respiration
Cellular respiration occurs in the mitochondria of ALL eukaryotic cells — both plant and animal. It is fundamentally the same process in both: glucose + oxygen → CO₂ + water + ATP. The other processes listed differ significantly between plants and animals.

A substance that speeds up a chemical reaction without being used up. Enzymes are biological catalysts. Lowers activation energy.
Denaturation
tap to flip
When an enzyme loses its 3D shape due to extreme pH or temperature. The active site no longer fits the substrate — enzyme stops working.
ATP
tap to flip
Adenosine triphosphate — the cell's energy currency. Stores and releases energy when a phosphate group is removed (ATP → ADP + P).
Photosynthesis
tap to flip
Process that converts light energy → chemical energy (glucose). Occurs in chloroplasts. 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂.
Cellular Respiration
tap to flip
Process that breaks down glucose to release ATP energy. Occurs in mitochondria. C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP.
Chloroplast
tap to flip
Organelle in plant/algae cells where photosynthesis occurs. Contains chlorophyll which absorbs light energy.
Mitochondria
tap to flip
Organelle where cellular respiration occurs. "Powerhouse of the cell." Found in BOTH plant and animal cells. Converts glucose → ATP.
Transcription
tap to flip
First step of protein synthesis: DNA → mRNA. Occurs in the NUCLEUS. DNA code is copied into a messenger RNA strand.
Translation
tap to flip
Second step of protein synthesis: mRNA → protein. Occurs at RIBOSOMES. mRNA codons are read and amino acids are assembled into a protein.
Codon
tap to flip
A sequence of THREE mRNA bases that codes for one amino acid. 64 possible codons. AUG = start codon; UAA, UAG, UGA = stop codons.

Top 5 SOL Tips for BIO.2
  1. Water's properties come from hydrogen bonds between polar molecules.
  2. Lipids = insulation + long-term energy. Carbs = quick energy.
  3. Enzymes are NOT consumed; they are reused. Shape = function.
  4. Photosynthesis & respiration are opposite reactions — products of one are reactants of the other.
  5. Protein synthesis: DNA → mRNA (transcription, nucleus) → protein (translation, ribosome).

    has a high specific heat

Cohesion between water molecules creates surface tension — a thin "skin" on the water's surface that supports the weight of small insects. This is a direct result of hydrogen bonds between water molecules.
2(2004-28) The unique properties of water enable life to exist on Earth. Which of these is a property of pure water?BIO.2a
  • A
    It dissolves many substances.
  • B
    Its solid form is more dense than its liquid.
  • C
    It is slightly more acidic than air.
  • D
    It has a low heat absorption capacity.
Water is the "universal solvent" — its polarity allows it to dissolve more substances than any other liquid. Ice is LESS dense than liquid water (it floats), water has HIGH heat capacity, and pure water is neutral (pH 7), not acidic.

  • B

Ice floats because solid water is less dense than liquid water. This creates an insulating layer on top, preventing the entire lake from freezing solid and protecting aquatic life below.

Water makes up approximately 70% of most cells. It is the most abundant molecule in living organisms, serving as a solvent for biochemical reactions and maintaining cell structure.

6(2004-43) Proteins are formed from monomers (subunits) called —BIO.2b
  • A
    nucleic acids
  • B
    fatty acids
  • C
    nucleotides

Proteins are polymers made of amino acid monomers linked by peptide bonds. Fatty acids are parts of lipids, nucleotides are monomers of nucleic acids (DNA/RNA), and nucleic acids are macromolecules, not monomers.
7(2005-19) Both lipids and carbohydrates are important in animal cells because both —BIO.2b
  • A
    provide insulation
  • B
    store energy
  • C
    form cell walls
  • D
    contain nitrogen
Both lipids AND carbohydrates store energy. Carbs provide quick/short-term energy; lipids provide long-term energy storage. Insulation is lipids only. Cell walls are carbohydrates only (cellulose in plants). Nitrogen is found in proteins and nucleic acids.
8(2005-38) Amino acids link together by peptide bonds to form proteins. In which cellular organelle would this process occur?BIO.2b
  • A
    Mitochondrion
  • B
    Ribosome
  • C

Translation (protein assembly) occurs at the ribosome. mRNA codons are read and amino acids are linked by peptide bonds to build a protein. Mitochondria make ATP, lysosomes digest waste, and the Golgi packages/ships proteins.
9(2006-18) What is the function of enzymes in biological systems?BIO.2c

Enzymes are biological catalysts — they speed up chemical reactions by lowering activation energy. They are NOT consumed or changed in the reaction. They bind to substrates at the active site but are released unchanged.

17(2001-26) The graph shows how dissolved O₂ and CO₂ levels changed in a pond over a 24-hour period. What caused the decrease in O₂ concentration during the night?BIO.2e
  • A
    Increased evaporation
  • B
    Decreased photosynthesis
  • C
    Increased respiration
  • D
    Decreased temperatures
At night there is no sunlight, so photosynthesis stops (decreases). Photosynthesis produces O₂, so when it stops, O₂ levels drop. Meanwhile, respiration continues 24/7, consuming O₂. The PRIMARY cause is decreased photosynthesis — no light = no O₂ production.
18(2001-33) The processes of photosynthesis and respiration can be thought of as a cycle because —BIO.2e

  • B
    both give off oxygen to be used by animals
  • C
    the products of one are used as the raw materials of the other
  • D
    they both have the same purpose
Photosynthesis produces glucose + O₂, which are the reactants for cellular respiration. Respiration produces CO₂ + H₂O, which are the reactants for photosynthesis. They are reverse reactions forming a cycle. Note: Plants do BOTH processes — A is wrong.
19(2005-9) The energy in the food produced by autotrophs or taken into the bodies of heterotrophs must be changed into a form that cells can use. The energy-transferring molecule used by cells is —BIO.2e

  • B
    RNA
  • C
    ATP
  • D
    DNA
ATP (adenosine triphosphate) is the universal energy currency of cells. Cellular respiration breaks down glucose and converts the energy into ATP, which cells then use for all their activities. CO₂ is a waste product; RNA and DNA store genetic information.
