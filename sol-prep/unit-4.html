BIO.4a/b — Viruses
Virus Structure, Host Dependence & Replication Cycles
🎯
The #1 BIO.4 Concept: Viruses Are NOT Alive
The SOL will test whether you understand that viruses are considered nonliving. They are NOT cells. They cannot reproduce on their own. They have no metabolism. They are essentially a piece of genetic material wrapped in a protein coat. Every BIO.4 question starts from this fundamental distinction.
What Is a Virus?

A virus is a tiny infectious agent that can only replicate inside the living cells of an organism. Viruses are NOT cells — they lack a cell membrane, cytoplasm, ribosomes, and all the other organelles that cells have. A virus is essentially a package of genetic instructions wrapped in a protein shell.

The VDOE framework states: "Viruses are small, infectious agents that replicate only inside the living cells of organisms." This is the key: viruses depend on a host cell for ALL metabolic processes. Without a host, a virus is inert — it cannot grow, reproduce, respond to stimuli, or carry out any life process.

Virus Structure

All viruses share two basic structural components:

ComponentDescription
Nucleic acid coreThe genetic material — either DNA or RNA (never both). Can be single-stranded or double-stranded. Contains instructions for making new viruses.
Capsid (protein coat)A protective shell made of protein that surrounds the nucleic acid. Gives the virus its shape. Some viruses also have an outer envelope (lipid membrane stolen from the host cell).

That's it. No ribosomes, no mitochondria, no cytoplasm, no cell membrane of their own. This extreme simplicity is WHY viruses are considered nonliving — they lack the machinery to carry out life processes independently.

Why Viruses Are Considered Nonliving
Characteristic of LifeLiving CellsViruses
Made of cellsYesNo — viruses are NOT cells
Reproduce independentlyYesNo — must use a host cell
MetabolismYesNo — no metabolic activity outside host
Grow and developYesNo
Respond to stimuliYesNo
Contain genetic materialYes (DNA)Yes (DNA or RNA) — this is the ONE thing they share
🧠
Memory: What Viruses Share with Living Things
The ONLY characteristic viruses share with living cells is that they contain nucleic acids (DNA or RNA). On the SOL, if a question asks "which characteristic do viruses share with living cells?" the answer is always about genetic material/nucleic acids. Never ribosomes, never nucleus, never metabolism.
How Viruses Infect Host Cells

Viruses replicate through two main cycles:

The Lytic Cycle (Immediate Destruction):

StepWhat Happens
1. AttachmentVirus attaches to specific receptor proteins on the host cell surface
2. EntryVirus injects its nucleic acid into the host cell (or the whole virus enters)
3. ReplicationViral DNA/RNA takes over the host's machinery (ribosomes, enzymes) to make copies of viral parts
4. AssemblyNew virus particles are assembled inside the host cell
5. Lysis (burst)The host cell BURSTS open (lyses), releasing hundreds of new viruses that infect other cells

The Lysogenic Cycle (Delayed Destruction):

In the lysogenic cycle, the virus does NOT immediately destroy the host. Instead, the viral DNA integrates into the host cell's DNA and becomes a provirus. Every time the host cell divides, it copies the viral DNA along with its own. The virus "hides" in the host's genome, sometimes for years. Eventually, a trigger (stress, UV light, chemical signal) causes the provirus to exit the host DNA and switch to the lytic cycle, destroying the cell.

⚠️
SOL Key: Lysogenic Always Leads to Lytic
The lysogenic cycle is a "waiting phase" that ALWAYS eventually ends with the lytic cycle. The virus delays its reproduction but never abandons it. If the SOL asks "what happens at the end of the lysogenic cycle?" the answer is: the virus enters the lytic cycle and destroys the host cell.
Viruses and Genetic Variation

Because viruses insert their DNA/RNA into host cells, they can introduce new genetic material into the host organism. This is one way that viruses drive evolution and introduce genetic variation into populations. Viruses that infect bacteria (called bacteriophages) can transfer genes between different bacterial cells, sometimes giving the bacteria new capabilities like antibiotic resistance.

BIO.4b/c — Bacteria
Bacterial Structure, Reproduction & Classification
🎯
Bacteria Are Living Organisms — Viruses Are Not
Unlike viruses, bacteria ARE living cells. They are prokaryotic (no membrane-bound nucleus), but they have all the characteristics of life: they grow, reproduce, respond to stimuli, metabolize nutrients, and maintain homeostasis. The SOL will ask you to compare bacteria and viruses — knowing this fundamental difference is critical.
Bacterial Cell Structure

Bacteria are prokaryotic cells — they lack a membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. Despite being simple, bacterial cells have all the machinery needed for independent life:

StructureFunction
Cell wallRigid outer layer providing shape and protection. Classified as Gram-positive (thick cell wall, stains purple) or Gram-negative (thin cell wall + outer membrane, stains pink). This distinction affects which antibiotics work.
Cell membraneControls what enters and exits the cell (selectively permeable)
CytoplasmGel-like fluid where chemical reactions occur
Circular DNASingle circular chromosome in the nucleoid region (NOT enclosed in a nucleus)
PlasmidSmall circular DNA separate from the chromosome; often carries antibiotic resistance genes; can be transferred between bacteria
RibosomesMake proteins (smaller than eukaryotic ribosomes, but same function)
Flagellum (some)Whip-like tail for movement
Pilus/Pili (some)Hair-like projections for attachment or DNA transfer during conjugation

How Bacteria Reproduce

Binary fission is the primary method of bacterial reproduction. It is asexual — one cell splits into two genetically identical cells. Here are the steps:

StepWhat Happens
1. DNA ReplicationThe single circular chromosome is copied, producing two identical copies
2. Cell GrowthThe cell elongates, and the two DNA copies move to opposite ends
3. Septum FormationA new cell wall (septum) begins to form across the middle of the cell
4. DivisionThe septum completes, dividing the cell into two identical daughter cells

The process is fast: some bacteria can divide every 20 minutes under ideal conditions, meaning one bacterium can become millions in just a few hours. This rapid reproduction is why bacterial infections can spread so quickly — and why antibiotic resistance can evolve in a short time.

Conjugation is a rare form of sexual reproduction in bacteria. Here's how it works: two bacterial cells come close together and one extends a pilus (a tube-like protein bridge) to connect to the other cell. Through this pilus, the donor bacterium transfers a copy of a plasmid — a small, circular piece of DNA separate from the main chromosome. Plasmids often carry genes for antibiotic resistance, toxin production, or the ability to metabolize unusual substances.

What makes conjugation important: it allows bacteria to share genes even between different species. This is one of the main ways antibiotic resistance spreads so rapidly through bacterial populations. A resistant bacterium can pass its resistance plasmid to a completely different bacterial species through conjugation.

The VDOE framework notes that "sexual reproduction in bacteria is rare" and students are NOT expected to know other mechanisms beyond binary fission and conjugation.

Classifying Bacteria by Energy Source

The VDOE framework requires you to classify bacteria by how they obtain energy:

TypeEnergy SourceExample
HeterotrophConsumes other organisms or organic materialMost disease-causing bacteria; decomposers
PhotoautotrophUses sunlight to make food (like plants)Cyanobacteria (blue-green "algae")
ChemoautotrophUses chemical energy from inorganic compounds (not sunlight, not food)Bacteria near deep-sea hydrothermal vents
🧠
Memory: Auto = Self, Hetero = Other
Autotrophs make their OWN food (auto = self). Heterotrophs eat OTHER organisms (hetero = different/other). Photo = light. Chemo = chemicals. So a photoautotroph uses light to make its own food, and a chemoautotroph uses chemicals to make its own food.

BIO.4d/e — Disease & Germ Theory
Effects on Health, Germ Theory & the Nature of Science
🎯
Germ Theory = Nature of Science Example
The VDOE framework specifically uses germ theory as an example of how scientific understanding changes over time. You need to know the basic story: people used to believe in spontaneous generation → Pasteur disproved it → Koch proved specific germs cause specific diseases → modern health practices emerged. The SOL tests this as a Nature of Science concept, not just a history question.
How Bacteria and Viruses Affect Living Systems

Both bacteria and viruses can cause disease, but they also play essential roles in ecosystems:

Harmful effects:

  • Bacterial diseases: strep throat, tuberculosis, food poisoning (Salmonella, E. coli), tetanus, pneumonia
  • Viral diseases: common cold, influenza, HIV/AIDS, COVID-19, chickenpox, rabies, Ebola
  • Viruses have killed millions — the influenza pandemic of 1918 killed 50+ million people worldwide
  • Plants can also be infected by viruses, damaging crops and food supplies

Beneficial effects (especially bacteria):

  • Nutrient cycling: Bacteria decompose dead organisms, returning nutrients (nitrogen, carbon, phosphorus) to the soil
  • Nitrogen fixation: Certain bacteria convert atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) into forms plants can use
  • Digestion: Gut bacteria help humans digest food and produce vitamins
  • Food production: Bacteria are used to make yogurt, cheese, sauerkraut, and vinegar
  • Bioremediation: Bacteria can break down oil spills and toxic waste
Bacteria and the Nitrogen Cycle

One of the most important roles bacteria play in ecosystems is nitrogen fixation. All living things need nitrogen to build proteins and DNA, but most organisms cannot use nitrogen gas (N₂) directly from the atmosphere. Nitrogen-fixing bacteria (like Rhizobium, found in root nodules of legume plants) convert atmospheric N₂ into ammonia (NH₃), which plants can absorb through their roots and use to build amino acids and proteins. Without these bacteria, plants couldn't grow, and the entire food chain would collapse.

Other bacteria are decomposers — they break down dead organisms and waste, releasing nitrogen, carbon, and phosphorus back into the soil and atmosphere. This recycling is essential for keeping nutrient cycles running. The VDOE framework states: "Bacteria play important roles in the global ecosystem, including a lead role in the cycling of nutrients."

Viruses and Microbial Evolution

The VDOE framework states: "Viruses are important microbial predators that influence global biochemical cycles and drive microbial evolution." Here's what that means:

Viruses as microbial predators: Bacteriophages (viruses that infect bacteria) kill an estimated 20-40% of ocean bacteria every day. When these bacteria burst open (lysis), they release their cellular contents — carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, and other nutrients — back into the water. This "viral shunt" recycles enormous amounts of nutrients that other organisms then use, making viruses a critical part of global nutrient cycles.

Viruses drive microbial evolution: When viruses infect bacteria, they sometimes accidentally pick up pieces of the host's DNA and transfer it to the next bacterium they infect. This process, called transduction, moves genes between bacteria — including genes for antibiotic resistance, toxin production, or new metabolic abilities. This viral-mediated gene transfer is a major driver of bacterial evolution and genetic diversity.

🔑
Key Concept: Bacteria and Viruses Are Not Just "Bad"
Students often think of bacteria and viruses only as disease-causing agents. But bacteria are essential for nutrient cycling, nitrogen fixation, and digestion, while viruses play critical roles in regulating microbial populations and driving evolution. The SOL expects you to understand BOTH the harmful and beneficial effects of these microorganisms.
The Germ Theory of Disease

The germ theory states that many diseases are caused by microorganisms (bacteria, viruses, fungi, and other pathogens) that invade the body. This seems obvious today, but for most of human history, people believed disease was caused by "bad air" (miasma), evil spirits, or imbalanced body fluids.

Louis Pasteur (1860s): Pasteur conducted his famous swan-neck flask experiment to disprove spontaneous generation — the idea that living organisms could arise from non-living matter. He showed that broth in a flask with a curved neck (which trapped airborne microbes) stayed clear indefinitely, while broth exposed to air quickly grew cloudy with bacteria. This proved that microorganisms come from OTHER microorganisms, not from thin air. Pasteur also developed pasteurization (heating liquids to kill harmful bacteria) and created vaccines for rabies and anthrax.

Robert Koch (1880s): Koch proved that specific bacteria cause specific diseases. He developed Koch's postulates, a set of four criteria for establishing that a particular microorganism causes a particular disease:

StepKoch's Postulate
1The microorganism must be found in all organisms with the disease, but not in healthy ones
2The microorganism must be isolated from the diseased organism and grown in pure culture
3The cultured microorganism must cause the same disease when introduced into a healthy host
4The same microorganism must be re-isolated from the newly infected host
🔑
Germ Theory Illustrates the Nature of Science
The VDOE framework explicitly connects germ theory to how science works: (1) Old ideas (spontaneous generation) were replaced when new evidence contradicted them. (2) Technology (microscopes) enabled new discoveries. (3) Multiple scientists built on each other's work (Pasteur → Koch). (4) The theory led to practical changes (sanitation, vaccines, antibiotics). This is exactly how all scientific knowledge develops — through evidence, testing, and revision.
Modern Health Practices from Germ Theory

The acceptance of germ theory revolutionized medicine and public health:

  • Sanitation: Proper sewage systems, clean water supplies, handwashing
  • Safe food handling: Pasteurization, refrigeration, cooking temperatures
  • Aseptic technique: Sterilizing surgical instruments, wearing gloves, disinfecting surfaces
  • Vaccination: Exposing the immune system to weakened/dead pathogens to build immunity
  • Quarantine: Isolating infected individuals to prevent disease spread

BIO.4e — Vaccines & Antibiotics
Prevention, Treatment & Antibiotic Resistance
🎯
Antibiotics vs. Viruses Is a Guaranteed SOL Question
The SOL WILL ask you whether a specific disease can be treated with antibiotics. The rule is simple: antibiotics kill bacteria, NOT viruses. If the disease is caused by a virus (cold, flu, HIV), antibiotics won't help. If it's caused by a bacterium (strep throat, TB), antibiotics CAN help.
Vaccines: Prevention Through Immunity

A vaccine is a preparation containing a dead, weakened, or partial form of a pathogen. When injected (or taken orally), it doesn't cause the disease, but it DOES trigger the body's immune system to build a defense against that pathogen. The immune system creates antibodies and memory cells that "remember" the pathogen. If the real pathogen invades later, the immune system can destroy it quickly before it causes illness.

Vaccines have eradicated smallpox, nearly eliminated polio, and dramatically reduced measles, mumps, rubella, tetanus, and many other deadly diseases. They are one of the most important public health tools in human history.

🔑
Vaccines Prevent; Antibiotics Cure
Vaccines = given BEFORE infection to PREVENT disease (build immunity). Antibiotics = given AFTER infection to CURE bacterial disease (kill bacteria). They serve completely different purposes. The SOL tests this distinction.
Antibiotics: Treatment for Bacterial Infections

Antibiotics are medicines that kill bacteria or stop their growth. They work by targeting structures or processes that bacteria have but human cells don't — for example, destroying the bacterial cell wall (which human cells lack) or blocking bacterial protein synthesis (using the differences between bacterial and human ribosomes).

Critical SOL fact: Antibiotics do NOT work on viruses. Why? Because viruses don't have cell walls, ribosomes, or their own metabolism — there's nothing for the antibiotic to target. A virus hides inside your own cells, and the antibiotic can't attack your cells without harming you. This is why your doctor won't prescribe antibiotics for a cold or flu.

Antibiotic Resistance: A Growing Crisis

When antibiotics are overused or misused (e.g., not finishing the full prescription, using antibiotics for viral infections, or giving antibiotics to livestock), some bacteria with random mutations that make them resistant to the antibiotic survive and reproduce. Because bacteria reproduce so rapidly (every 20 minutes), resistant populations can develop within weeks or months. Eventually, the antibiotic becomes ineffective against that bacterial strain.

This is natural selection in action: the antibiotic creates a selective pressure, resistant bacteria survive and pass on their resistance genes, and the population evolves. The VDOE framework asks students to "use evidence from scientific literature and research to support a claim on the use or misuse of vaccines or antibiotics."

⚠️
SOL Trap: WHY Resistance Develops Quickly
Students know that antibiotic resistance is bad, but they miss WHY it happens so fast. The answer is always: bacteria reproduce rapidly. Fast reproduction = fast evolution. If the SOL asks "why has antibiotic resistance developed in a relatively short time?" the answer is always about rapid bacterial reproduction.

Ecosystem Roles (BIO.4d)

Bacteria: decompose dead organisms (nutrient cycling); nitrogen fixation (N₂ → NH₃ for plants); gut bacteria aid digestion; food production (yogurt, cheese). Viruses: bacteriophages lyse bacteria, releasing nutrients (viral shunt); transfer genes between bacteria (transduction), driving microbial evolution.

Germ Theory & Prevention (BIO.4e)

Pasteur: disproved spontaneous generation (swan-neck flask). Koch: specific germs cause specific diseases (Koch's postulates: isolate, culture, infect, re-isolate). Vaccines = prevent disease (dead/weakened pathogen → immune response). Antibiotics = cure BACTERIAL disease only. Antibiotic resistance = overuse → resistant bacteria survive → rapid reproduction spreads resistance.

1 A virus consists of — BIO.4a

    nucleic acid surrounded by a protein coat (capsid)
  • B
    a cell membrane, cytoplasm, and ribosomes

    a cell wall, circular DNA, and flagellum

    mitochondria, nucleus, and Golgi apparatus
A virus = nucleic acid core (DNA or RNA) + capsid (protein coat). That's it. Viruses lack all cellular structures like membranes, ribosomes, and organelles. Choices B, C, and D describe parts of actual cells.
2 Why are viruses considered nonliving? BIO.4a
  • A
    They cannot carry out life processes without a host cell
  • B
    They are too small to be seen
  • C
    They do not contain any genetic material
  • D
    They only infect plants
Viruses cannot reproduce, metabolize, or carry out ANY life process without hijacking a host cell. They DO contain genetic material (DNA or RNA), so C is wrong. Viruses infect all types of organisms, not just plants.
3 (2002-41) Which characteristic do viruses possess in common with living cells? BIO.4a
  • A
    They contain a nucleus and organelles
  • B
    They contain nucleic acids such as DNA or RNA
  • C
    They make their own food
  • D
    They reproduce independently
The ONLY characteristic viruses share with living cells is containing nucleic acids (DNA or RNA). Viruses lack nucleus, organelles, metabolism, and cannot reproduce independently.
4 A virus that integrates its DNA into a host cell's chromosome and remains dormant is in the — BIO.4b
  • A
    lytic cycle
  • B
    binary fission phase
  • C
    lysogenic cycle
  • D
    conjugation phase
In the lysogenic cycle, viral DNA integrates into the host chromosome as a provirus and lies dormant, being copied with each cell division until a trigger activates the lytic cycle.
5 The lytic cycle ends when — BIO.4b
  • A
    viral DNA integrates into host chromosome
  • B
    the host cell begins normal division
  • C
    the virus exits without damaging the host
  • D
    the host cell bursts, releasing new viruses
Lytic = lysis = burst. The host cell is destroyed, releasing hundreds of new virus particles that infect neighboring cells. Integration into the chromosome is the lysogenic cycle.
6 Bacteria transfer genetic material between cells through a pilus during a process called — BIO.4b
  • A
    binary fission
  • B
    conjugation
  • C
    transduction
  • D
    lysis
Conjugation is the rare sexual reproduction in bacteria where DNA (often a plasmid) is transferred through a pilus connecting two cells. Binary fission is asexual splitting. Transduction is virus-mediated gene transfer.
7 Which is TRUE about bacteria but NOT viruses? BIO.4c
  • A
    They contain genetic material
  • B
    They can cause disease
  • C
    They can mutate
  • D
    They can reproduce independently
Bacteria reproduce independently through binary fission. Viruses CANNOT reproduce without a host cell. Both contain genetic material, cause disease, and can mutate.
8 Deep-sea bacteria near hydrothermal vents use chemical energy from inorganic compounds to make food. These bacteria are classified as — BIO.4c
  • A
    heterotrophs
  • B
    photoautotrophs
  • C
    chemoautotrophs
  • D
    decomposers
Chemoautotrophs use chemical energy from inorganic compounds (not sunlight, not organic food) to make their own food. No sunlight reaches the deep ocean floor.

  • A
    there are many different types of bacteria
  • B
    bacteria are very small
  • C
    travelers carry bacteria around the world
  • D
    bacteria reproduce rapidly
Bacteria can divide every 20 minutes. Rapid reproduction means resistant individuals quickly become the dominant population through natural selection.
10 Cyanobacteria are classified as photoautotrophs because they — BIO.4c
  • A
    consume other organisms for energy
  • B
    use chemicals from deep-sea vents
  • C
    use sunlight to make their own food
  • D
    decompose dead organisms for nutrients
Photo = light, auto = self. Photoautotrophs use sunlight to make their own food, just like plants. Cyanobacteria were the first organisms to produce oxygen through photosynthesis.
11 Nitrogen-fixing bacteria are essential for plant growth because they — BIO.4d
  • A

  • B
    convert atmospheric nitrogen into forms plants can absorb
  • C
    kill harmful fungi in the soil
  • D
    break down plant cell walls for easier nutrient absorption
Plants need nitrogen to build proteins and DNA but cannot use N₂ gas from the atmosphere. Nitrogen-fixing bacteria (like Rhizobium in legume root nodules) convert N₂ into ammonia (NH₃), which plants CAN absorb.
12 Bacteriophages (viruses that infect bacteria) contribute to ecosystems by — BIO.4d
  • A

  • B

  • C

  • D
    preventing all bacterial diseases in animals
When bacteriophages lyse (burst) bacteria, the bacterial cell contents (carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus) are released back into the environment. This "viral shunt" recycles enormous amounts of nutrients in ocean ecosystems.

  • A

  • B
    direct contact with infected person
  • C
    contaminated water
  • D

Smallpox spreads through direct person-to-person contact. People who moved from cities but brought infected family members still got sick, proving direct contact was the transmission method.
14 Viruses can drive microbial evolution because they — BIO.4d
  • A
    reproduce faster than bacteria
  • B
    are larger than most microorganisms
  • C
    can transfer genes between different bacteria through transduction
  • D
    produce antibiotics that kill competing bacteria
Through transduction, viruses accidentally pick up host DNA and transfer it to the next bacterium they infect. This moves genes (including antibiotic resistance) between bacteria, driving microbial evolution and genetic diversity.

  • B

  • C

  • D

Vaccines contain dead or weakened pathogens that trigger an immune response and create antibodies + memory cells, providing protection against future exposure without causing the actual disease.

  • A

  • D

Koch developed a 4-step process (isolate, culture, infect, re-isolate) to prove that a specific microorganism causes a specific disease. This was foundational to modern microbiology.
17 A doctor prescribes antibiotics for a patient with strep throat but NOT for a patient with the flu. This is because antibiotics — BIO.4e

    kill viruses but have no effect on bacteria

  • D

Strep throat is caused by bacteria (Streptococcus), so antibiotics work. The flu is caused by a virus (influenza), so antibiotics are useless. Antibiotics target bacterial structures that viruses don't have.

  • B
    numbers of alternatives to streptomycin
  • C
    artificial production of streptomycin
  • D
    early detection of bacterial infections
Overuse of streptomycin kills susceptible bacteria but leaves resistant ones alive to reproduce. More use = more selective pressure = more resistance. This is natural selection in action.

  • A

  • B
    consuming all dead organisms before they decompose
  • C

  • D
    preventing all diseases in plants and animals
Bacteria are key decomposers that break down dead organisms and return nutrients (nitrogen, carbon, phosphorus) to the soil and atmosphere, keeping nutrient cycles running.

  • C

  • D

Independent reproduction is a key characteristic of life. Bacteria can reproduce on their own = living. Viruses cannot = nonliving. Bacteria are actually LARGER than viruses, contain DNA, and CAN cause disease.
