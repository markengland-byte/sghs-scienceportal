Genome
tap
An organism's complete set of DNA instructions. In eukaryotes, the genome is packaged into chromosomes inside the nucleus. The human genome contains ~3 billion base pairs of DNA across 46 chromosomes.
Chromatin
tap
The loose, uncondensed form of DNA wrapped around histone proteins during interphase. DNA + histones = nucleosomes = chromatin. When the cell prepares to divide, chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes.
Sister Chromatids
tap
Two identical copies of a single chromosome, joined at the centromere after DNA replication. Formed during the S phase. Each chromatid is an exact copy of the other. They separate during anaphase of mitosis (or anaphase II of meiosis).
Centromere
tap
The region of the chromosome where sister chromatids are most tightly held together by cohesin proteins. The kinetochore — the protein structure that attaches to spindle fibers — assembles at the centromere.
Diploid (2n)
tap
A cell with two complete sets of chromosomes — one set from each parent. Human somatic (body) cells are diploid: 2n = 46 chromosomes (23 pairs). The "2n" notation means two copies of each chromosome number.
Haploid (n)
tap
A cell with one complete set of chromosomes. Human gametes (eggs and sperm) are haploid: n = 23 chromosomes. Produced by meiosis. When two haploid gametes fuse at fertilization, the resulting zygote is diploid (2n = 46).
Homologous Chromosomes
tap
Pairs of chromosomes with the same genes in the same locations, but carrying alleles (versions) from different parents. Humans have 23 pairs of homologs. One came from mom, one from dad. They are similar but NOT identical copies.
Kinetochore
tap
Protein complex assembled at the centromere of each sister chromatid. The attachment point for spindle microtubules during mitosis and meiosis. Kinetochore fibers pull chromatids to opposite poles during anaphase.

From DNA to Chromosomes

A cell's DNA contains all the instructions needed to build and run a living organism. According to OpenStax, "if the DNA from all 46 chromosomes in a human cell nucleus were laid out end-to-end, it would measure approximately two meters; however, its diameter would be only 2 nm." To fit this enormous molecule into a nucleus only ~10 micrometers across, DNA is packaged with histone proteins into a structure called chromatin.

Chromatin is organized into nucleosomes — beads of 8 histone proteins with DNA wrapped around them. During interphase, chromatin is loosely organized so genes can be expressed. When a cell prepares to divide, chromatin is progressively compacted into the dense, visible structures we call chromosomes. This compaction is essential so chromosomes can be pulled apart without tangling.

Fig. 1.1 — DNA packaging levels from double helix to condensed chromosome. The centromere joins sister chromatids.
Diploid vs. Haploid

Human somatic cells (body cells — skin, muscle, liver, etc.) are diploid (2n = 46). They carry 23 pairs of chromosomes — one chromosome of each pair came from your mother, the other from your father. These pairs are called homologous chromosomes (homologs). They have the same genes in the same order but may have different alleles.

Human gametes (eggs and sperm) are haploid (n = 23). They carry only one chromosome from each homologous pair. When an egg (n=23) is fertilized by a sperm (n=23), the resulting zygote is diploid (2n=46), restoring the normal chromosome number. This is why meiosis must reduce chromosome number by half — so fertilization doesn't double it every generation.

🧠
Sister Chromatids vs. Homologous Chromosomes — Don't Mix These Up
Sister chromatids: identical copies of the SAME chromosome, joined at the centromere after S-phase. They are produced by DNA replication and are genetically identical. Homologous chromosomes: two different chromosomes that carry the same genes but may have different alleles. One came from mom, one from dad. These are NOT identical.

The Cell Cycle & Interphase
The cell cycle is the ordered series of events a cell goes through from one division to the next. Interphase is NOT a resting phase — it's when the most important preparation happens.
⏱ ~30 min
📖 OpenStax Biology 2e, 10.2
🎯 Lesson Objectives

  • Describe what happens in G1, S, and G2 phases of interphase.
  • Explain why interphase is NOT a resting phase.
  • Define G0 phase and identify which cells enter it.
  • State that DNA replication occurs during S phase.
Key Vocabulary
Click Every Card to Flip It
Cell Cycle
tap
The ordered sequence of events a cell goes through from one division to the next. Two major phases: Interphase (growth + DNA replication) and the Mitotic Phase (M phase — division). Tightly regulated to prevent errors.
Interphase
tap
The long preparatory phase between cell divisions. Divided into G1, S, and G2. The cell grows, replicates its DNA, and prepares for division. NOT a resting phase — the cell is metabolically very active. Makes up ~90% of the total cell cycle.
G1 Phase
tap
"First Gap" phase. Cell grows in size, synthesizes proteins, produces organelles, and carries out normal cell functions. The cell "decides" whether to commit to division at the G1 checkpoint. In a 24-hour cell cycle, G1 lasts ~9 hours.
S Phase
tap
"Synthesis" phase. DNA replication occurs — each chromosome is copied so that after S phase, the cell has two complete copies of its genome (as sister chromatids). Centrosomes are also duplicated. Lasts ~10 hours in a 24-hour cycle.
G2 Phase
tap
"Second Gap" phase. Cell continues to grow and prepares specifically for division: synthesizes proteins for spindle formation, checks DNA for errors (G2 checkpoint), replenishes energy stores. Lasts ~4.5 hours. After G2, the cell enters mitosis.
G0 Phase
tap
A non-dividing state where cells exit the cell cycle. Not resting — just not preparing to divide. Some cells enter G0 temporarily and can return to G1 (like liver cells after injury). Others enter G0 permanently: mature neurons and cardiac muscle cells never divide again.

The Cell Cycle: Two Major Phases

According to OpenStax, "the cell cycle is an ordered series of events involving cell growth and cell division that produces two new daughter cells." It has two major phases: a long interphase and a shorter mitotic phase (M phase).

Fig. 2.1 — The cell cycle. Interphase (G1+S+G2) makes up ~90% of the cycle. The M phase (mitosis + cytokinesis) is brief by comparison.

⚠️
Common Misconception: Interphase is NOT "resting"
Students often think interphase is when the cell is doing nothing — just waiting. In reality, interphase is when the cell does MOST of its work: growing, synthesizing proteins, replicating 2 meters of DNA, and preparing all the machinery for division. A cell in G0 is truly non-dividing, but it is still metabolically active.

Lesson 2 Quiz — The Cell Cycle & Interphase

5 questions · includes graph interpretation

1In which phase of the cell cycle does DNA replication occur?
  • A
    G1 phase
  • B
    S (Synthesis) phase
  • C
    G2 phase
  • D
    M (Mitotic) phase
DNA replication occurs during the S (Synthesis) phase of interphase. The "S" stands for synthesis — specifically, DNA synthesis (replication). After S phase, the cell has twice as much DNA as before, with each chromosome now consisting of two identical sister chromatids. This must happen BEFORE mitosis so that each daughter cell gets a complete copy of the genome.
2A researcher treats cells with a drug that blocks protein synthesis. Which phase of the cell cycle would be MOST directly impaired?
  • A
    S phase only — because DNA replication requires protein enzymes
  • B
    M phase only — because spindle fibers are made of protein
  • C
    G0 phase — because non-dividing cells make the most protein
  • D
    G1 phase most directly — because G1 is dedicated to cell growth and protein synthesis in preparation for division
G1 phase is primarily devoted to cell growth and protein synthesis. The cell must synthesize proteins needed for: DNA replication enzymes (for S phase), spindle proteins (for M phase), and organelle production. Blocking protein synthesis in G1 would prevent the cell from passing the G1 checkpoint and entering S phase. While M phase also needs proteins, G1 is the primary growth/synthesis phase.
3📊 Based on the cell cycle graph, which phase takes up the most time in a 24-hour cell cycle?
  • A
    M phase — because mitosis is the most complex process
  • B
    G2 phase — the cell needs the most time to prepare
  • C
    S phase — DNA replication of 46 chromosomes takes approximately 10 hours
  • D
    G1 phase — it takes 12 hours of growth
In a typical 24-hour mammalian cell cycle, S phase (~10 hours) takes the longest. This makes biological sense — replicating ~6 billion base pairs of DNA accurately across 46 chromosomes takes significant time. M phase is surprisingly brief (~30 minutes) — once chromosomes are lined up, separation is rapid. This is why most cells viewed under a microscope appear to be in interphase — they spend very little time in division.
4Mature nerve cells (neurons) in the adult brain never divide. Which statement best describes their cell cycle status?
  • A
    They have permanently exited the cell cycle into G0 phase — they remain metabolically active but will never divide again
  • B
    They are permanently stuck in S phase, preventing DNA replication
  • C
    They are in an extended G1 phase and could re-enter the cycle if stimulated
  • D
    They have lost all their chromosomes and therefore cannot divide
Neurons (and cardiac muscle cells) permanently exit the cell cycle into G0 phase after differentiation. They are still alive and metabolically active — they conduct impulses, form synapses, etc. — they just never divide again. This has clinical implications: brain injuries and heart attacks cause permanent cell loss because these cells cannot regenerate through division. This is why neurodegenerative diseases are so devastating.
5At the start of S phase, a diploid human cell has 46 chromosomes. How many chromosomes does it have at the END of S phase?
  • A
    92 chromosomes — because each chromosome was duplicated
  • B

This is a critical concept and a frequent exam trap. After S phase, the chromosome COUNT is still 46, but each chromosome now consists of two sister chromatids joined at the centromere. We don't say "92 chromosomes" — we say "46 chromosomes, each made of 2 sister chromatids." The number only changes during anaphase when chromatids separate (briefly going to 92 "chromosomes") and then returning to 46 in each daughter cell.

Phases of Mitosis
Mitosis is the precise choreography of chromosome separation. Five stages — each with a specific job — ensure every daughter cell gets an exact copy of the genome.
⏱ ~35 min
📖 OpenStax Biology 2e, 10.2
🎯 Lesson Objectives

  • Use the mnemonic PMAT (or PPMAT) to recall the order.
  • Identify the key event that occurs in each phase.
  • Describe the role of the mitotic spindle and kinetochores.
  • Explain what the metaphase plate is and why it matters.
Key Vocabulary
Click Every Card to Flip It
Prophase
tap
1st phase of mitosis. Chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes. The mitotic spindle begins forming from centrosomes. Nucleolus disappears. Nuclear envelope begins to break down. Chromosomes consist of two sister chromatids at this point.
Prometaphase
tap
2nd phase. Nuclear envelope fully breaks down. Spindle fibers (microtubules) attach to kinetochores on each sister chromatid. Chromosomes begin moving toward the cell center. Spindle fibers from OPPOSITE poles attach to the two kinetochores of each chromosome.
Metaphase
tap
3rd phase. ALL chromosomes align at the metaphase plate — the imaginary equatorial line midway between the two poles. Kinetochores are under equal tension from both poles. The metaphase checkpoint ensures all chromosomes are properly attached before proceeding.
Anaphase
tap
4th phase. Cohesin proteins holding sister chromatids together are cleaved. Sister chromatids SEPARATE and are pulled to OPPOSITE POLES by shortening spindle fibers. Each pole now has a complete set of chromosomes. Cell elongates. This is the shortest phase — rapid and precise.
Telophase
tap
5th phase. Chromosomes arrive at opposite poles and begin to decondense back into chromatin. A new nuclear envelope forms around each set of chromosomes. Nucleoli reappear. The spindle disassembles. Two nuclei are now present in one cell — division of the nucleus (karyokinesis) is complete.
Mitotic Spindle
tap
The protein structure (made of microtubules) that separates chromosomes during mitosis. Formed from centrosomes at opposite poles. Two types of spindle fibers: kinetochore fibers (attach to chromosomes) and polar fibers (push poles apart). Disrupted by drugs like colchicine and taxol.

Prophase → Prometaphase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase. Try: "Please Pass Me A Thumbtack" or simply remember PMAT (combining prophase and prometaphase). Every stage builds on the previous one.

Metaphase is defined by chromosome alignment at the metaphase plate — the imaginary plane equidistant from both poles. This alignment is critical: it ensures each pole has equal access to one chromatid from every chromosome. The metaphase checkpoint (spindle assembly checkpoint) verifies that all kinetochores are properly attached to spindle fibers from opposite poles before the cell proceeds to anaphase.
2Sister chromatids are held together at the centromere by a protein called cohesin. In which phase are these cohesin proteins cleaved, causing chromatid separation?

  • B
    Prophase — chromosomes must separate before they condense
  • C
    Telophase — chromatids separate as the nuclear envelope reforms
  • D
    Prometaphase — spindle attachment pulls them apart
Anaphase is when cohesin is cleaved by the enzyme separase, releasing sister chromatids. Once released, spindle fibers from opposite poles shorten, pulling each chromatid to its respective pole. This is the most dramatic moment of mitosis — the chromosomes visibly split and move apart. Anaphase is also the briefest phase. After anaphase, each pole has a complete set of chromosomes (46 in humans).
3The nuclear envelope breaks down during mitosis and reforms at the end. When does it BREAK DOWN and when does it REFORM?
  • A
    Breaks down in metaphase; reforms in anaphase
  • B
    Breaks down in anaphase; reforms in telophase
  • C
    Breaks down in prophase; reforms in prophase of the next cycle
  • D
    Breaks down in prometaphase (completing what started in prophase); reforms in telophase around each set of chromosomes
The nuclear envelope begins to break down in late prophase and is fully gone by prometaphase (spindle fibers need access to chromosomes). It reforms in telophase around each set of chromosomes at the poles, creating two new nuclei. This is a key sequence to know: nuclear breakdown → spindle can access chromosomes → chromosomes separate → nuclear envelope reforms around each set → karyokinesis complete.

In a root tip slide, the proportion of cells in each phase reflects how long the cell spends there. Seeing many cells in prophase means cells spend more time there. Anaphase is typically the rarest to observe because it's so rapid — chromosomes separate quickly once cohesin is cleaved.

Prophase: scattered condensing chromosomes. Prometaphase: chromosomes moving, no clear line. Anaphase: V-shapes moving to poles.

Cytokinesis
Mitosis divides the nucleus. Cytokinesis divides the cytoplasm. The process looks different in animal and plant cells — but the result is the same: two genetically identical daughter cells.
⏱ ~20 min
📖 OpenStax Biology 2e, 10.2
🎯 Lesson Objectives
  • Define cytokinesis and explain when it occurs relative to mitosis.
  • Describe the cleavage furrow mechanism in animal cells.
  • Describe the cell plate mechanism in plant cells.
  • Explain why these mechanisms differ between plant and animal cells.
  • State the final products of mitosis + cytokinesis.
Core Content — OpenStax Biology 2e, 10.2
Splitting the Cell in Two

Mitosis (nuclear division) is followed by cytokinesis — the division of the cytoplasm into two daughter cells. According to OpenStax, "the final stage of the cell division process...is called cytokinesis — the physical separation of the cytoplasmic components into the two daughter cells." The mechanism differs significantly between animal and plant cells due to the presence of a rigid cell wall in plants.

🐾 Animal Cell Cytokinesis

  • An actin ring (cleavage furrow) forms at the cell equator
  • The ring contracts like a drawstring
  • Cell membrane pinches inward progressively
  • Cell "pinches off" into two cells
  • Starts during late anaphase/telophase
  • Result: two cells sharing no wall material

🌿 Plant Cell Cytokinesis

  • Golgi vesicles containing cell wall material gather at the metaphase plate
  • Vesicles fuse to form a cell plate (phragmoplast)
  • Cell plate grows outward from center to edges
  • Eventually fuses with existing cell wall
  • Creates a new shared cell wall between daughters
  • Cannot use a cleavage furrow — rigid cell wall prevents pinching
🔑
Why the Difference?
Plant cells have a rigid cellulose cell wall. A cleavage furrow — which works by contracting and pinching the membrane — would not work against a rigid wall. Instead, plant cells build a NEW wall from the inside out using vesicles from the Golgi apparatus. The cell plate grows from the center outward until it reaches the existing cell walls, completing division.
💡
Final Products of Mitosis + Cytokinesis
One diploid parent cell (2n) → two genetically identical diploid daughter cells (2n). Each daughter cell is an exact genetic copy of the parent. This is the definition of a mitotic division — clonal reproduction at the cellular level.

Proto-oncogene
tap
A normal gene that promotes cell growth and division in a regulated way. Examples: genes for growth factor receptors, signaling proteins, cyclins. When mutated or overexpressed, a proto-oncogene becomes an oncogene — a cancer-causing version that drives uncontrolled division.
Oncogene
tap
A mutated proto-oncogene that causes cells to divide uncontrollably. Acts like a stuck accelerator — permanently "on" regardless of normal signals. Examples: mutated RAS (found in many cancers), HER2 (breast cancer). Dominant mutation — one mutated copy is enough.

tap
A gene that normally slows or stops cell division. Acts as a brake on the cell cycle. Examples: p53, Rb (retinoblastoma protein), BRCA1/2. Both copies must be mutated for the braking function to be lost (recessive). Loss of tumor suppressors removes the cell cycle brakes.

tap
The spread of cancer cells from the primary tumor to other parts of the body via blood or lymph. Cancer cells that metastasize must escape the tumor, enter circulation, survive, and colonize new tissues.

Cancer: When Control Fails

According to OpenStax, "cancer is the result of unchecked cell division caused by a breakdown of the mechanisms that regulate the cell cycle. The loss of control begins with a change in the DNA sequence of a gene that codes for one of the regulatory molecules. Faulty instructions lead to a protein that does not function as it should."

  • Mutated proto-oncogenes
  • Dominant — one mutated copy enough
  • Drive cell division even without signals
  • Examples: mutated RAS, HER2 amplification
  • Analogy: gas pedal stuck to floor

  • Normally slow or stop division

A single mutation rarely causes cancer. The Problem Meiosis Solves

Imagine if fertilization simply combined two full sets of chromosomes every generation: 46 + 46 = 92 in the first generation, 92 + 92 = 184 in the second, and so on. Within a few generations, organisms would have thousands of chromosomes and the genome would be unmanageable. Meiosis solves this problem by cutting the chromosome number in half before fertilization.

According to OpenStax, sexual reproduction "involves the production by parents of two haploid cells and the fusion of two haploid cells to form a single, genetically recombined diploid cell — a genetically unique organism." Meiosis produces these haploid gametes (eggs and sperm in animals). When two haploid gametes fuse at fertilization, the diploid number is restored.

Fig. 7.1 — The sexual life cycle. Meiosis halves the chromosome number; fertilization restores it. This cycle repeats every generation.
🔑
Meiosis Also Generates Genetic Diversity
Beyond just halving chromosome number, meiosis creates genetically unique gametes through: (1) crossing over — exchange of DNA between homologous chromosomes; (2) independent assortment — random orientation of homolog pairs. With 23 chromosome pairs, independent assortment alone can produce 2²³ = ~8 million chromosome combinations. Add crossing over and the number is essentially infinite.

Synapsis
tap
The precise pairing of homologous chromosomes during Prophase I of meiosis. Homologs align gene-by-gene along their entire length, held together by the synaptonemal complex. This pairing enables crossing over to occur at corresponding locations.
Tetrad (Bivalent)
tap
The structure formed when two homologous chromosomes (each consisting of two sister chromatids) pair up during Prophase I. A tetrad = 4 chromatids total (2 from each homolog). Also called a bivalent. Crossing over occurs within the tetrad.
Crossing Over
tap
The exchange of homologous segments between non-sister chromatids of a tetrad during Prophase I. Creates new combinations of alleles on a single chromosome (recombinant chromosomes). The point of exchange is called a chiasma (plural: chiasmata). A major source of genetic variation.
Independent Assortment
tap
The random, independent orientation of each homologous pair at the metaphase I plate. Each pair can orient with either homolog facing either pole — the orientation of one pair has no effect on any other pair. Generates 2²³ ≈ 8 million chromosome combinations in humans.
Anaphase I
tap
The phase of Meiosis I when HOMOLOGOUS CHROMOSOMES (not sister chromatids) separate and move to opposite poles. This is the actual reduction division — chromosome number goes from 2n to n. Sister chromatids remain joined at their centromeres and will separate in Meiosis II.

Meiosis II — The Equational Division

Meiosis II is similar to mitosis — it separates sister chromatids. However, unlike mitosis, it occurs in haploid cells (the products of Meiosis I), so the daughter cells are also haploid. According to OpenStax, "the second division of meiosis is similar to a mitotic division, except that the daughter cells do not contain identical genomes because of crossover and chromosome recombination."

PhaseKey Events
Prophase IINuclear envelope breaks down; spindle forms in each haploid cell; chromosomes (still with 2 chromatids) condense
Metaphase IIChromosomes align individually at metaphase plate (like mitosis metaphase); one chromosome from each pair in each cell
Anaphase IISister chromatids SEPARATE and are pulled to opposite poles — like mitotic anaphase; centromeric cohesin is finally cleaved
Telophase IINuclear envelopes reform; chromosomes decondense; cytokinesis produces 4 haploid cells total
Three Sources of Genetic Variation in Meiosis

1. Crossing Over (Prophase I)

  • Exchange of DNA between non-sister chromatids
  • Creates recombinant chromosomes
  • New allele combinations not in either parent
  • Occurs at chiasmata during synapsis
  • At least 1 crossover per chromosome pair

2. Independent Assortment (Metaphase I)

  • Random orientation of homolog pairs
  • Each pair faces either pole independently
  • 2²³ ≈ 8 million combinations in humans
  • Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment
  • Acts on whole chromosomes
🔑
3. Random Fertilization
Even after meiosis generates ~8 million different egg types AND ~8 million different sperm types, fertilization randomly combines one of each. The number of genetically distinct zygotes possible: 8 million × 8 million = 64 trillion possible combinations from just independent assortment — before adding crossing over. This is why every human (except identical twins) is genetically unique.
