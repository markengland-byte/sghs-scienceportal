Introduction to Cell Signaling
From single-celled yeast to 37 trillion human cells — all living things use chemical signals to coordinate behavior. Cell signaling is one of the most conserved systems in all of biology.

Signal Molecule (Ligand)
tap
A molecule released by a signaling cell that binds to a receptor on a target cell. Ligands are often called first messengers. Examples: epinephrine, insulin, estrogen, acetylcholine.
Receptor
tap
A protein (on the cell surface or inside the cell) that specifically binds a signal molecule and triggers a cellular response. Binding causes a conformational change that initiates signal transduction.
Reception
tap
Stage 1 of cell signaling. A signal molecule binds to a specific receptor protein, causing a conformational (shape) change. This is the detection step. Highly specific — like a lock and key.
Transduction
tap
Stage 2 of cell signaling. The signal is converted and relayed through a series of molecular changes (signal transduction cascade). Often amplifies the signal enormously — one ligand can activate thousands of downstream molecules.
Response
tap
Stage 3 of cell signaling. The cellular activity that results from the transduced signal. Can be: gene expression changes, enzyme activation/inhibition, ion channel opening, cytoskeletal changes, or apoptosis.
Target Cell
tap
A cell that has a specific receptor for a given signal molecule. Not all cells respond to all signals — only cells with the matching receptor are target cells. The same signal can produce different responses in different target cell types.
Core Content — OpenStax Biology 2e, 9.1
Why Cells Need to Communicate

In a multicellular organism, cells must coordinate their activities to maintain homeostasis, respond to environmental changes, direct development, and regulate growth. Without cell communication, your immune cells couldn't find pathogens, your muscles couldn't contract on demand, and cell division would be uncontrolled — leading to cancer.

🔬
Evidence from Yeast: Cell Signaling Is Ancient
According to OpenStax, "yeast cells and human cells use similar methods of cell communication." Yeast use chemical signals called mating factors to coordinate reproduction — and the molecular machinery is remarkably similar to human signaling pathways. This conservation across 1.5 billion years of evolution indicates that cell signaling is fundamental to all eukaryotic life.

Specificity: Why Not Every Cell Responds to Every Signal
A signal molecule only affects cells that have the correct receptor. Epinephrine (adrenaline) circulates throughout the blood, but it only triggers muscle cells to break down glycogen because muscle cells have the right receptor. Liver cells have different receptors and respond differently to the same epinephrine signal. Same ligand → different receptors → different responses.

Lesson 1 Quiz — Introduction to Cell Signaling

5 questions · includes data interpretation

1Which sequence correctly describes the three stages of cell signaling?
  • A
    Response → Transduction → Reception
  • B
    Reception → Transduction → Response — ligand binds receptor, signal is relayed and amplified, then a cellular change occurs
  • C
    Transduction → Reception → Response
  • D
    Reception → Response → Transduction
The universal order is Reception → Transduction → Response. Reception: the ligand binds its specific receptor (detection). Transduction: the signal is converted and amplified through a cascade of molecular changes. Response: the cell carries out the appropriate activity — changing gene expression, activating enzymes, opening channels, or undergoing apoptosis. This three-stage framework applies to virtually all cell signaling events.
2Epinephrine circulates throughout the blood but only affects cells with the correct receptor. This demonstrates:
  • A
    Amplification — epinephrine activates more molecules at each step
  • B
    The transduction stage — epinephrine initiates a cascade immediately
  • C
    Specificity — only cells with the matching receptor for epinephrine are target cells and will respond
  • D
    Feedback inhibition — epinephrine shuts off cells that don't need it
Specificity in cell signaling means only cells with the correct receptor respond to a signal. Even though epinephrine is in the blood reaching every cell, only cells expressing the epinephrine receptor become "target cells." Other cells simply ignore the signal. This is how your body can send a global signal (blood-borne hormone) but get a specific, targeted response (only relevant cells react).
3Yeast mating factors use signaling pathways similar to those in human cells. What does this suggest?
  • A
    Yeast and humans evolved independently and developed identical systems by chance
  • B
    Yeast signaling is simpler and serves as a model only for very primitive organisms
  • C
    Humans evolved from yeast relatively recently
  • D
    Cell signaling pathways are evolutionarily ancient and conserved — the core mechanisms appeared in a common ancestor of all eukaryotes over 1.5 billion years ago
The similarity between yeast and human signaling pathways is evidence that these mechanisms evolved in a common eukaryotic ancestor and were so critical to survival that they were preserved through billions of years of evolution. This evolutionary conservation — the same molecular components performing the same functions across wildly different organisms — is one of biology's most powerful concepts. It's why yeast is used as a model organism to study human disease.
4📊 Based on the amplification graph, why is signal amplification essential to cell signaling?
  • A
    A single ligand molecule would be insufficient to produce a detectable cellular response without amplification — the cascade multiplies the signal so a tiny stimulus produces a large effect
  • B
    Amplification slows the signal down so the cell has time to respond appropriately
  • C
    Amplification ensures that all cells respond to the signal, not just target cells
  • D
    Amplification reduces the number of receptors needed on the cell surface
Signal amplification is essential because signal molecules often exist in tiny concentrations (nanomolar range). Without amplification, one ligand binding one receptor would produce one activated downstream molecule — far too little to change cell behavior. With a cascade, each activated protein can activate many more downstream molecules: 1 ligand → 1 receptor → 10 G proteins → 100 adenylyl cyclase → 1,000 cAMP → 10,000 kinase activations → massive response. This is why hormones work in extremely low concentrations.
5During the reception stage, what happens when a ligand binds its receptor?
  • A
    The ligand enters the cell and directly activates genes in the nucleus
  • B
    The receptor is destroyed and must be replaced before another signal can be received
  • C
    The receptor undergoes a conformational (shape) change that initiates the signal transduction cascade — the ligand itself typically does not enter the cell
  • D
    The cell immediately divides in response to the signal
Ligand binding causes a conformational change (shape change) in the receptor — this is the molecular "switch" that starts the transduction cascade. The ligand itself typically does NOT enter the cell (at least for hydrophilic signal molecules like epinephrine). The receptor acts as a transducer: extracellular signal in → intracellular change out. The shape change may expose active sites, enable protein-protein interactions, or activate enzymatic activity built into the receptor.

Gap Junctions: The Direct Contact Superhighway

Gap junctions are protein channels (connexins) that directly connect the cytoplasm of adjacent cells, allowing small molecules, ions, and electrical signals to pass freely between cells without entering the extracellular space. This is the fastest form of cell communication — no signal molecule, no receptor, no transduction needed. Gap junctions are critical in cardiac muscle (all heart cells contract as one unit), smooth muscle, and developing embryos.

🔑
Paracrine vs. Endocrine: Local vs. Global
Paracrine signals are rapidly degraded or taken up locally — they don't survive to reach distant cells. This keeps them local. Endocrine signals (hormones) are stable enough to survive the journey through blood (minutes to hours). The tradeoff: paracrine signals are faster and more precise (targeting neighbors only); endocrine signals are slower but can coordinate responses across the entire body.

Lesson 2 Quiz — Types of Cell Communication

5 questions · includes distance/speed graph

1Neurons release neurotransmitters that diffuse across the synaptic cleft to bind receptors on the next neuron. This is an example of:
  • A
    Endocrine signaling — neurotransmitters travel through blood
  • B
    Autocrine signaling — the neuron signals to itself
  • C
    Paracrine signaling — the signal travels a short local distance (the synapse) to affect neighboring cells
  • D
    Direct contact signaling via gap junctions
Paracrine signaling: the signal (neurotransmitter) travels a short local distance across the synaptic cleft (~20 nm) to affect the adjacent neuron or muscle cell. It doesn't enter the bloodstream and is rapidly broken down by enzymes in the synapse (like acetylcholinesterase degrading acetylcholine). This precise, local, rapid signaling is exactly why neurons can transmit signals at millisecond speed — the signal doesn't have to travel far.
2Cardiac muscle cells are connected by gap junctions. When one cell receives an electrical signal, all connected cells contract together. What type of signaling is this?
  • A
    Direct contact signaling — gap junctions allow electrical signals (ions) to flow directly between cells without any extracellular signal molecule
  • B
    Paracrine signaling — the signal diffuses locally to nearby cardiac cells
  • C
    Endocrine signaling — the heart uses hormones to coordinate contraction
  • D
    Autocrine signaling — each cell signals itself to contract
Gap junctions are the hallmark of direct contact signaling. Connexin proteins form channels between adjacent cells, allowing ions (like Ca²⁺ and K⁺) and small molecules to flow freely between cytoplasms. In cardiac muscle, this is essential — all heart muscle cells must contract simultaneously for effective pumping. Electrical signals (action potentials) spread instantly through gap junctions without any secreted signal molecule. This is the fastest possible cell communication.
3Insulin is produced by pancreatic beta cells and travels through the blood to stimulate glucose uptake by muscle and liver cells throughout the body. This is:
  • A
    Paracrine signaling — insulin affects nearby pancreatic cells
  • B
    Autocrine signaling — beta cells respond to their own insulin
  • C
    Direct contact signaling — insulin travels through gap junctions
  • D
    Endocrine signaling — a hormone secreted into the bloodstream affects distant target cells throughout the body
Endocrine signaling: hormones are released into the bloodstream and travel long distances to affect target cells throughout the body. Insulin from the pancreas reaches liver cells, muscle cells, and fat cells — all distant from the pancreas. Endocrine signals take longer to work (minutes) compared to paracrine (seconds) or direct contact (milliseconds), but they can coordinate responses across the entire body simultaneously. This is why a single insulin injection can regulate blood glucose globally.
4📊 Based on the distance/speed chart, which type of signaling has the LONGEST communication distance but the SLOWEST response?
  • A
    Direct contact — it has the longest range but is very slow
  • B
    Endocrine signaling — hormones travel through the entire body (meters) but take minutes to hours to produce a response
  • C
    Paracrine signaling — it covers the most distance while remaining local
  • D
    Autocrine — it is both the slowest and travels the farthest
Endocrine signaling covers the greatest distance (centimeters to meters through the circulation) but is the slowest. Hormones must be secreted, enter the blood, circulate throughout the body, and then bind receptors on distant target cells — a process taking minutes to hours. Direct contact is the fastest (milliseconds) over the shortest distance. This inverse relationship between speed and distance is fundamental to understanding why different signaling systems evolved for different physiological needs.
5Why are paracrine signals (like neurotransmitters) rapidly broken down or removed from the space around the signaling cell?
  • A
    To increase the speed of the signal — breakdown makes it faster
  • B
    To recycle the signal molecules for reuse by the same cell
  • C
    To keep the signal local and allow precise control — if paracrine signals persisted, they would spread throughout the body and lose their targeted, local effect
  • D
    Paracrine signals are not broken down — they persist indefinitely
Rapid degradation keeps paracrine signals local and precise. Neurotransmitters are broken down by enzymes (acetylcholinesterase) or actively pumped back into the neuron (reuptake) within milliseconds of release. This ensures the signal only affects the immediately adjacent cell. If neurotransmitters persisted and spread, you couldn't control individual muscle fibers or individual neural circuits — every signal would become a global event. Precision requires rapid termination.

The most important property of a signal molecule is whether it can cross the hydrophobic phospholipid bilayer of the plasma membrane. This determines where the receptor must be located and therefore the entire downstream signaling mechanism.

🧠
Mnemonic: "Steroids Go Inside; Peptides Stay Outside"
Steroid hormones (hydrophobic) cross the membrane → intracellular receptors → slow gene changes. Peptide hormones (hydrophilic) can't cross → cell-surface receptors → fast enzyme activation. This single rule predicts the entire signaling mechanism. If you know a molecule's solubility, you know where its receptor is, how fast it acts, and how long its effects last.

1Estrogen is a steroid hormone. Where would you expect to find its receptor, and why?

    On the cell surface — all hormones use surface receptors for speed
  • B

Estrogen is a steroid hormone (hydrophobic / lipid-soluble) → it can diffuse freely through the phospholipid bilayer → its receptor is intracellular (estrogen receptor, ER, in the cytoplasm or nucleus). Once estrogen binds the receptor, the complex acts as a transcription factor — directly regulating gene expression. This is why estrogen effects take hours (must synthesize new proteins) but are long-lasting (new protein levels persist after estrogen is removed).
2Epinephrine (adrenaline) is a hydrophilic amino acid-derived hormone. What does this tell you about its receptor location and the speed of its effects?
  • A
    Intracellular receptor — slow effects through gene expression changes
  • B
    No receptor needed — it enters cells directly to activate enzymes
  • C
    Intracellular receptor — fast effects because it enters cells quickly
  • D
    Cell-surface receptor — fast effects (seconds) by activating pre-existing intracellular proteins through a cascade
Epinephrine is hydrophilic → it cannot cross the membrane → its receptor (adrenergic receptor, a GPCR) is on the cell surface. Epinephrine binds → receptor activates G proteins → G proteins activate adenylyl cyclase → cAMP produced → protein kinase A activated → glycogen breakdown. This entire cascade takes seconds — no new protein synthesis needed. This is why a burst of adrenaline produces near-immediate "fight or flight" responses.
3Why do steroid hormones (like cortisol) produce longer-lasting effects than peptide hormones (like insulin)?
  • A
    Steroid hormones act through gene expression — they cause new protein synthesis; the new proteins persist long after the steroid is removed, extending the effect for hours or days
  • B
    Steroid hormones are stored in larger amounts and released more slowly
  • C
    Steroid hormones have larger receptors that stay active longer
  • D
    Peptide hormones are broken down by enzymes but steroids are not
Steroids act by changing gene expression — they alter which proteins are made. Once new proteins are synthesized (enzymes, structural proteins, transcription factors), those proteins remain in the cell long after the steroid signal is gone. Peptide hormones activate pre-existing proteins (phosphorylation, allosteric changes) — effects last only as long as the signal is present. This is why a single dose of a steroid drug (like prednisone) can have effects for days.

  • B

  • D
    Peptide hormones — fastest and longest acting
Neurotransmitters act in milliseconds (extremely fast) at the synapse but are removed within milliseconds to seconds by enzymatic degradation or reuptake. They are the fastest and most transient of all signal molecules — designed for high-frequency, precise communication between neurons. Compare this to steroid hormones: slow onset (hours) but long duration (days). Signal molecules span the entire spectrum from millisecond/millisecond to hours/days.
5Nitric oxide (NO) is a gas produced by endothelial cells that causes smooth muscle relaxation (vasodilation). Why can NO use a different receptor strategy than peptide hormones?

Nitric oxide is a small nonpolar gas that diffuses freely through membranes. Unlike most hydrophilic signal molecules, NO doesn't need a cell-surface receptor — it diffuses directly into the target cell and activates guanylyl cyclase (an intracellular enzyme that produces cGMP). This is why nitroglycerin (which releases NO) works so rapidly for angina — NO diffuses instantly into smooth muscle cells, activates guanylyl cyclase, produces cGMP → muscle relaxation → vasodilation → reduced cardiac workload.

GPCRs: The Body's Most Versatile Receptor Family

GPCRs are the largest family of membrane receptors in humans — over 1,000 genes encode different GPCRs. They mediate responses to hormones, neurotransmitters, light (rhodopsin in the eye), smell (olfactory receptors), and taste. Most common medications target GPCRs: beta-blockers (heart), antihistamines (allergies), opioids (pain), antipsychotics (brain).

🔑
RTK Dimerization: Why Two Receptors Are Better Than One
Unlike GPCRs, RTKs must dimerize (pair up) to be activated. When a growth factor binds, it bridges two RTK monomers → they pair → each phosphorylates the other on tyrosine residues (cross-phosphorylation) → the phosphorylated tyrosines act as docking sites for multiple relay proteins → one RTK can simultaneously activate multiple different signaling pathways. This "fan-out" activation explains why growth factors produce such diverse and complex cellular responses.

Lesson 4 Quiz — Cell-Surface Receptors

5 questions · includes receptor speed graph

1A GPCR (G-protein coupled receptor) is activated by a ligand. What is the IMMEDIATE molecular event after ligand binding?
  • A
    The receptor dimerizes with another GPCR and phosphorylates itself
  • B
    A ligand-gated ion channel opens and ions flow into the cell
  • C
    The receptor undergoes a conformational change that causes the associated G-protein to exchange GDP for GTP — activating the G-protein α subunit, which dissociates and activates an effector enzyme
  • D
    The receptor directly phosphorylates tyrosine residues on downstream proteins
GPCR mechanism: Ligand binds → receptor shape change → G-protein (heterotrimeric: α, β, γ) activated → α subunit exchanges GDP for GTP → α subunit dissociates from β-γ complex → activated α subunit binds and activates an effector enzyme (e.g., adenylyl cyclase) → second messenger (e.g., cAMP) produced → cascade continues. GTP hydrolysis by the α subunit itself eventually deactivates the G-protein — built-in "off switch."
2Why do receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs) require dimerization (pairing of two receptors) for activation?
  • A
    One RTK cannot span the membrane — two are needed to create a complete transmembrane pore
  • B
    The kinase active site of one RTK can only phosphorylate the other RTK's intracellular domain (cross/auto-phosphorylation) — a single RTK cannot phosphorylate its own activation sites
  • C
    Two ligands must bind simultaneously — one to each receptor — before either can be activated
  • D
    Dimerization is not required — RTKs can be activated as monomers
RTK dimerization enables cross-phosphorylation (autophosphorylation): each RTK in the dimer phosphorylates key tyrosine residues on the OTHER receptor's intracellular tail. A single RTK cannot access its own activation sites because of geometric constraints — but the paired RTK can reach them. The most common mechanism in signal transduction is phosphorylation — the addition of a phosphate group (PO₄³⁻) to a protein, catalyzed by protein kinases. Phosphorylation changes the protein's shape, which can activate or inhibit it. Protein phosphatases remove phosphate groups, reversing the activation. This on/off switching system is fast, reversible, and infinitely regulatable.

Why the Same Signal Produces Different Responses

Epinephrine (adrenaline) binds adrenergic receptors throughout the body — yet it causes different responses in different tissues: heart muscle beats faster, liver releases glucose, bronchioles dilate, digestive tract slows down, pupils dilate. The signal (epinephrine) is the same. What differs is:

  • Which G-protein subtype is coupled to that receptor (Gs, Gi, or Gq → different effectors)
  • Which downstream proteins the cell expresses (glycogen phosphorylase in liver; myosin light chain kinase in smooth muscle)
  • Which genes are accessible for expression in that particular cell type
Apoptosis: Programmed Cell Death

Apoptosis is the orderly, genetically controlled self-destruction of a cell. It is essential for development (sculpting fingers and toes by eliminating interdigital cells), immune function (killing autoreactive T cells), and tumor suppression (eliminating DNA-damaged cells). Apoptosis is executed by caspases — proteases that dismantle cellular components in an organized sequence. The cell shrinks, DNA fragments, and cell-derived vesicles are phagocytized — no inflammatory response.

Cancer requires at minimum: (1) pro-growth signals become permanently active (e.g., mutant Ras); AND (2) apoptosis signals fail (e.g., p53 tumor suppressor mutated — p53 normally triggers apoptosis in DNA-damaged cells). When both fail simultaneously, cells divide without limit AND don't die when they should → tumor formation. Most cancers require mutations in multiple signaling control points.

RAS mutations are present in ~30% of all human cancers (higher in specific types). A drug that effectively inhibits mutant RAS would be transformative — treating pancreatic (~90%), colorectal (~50%), lung (~30%), and many other cancers. However, RAS has been called "undruggable" for decades because its surface is smooth without obvious binding pockets. Recent breakthroughs: sotorasib (approved 2021) and adagrasib specifically inhibit KRAS G12C mutation — one particular RAS variant.   • C

p53 is the "guardian of the genome." When DNA is damaged: DNA damage sensors → p53 stabilized and activated → p53 (transcription factor) activates: (1) p21 → inhibits CDKs → cell cycle arrest; (2) DNA repair genes; (3) BAX/PUMA → apoptosis if damage is irreparable. This prevents DNA-damaged cells from dividing and accumulating further mutations.
