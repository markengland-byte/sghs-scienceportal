Introduction to Biology · Lesson 1
What is Life?
What separates a living cell from an equal mass of nonliving chemicals? Eight fundamental properties define all living things — from the smallest bacterium to a 2,000-year-old sequoia.
📖 OpenStax Concepts of Biology — 1.1
⏱ ~40 min
🎯 6 Quiz Questions
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Define biology and explain why it cannot be reduced to a single sentence
  • List and explain the 8 properties shared by all living things
  • Distinguish between living things and nonliving things using all 8 criteria
  • Explain why no single property alone is sufficient to define life

The Fundamental Question
What Makes Something Alive?

A match flame moves, consumes fuel, produces waste, and generates heat. A crystal grows and exhibits order. A virus contains DNA and can reproduce — but only by hijacking a host cell. Rather than seeking one defining feature, biologists have identified eight properties that, taken together, characterize all living things.

No single property is sufficient — fire has some of them, viruses have most — but all living things share all eight. Biology, the study of life, is the science dedicated to understanding these properties and everything that emerges from them.

🔑
Definition of Biology
Biology is the science that studies life. From Greek bios (life) + logos (study). Because life is incredibly diverse, biology encompasses dozens of subdisciplines — molecular biology to ecology — all united by the study of living systems.
The Eight Properties
All Living Things Share These Characteristics
1. Order

All living things exhibit a high degree of order and organization. Atoms are arranged into molecules, molecules into organelles, organelles into cells. This organization is maintained at a significant energy cost. A dead cell still contains all the same molecules as a living one — but the organization has broken down.

2. Response to the Environment

All living organisms detect and respond to stimuli. A plant turns toward light (phototropism). A bacterium swims toward a nutrient source (chemotaxis). Your pupils dilate in dim light. This responsiveness allows organisms to maintain favorable conditions and avoid harm.

3. Reproduction

All living things are capable of reproduction — producing new individuals of the same species. Reproduction may be asexual (genetically identical offspring) or sexual (combining genetic material from two parents). Individual organisms don't need to reproduce to be alive — a mule is sterile but fully alive — but the capacity exists at the species level.

4. Growth and Development

All organisms grow and develop according to specific instructions encoded in DNA. Growth means an increase in size by incorporating materials from the environment. Development involves a predictable series of changes — a fertilized egg becomes an embryo, then an infant.

5. Homeostasis (Regulation)

Living things regulate their internal environment to maintain stable conditions despite external changes. Your body holds a core temperature near 37°C whether you're in a snowstorm or a desert. Blood glucose, pH, and water balance are all tightly regulated via feedback mechanisms.

6. Energy Processing (Metabolism)

All living things use energy to perform work. Organisms get energy from sunlight (autotrophs like plants) or by consuming organic molecules (heterotrophs like animals). The sum of all energy-transforming reactions is called metabolism. Without a continuous energy supply, life's organization breaks down.

7. Heredity

Living things pass genetic information to offspring via DNA. When cells divide, DNA is copied and distributed to daughter cells. Sexual reproduction combines DNA from two parents, producing variation — the raw material for evolution.

8. Evolutionary Adaptation

Living things evolve — populations change over generations through natural selection. Every species alive today is the product of billions of years of evolutionary change. This shared evolutionary history means all life on Earth is related and uses the same genetic code.

⚠️
What About Viruses?
Viruses have DNA/RNA, reproduce, and evolve. But they cannot perform metabolism on their own, don't maintain homeostasis, and require a host to reproduce. Most biologists consider viruses acellular — not truly alive. The debate around viruses highlights why we need all eight criteria together, not just a few.

👁️ Visual Connection Question · Figure 1.1
Look at the eight properties wheel. Notice that Heredity and Evolution are listed as separate properties. How are they connected? Could evolution occur without heredity? Could heredity occur without evolution? Explain your reasoning.
Model Answer: Heredity is the mechanism — faithful copying of DNA from parent to offspring. Evolution is the result — change in the frequency of heritable traits across generations. Evolution requires heredity: without inherited variation, natural selection has nothing to act on. However, heredity can theoretically occur without evolution — if all variants survive equally in a stable population, DNA is still inherited but the population's genetic composition doesn't change. In practice, some selection always occurs, so heredity and evolution are inseparable in natural populations.

Introduction to Biology · Lesson 2
Levels of Biological Organization
Life is organized in a nested hierarchy — from atoms to the biosphere. Each level has properties that emerge only at that level and cannot be predicted from the parts alone. Understanding scale is one of biology's most powerful conceptual tools.
📖 OpenStax Concepts of Biology — 1.1
⏱ ~35 min
🎯 6 Quiz Questions
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • List the levels of biological organization from atom to biosphere in correct order
  • Define emergent properties and explain why they are biologically significant
  • Give a specific example of an emergent property at two different levels
  • Distinguish between a population, a community, and an ecosystem
  • Explain why biologists must work at the appropriate level of organization for their question
The Big Picture
Biology Studies Life at Every Scale

One of the most distinctive features of biology is the enormous range of scales it covers. A molecular biologist studies protein shapes measured in nanometers. An ecologist might study energy flow through an entire forest measured in square kilometers. All of these researchers are doing biology — they are simply asking questions at different levels of a nested hierarchy.

Understanding this hierarchy reveals a profound principle: emergent properties. At each level of organization, new properties arise that could not be predicted from the level below. Life itself is the ultimate emergent property — no single atom, molecule, or organelle is alive, yet organize them in the right way and life emerges.

🔑
Emergent Properties
An emergent property is a characteristic that arises at a particular level of organization and does not exist at lower levels. Water is wet — but individual H₂O molecules are not. The brain produces consciousness — but no single neuron is conscious. Life itself is an emergent property of nonliving chemistry arranged in the right way.
The Levels in Detail
From Smallest to Largest
Atom → Molecule → Macromolecule

The foundation of all biology is chemistry. Atoms combine to form molecules. Small molecules combine into macromolecules — the four classes of biological macromolecules (carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids) are the building blocks of all life.

Organelle → Cell

Organelles are specialized structures within eukaryotic cells — the nucleus, mitochondria, chloroplasts. Together they form the cell — the fundamental unit of life. The cell is the smallest unit that can independently perform all life functions. Some organisms consist of a single cell; others consist of trillions.

Tissue → Organ → Organ System → Organism

In multicellular organisms, similar cells form tissues. Tissues combine to form organs (the heart, stomach, brain). Organs working together form organ systems. All organ systems together constitute an organism — an individual living thing.

Population → Community → Ecosystem → Biosphere

A population is all individuals of the same species in a defined area. A community is all populations of different species living and interacting in an area. An ecosystem includes the community plus all abiotic (nonliving) factors — water, soil, climate, light. The biosphere encompasses all ecosystems on Earth.

💡
Community vs. Ecosystem
Community = biotic only (all living organisms in an area). Ecosystem = community + abiotic factors (temperature, water, soil chemistry, sunlight). A pond community is all the fish, frogs, plants, and bacteria. The pond ecosystem also includes the water chemistry, temperature, and light levels.
🔗 Everyday Connection
The City as a Biological Analogy
A city mirrors biological organization. Individual workers (cells) form departments (tissues) within companies (organs) that make up industries (organ systems) within the city (organism). The city interacts with other cities (community) within a region (ecosystem) within the country (biosphere). And just as emergent properties arise in biology — a single neuron can't think, but organize billions of them and consciousness emerges — a city has properties no individual building possesses: traffic patterns, culture, economic cycles.

Emergent properties visible here include population density, birth and death rates, carrying capacity, and predator-prey dynamics — none of which exist in a single deer. A drought's effect can only be understood with population-scale data. This illustrates why biologists must work at the appropriate organizational level for their question.

Introduction to Biology · Lesson 3
Domains of Life & Classification
With an estimated 8.7 million species on Earth, biologists need a universal system for naming and grouping living things. The modern classification system reveals not just organization, but evolutionary history.
📖 OpenStax Concepts of Biology — 1.1
⏱ ~40 min
🎯 6 Quiz Questions
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Name and distinguish the three domains of life: Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya
  • List the major kingdoms and the domain each belongs to
  • Write a scientific name correctly using binomial nomenclature
  • Recite the taxonomic hierarchy from domain to species
  • Explain why modern classification is based on evolutionary relationships, not physical similarity
  • Distinguish prokaryotes from eukaryotes at the cellular level
Why Classify?
The Need for a Universal Naming System

The common name "robin" refers to completely different birds on different continents — the American robin (Turdus migratorius) and the European robin (Erithacus rubecula) are not closely related at all. Without a universal system, scientific communication across languages would be impossible.

The solution — developed by Swedish botanist Carl Linnaeus in the 18th century — is binomial nomenclature: a two-part Latin naming system that gives each species a unique name used by scientists worldwide. Modern taxonomy has expanded this into a complete hierarchical system that reflects evolutionary relationships.

🔑
Binomial Nomenclature Rules
Every species has a two-part scientific name: (1) Genus (capitalized) + (2) species epithet (lowercase). Both are italicized in print, underlined when handwritten. Example: Homo sapiens. After first use, the genus can be abbreviated: H. sapiens.

💡
Prokaryotes vs. Eukaryotes
The most fundamental distinction in cell biology. Prokaryotes (Bacteria + Archaea) lack a membrane-bound nucleus — DNA floats freely in the cytoplasm. Eukaryotes (Domain Eukarya) have a membrane-bound nucleus and complex organelles (mitochondria, ER, etc.). All multicellular organisms are eukaryotes.

The Taxonomic Hierarchy
Domain → Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species
🧠
Mnemonic — Taxonomic Ranks
Dear King Philip Came Over For Good Soup → Domain, Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species

Humans and gray wolves share Domain through Class — meaning we share a common ancestor that was the ancestor of all mammals (~80–100 million years ago). We diverge at Order. The more ranks two organisms share, the more recently they diverged and the more genetic similarity they have.

Introduction to Biology · Lesson 4
The Scientific Method
Science is not a collection of facts — it is a way of knowing. The scientific method is the process by which scientists ask questions, test ideas, and build reliable knowledge about the natural world.
📖 OpenStax Concepts of Biology — 1.2
⏱ ~40 min
🎯 6 Quiz Questions
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Describe the steps of the scientific method and explain why the order matters
  • Distinguish between an observation, a hypothesis, and a theory
  • Explain what makes a hypothesis scientific (testable and falsifiable)
  • Describe the difference between inductive and deductive reasoning
  • Explain the role of peer review and replication in validating scientific findings
  • Distinguish between a scientific theory and a scientific law
Science as a Process
How Scientists Know What They Know

Every scientific discovery — from the structure of DNA to the effectiveness of vaccines — came through observation, questioning, testing, and revision. The scientific method is the systematic approach scientists use to investigate questions about the natural world. It is not a rigid five-step recipe; it is a flexible framework that ensures conclusions are grounded in evidence rather than authority, tradition, or wishful thinking.

Three principles separate science from other ways of knowing: hypotheses must be testable, conclusions must follow from evidence, and findings must be reproducible.

The Steps in Detail
From Observation to Conclusion
1 & 2 — Observation and Question

Science begins with careful observation of the natural world — a statement of fact about something detected through the senses or instruments. Observations generate questions. A good scientific question is specific, focused, and answerable through investigation. Questions about values or the supernatural are outside the scope of science — science only addresses questions about the natural world that can be empirically investigated.

3 — Hypothesis

A hypothesis is a testable, falsifiable explanation for an observation. Note the structure: it proposes a specific causal mechanism and must be stated in a way that could be proven wrong. Hypotheses are often expressed as if–then statements: "If plants need light to grow, then plants receiving more light will grow taller than plants receiving less." This form makes the prediction explicit and the test obvious.

🔑
Falsifiability — The Heart of Science
A hypothesis must be falsifiable — it must be possible, at least in principle, to prove it wrong. "All swans are white" is falsifiable — find one black swan and it's disproven. "There are invisible forces that cannot be detected" is not falsifiable. Science only deals with falsifiable claims. This was philosopher Karl Popper's crucial insight.
4 — Experiment

The hypothesis is tested through a controlled experiment — a procedure designed to isolate the effect of the variable being tested. A well-designed experiment has a control group (the baseline, not exposed to the treatment) and an experimental group (exposed to the treatment). All other variables are kept the same.

5 & 6 — Data and Conclusion

The experiment generates data that must be analyzed statistically to determine whether results are meaningful. The conclusion states whether the data support or fail to support the hypothesis. Scientists say a hypothesis is supported or not supported — never "proven." If rejected, a new hypothesis is formed and tested — the cycle continues.

Theory vs. Law
The Most Misunderstood Words in Science

In everyday language, "theory" means a guess. In science, a theory is a well-substantiated explanation for a broad range of observations, supported by extensive evidence from multiple independent lines of research, tested and confirmed repeatedly. The cell theory, the theory of evolution, the germ theory of disease — these are not guesses. They are among the most rigorously tested explanations in science.

A scientific law describes a pattern in nature — usually mathematically — without explaining why the pattern exists. Laws describe; theories explain.

TermEveryday MeaningScientific Meaning
TheoryA guess or unproven ideaA comprehensive, well-tested explanation supported by extensive evidence
LawA rule that must be followedA mathematical description of a natural pattern (doesn't explain why)
HypothesisAn educated guessA testable, falsifiable prediction about a specific observation
Reasoning in Science
Inductive vs. Deductive Reasoning

Inductive reasoning moves from specific observations to general conclusions — how hypotheses are generated. You observe 100 crows, all black → you conclude "all crows are black." This can be overturned by a single counterexample.

Deductive reasoning moves from general principles to specific predictions — how experiments are designed. "All mammals have hair (hypothesis) → A whale is a mammal → Therefore whales must have hair (testable prediction)." If–then statements are deductive. Good science uses both.

🩺 Everyday Connection
Evidence-Based Medicine
When your doctor prescribes a medication, the scientific method is behind that decision. Clinical trials are controlled experiments: a treatment group receives the drug; a control group receives a placebo. Neither patients nor doctors know who gets which (double-blind design). Data are analyzed, peer-reviewed, and published. Only after multiple independent trials confirm safety and effectiveness does a drug get approved. This is the scientific method applied to human health — far more reliable than anecdote or tradition.

Introduction to Biology · Lesson 5
Experimental Design: Variables & Controls
The difference between a compelling experiment and a meaningless one is entirely in the design. Variables, controls, sample size, and statistical thinking determine whether an experiment actually tests what it claims to test.
📖 OpenStax Concepts of Biology — 1.2
⏱ ~40 min
🎯 6 Quiz Questions
🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Define and distinguish independent variable, dependent variable, and controlled variables
  • Explain the purpose of a control group in an experiment
  • Distinguish between qualitative and quantitative data
  • Explain why sample size matters and what statistical significance means
  • Describe the difference between a blind study and a double-blind study
  • Explain the difference between correlation and causation
The Core Challenge
Controlling Confounding Variables

Any child can do an experiment. Designing one that actually proves something — that rules out alternative explanations and produces reliable, reproducible results — requires careful planning. The central challenge is controlling confounding variables: factors other than the one being tested that could influence results.

Imagine testing whether fertilizer makes tomato plants grow faster. You apply fertilizer to plants in a sunny location and leave unfertilized plants in a shady spot. If fertilized plants grow faster, you can't conclude fertilizer is responsible — the extra sunlight might be the real cause. Good experimental design eliminates confounding variables.

🔑
Memory Trick: IV → DV
The Independent Variable is what you deliberately change (the INPUT — you're Independent, you decide). The Dependent Variable is what you measure (the OUTPUT — it Depends on what you did). IV on x-axis; DV on y-axis.
Control Groups and Placebos

A control group does not receive the experimental treatment. It is the "what happens without intervention" baseline that makes the experimental group's results meaningful. Without a control group, you have no way of knowing whether changes you observe were caused by your treatment or would have happened anyway.

In drug trials, the control group receives a placebo — an inactive treatment that looks identical to the real drug. This controls for the placebo effect: people often feel better simply because they believe they are being treated, regardless of pharmacological activity.

💡
Blind and Double-Blind Studies
In a blind study, participants don't know whether they received treatment or placebo — eliminating the placebo effect. In a double-blind study, neither the participants nor the researchers know who received which — also eliminating researcher bias in assessment. Double-blind, placebo-controlled, randomized trials are the gold standard for medical research.
Data and Statistics
Making Results Meaningful

Qualitative data describes characteristics without numbers ("the treated plants were greener"). Useful for observations and generating hypotheses but harder to analyze objectively.

Quantitative data uses measurements ("treated plants grew 2.4 cm/week vs. 1.1 cm/week for controls"). Can be analyzed statistically to determine whether differences are real.

Statistical significance (p < 0.05) means there's less than a 5% probability the observed difference occurred by chance. Sample size matters enormously — larger samples reduce random variation and make true differences detectable.

⚠️
Correlation ≠ Causation
Just because two things tend to occur together doesn't mean one causes the other. Ice cream sales and drowning rates both increase in summer — ice cream doesn't cause drowning; hot weather causes both. Establishing causation requires controlled experiments, not just correlations.

15Ice cream sales and drowning deaths both increase in summer. A student concludes ice cream causes drowning. What is wrong with this reasoning?
  • A
    Nothing — if two variables consistently correlate, causation is established
  • B
    The sample size is too small — more summers would confirm the causal link
  • C
    This is the correlation-causation fallacy — hot weather (confounding variable) causes both; ice cream and drowning are correlated but neither causes the other
  • D
    The reasoning is flawed only because ice cream is not consumed near water
Classic correlation ≠ causation. Hot weather causes both: people eat more ice cream AND swim more (leading to more drownings). 16A graph shows plant growth rate (y-axis) vs. light intensity (x-axis). The line rises steeply from 0–4 hours, then levels off. What does the plateau indicate?

  • B

20A student proposes: "Plants grow because they want sunlight." Explain why this is NOT a scientific hypothesis, and rewrite it as a properly formed scientific hypothesis.
