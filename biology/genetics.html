Mendel & the Laws of Inheritance
Gregor Mendel cracked the code of heredity in a monastery garden using math, pea plants, and 8 years of meticulous record-keeping — before anyone knew DNA existed.

Key Vocabulary

Gene
tap
A segment of DNA on a chromosome that encodes a heritable trait. Each gene has a specific location (locus) on a chromosome. Genes come in different versions called alleles.
Allele
tap
One of two or more versions of a gene. Example: the flower color gene has a purple allele (P) and a white allele (p). You inherit one allele from each parent. Alleles can be dominant or recessive.
Dominant / Recessive
tap
Dominant alleles mask recessive alleles when both are present. Written as uppercase (P = dominant) and lowercase (p = recessive). A dominant allele only needs ONE copy to show its phenotype. A recessive allele needs TWO copies (homozygous).
Genotype / Phenotype
tap
Genotype = the actual allele combination an organism has (PP, Pp, or pp). Phenotype = the observable trait expressed (purple or white flowers). Two organisms with different genotypes (PP and Pp) can have the same phenotype (both purple).
Homozygous / Heterozygous
tap
Homozygous = two identical alleles (PP = homozygous dominant; pp = homozygous recessive). Heterozygous = two different alleles (Pp). Heterozygous individuals are called carriers when the recessive allele causes a disorder.
Law of Segregation
tap
Mendel's 1st Law: The two alleles for each trait separate during gamete formation, so each gamete carries only ONE allele per gene. During fertilization, offspring receive one allele from each parent. Chromosomal basis: homologs separate in meiosis I.
Law of Independent Assortment
tap
Mendel's 2nd Law: Alleles for DIFFERENT genes assort into gametes independently of one another (when genes are on different chromosomes). Chromosomal basis: independent orientation of homolog pairs at metaphase I. Generates 2²³ gamete types in humans.
Test Cross
tap
A cross between an organism of unknown genotype (dominant phenotype) and a homozygous recessive individual (pp). If any offspring are recessive, the unknown parent must be heterozygous (Pp). Mendel's method for determining genotype.

Mendel's Experimental Design

According to OpenStax, "Mendel's work was unique for its time in that he employed a quantitative, mathematical analysis and used large sample sizes." He worked with the garden pea Pisum sativum for 8 years, choosing it because of its well-defined traits, short generation time, and ability to self-fertilize for creating true-breeding lines.

The Two Laws — Chromosomal Basis
Law of Segregation: Alleles separate because homologous chromosomes separate in Meiosis I — each gamete gets one chromosome from each pair. Law of Independent Assortment: Genes on different chromosomes assort independently because homolog pairs orient randomly at Metaphase I. Genes on the SAME chromosome are LINKED and do NOT assort independently (they tend to be inherited together — unless crossing over occurs).

Lesson 1 Quiz — Mendel & Laws of Inheritance

5 questions · includes data interpretation

1Mendel crossed true-breeding purple-flowered plants (PP) with true-breeding white-flowered plants (pp). All F1 offspring were purple. What does this demonstrate?
  • A
    White is dominant over purple
  • B
    F1 plants are homozygous dominant
  • C
    Purple is dominant over white — heterozygous Pp plants express the dominant phenotype only
  • D
    Both traits blended to produce light purple
All F1 offspring are Pp (heterozygous). They all show the purple phenotype because purple is the dominant allele — it masks the white recessive allele. This is complete dominance: when a dominant allele is present (even in one copy), it determines the phenotype. The white allele is still there genetically — it reappears in F2 when two Pp plants cross and produce pp offspring.
2Mendel's Law of Segregation states that alleles separate during gamete formation. What is the CHROMOSOMAL basis of this law?
  • A
    DNA replication splits the two alleles into separate strands during S phase
  • B
    Homologous chromosomes (each carrying one allele) separate during Meiosis I, so each gamete receives only one allele per gene
  • C
    Sister chromatids separate in Anaphase II, splitting alleles into separate cells
  • D
    Crossing over in Prophase I mixes alleles between chromosomes
The Law of Segregation has a direct chromosomal explanation: homologous chromosomes (each carrying one allele of a gene — one from mom, one from dad) separate during Anaphase I of Meiosis. Each resulting gamete gets only one chromosome from each homologous pair — therefore only one allele per gene. When gametes fuse at fertilization, the two alleles are reunited in the offspring.
3📊 Based on Mendel's F2 data graph, what ratio did he consistently observe for all seven traits?
  • A
    1:1 dominant to recessive
  • B
    2:1 dominant to recessive
  • C
    4:1 dominant to recessive
  • D
    Approximately 3:1 dominant to recessive — about 75% dominant phenotype, 25% recessive
Mendel consistently observed a ~3:1 phenotypic ratio in F2 crosses for all 7 traits. This is mathematically explained by the Punnett square: when two Pp plants cross, they produce PP, Pp, Pp, and pp in a 1:2:1 genotypic ratio — but since PP and Pp both show the dominant phenotype, the phenotypic ratio is 3 dominant : 1 recessive. This was Mendel's key mathematical insight — the ratios were too consistent to be coincidence.
4A tall pea plant (dominant phenotype) is crossed with a short plant (homozygous recessive). Half the offspring are tall and half are short. What is the genotype of the tall parent?
  • A
    Tt — this is a test cross; a 1:1 ratio indicates the unknown parent is heterozygous
  • B
    TT — homozygous dominant would produce all tall offspring
  • C
    tt — but this would produce all short offspring
  • D
    Cannot be determined without more information
This is a test cross — crossing an unknown dominant phenotype with a homozygous recessive (tt). If all offspring are tall → unknown parent is TT. If offspring are 50% tall, 50% short → unknown parent is Tt (heterozygous). Here, the 1:1 ratio of tall:short confirms the parent is Tt. Test crosses are Mendel's elegant tool for revealing hidden genotypes.
5The Law of Independent Assortment ONLY applies when two genes are located on different chromosomes (or far apart on the same chromosome). Why?
  • A
    Genes on the same chromosome always have identical alleles
  • B
    Genes on different chromosomes are always dominant over genes on the same chromosome
  • C
    Genes on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together (linked) because they travel on the same chromosome during meiosis — independent assortment requires genes to be on separate chromosomes that orient independently at Metaphase I
  • D
    All genes are actually on different chromosomes in diploid organisms
Gene linkage: genes on the SAME chromosome tend to be inherited together because they travel on the same chromosome during meiosis. They violate the Law of Independent Assortment unless crossing over separates them. Independent assortment requires that genes be on separate chromosomes — each chromosome pair orients independently at Metaphase I, so the two chromosomes (and their genes) end up in gametes independently. Mendel got lucky — all 7 of his traits were on different chromosomes!

Patterns of Dominance

Three Dominance Patterns

⚠️
Incomplete Dominance ≠ Blending Inheritance
Incomplete dominance looks like blending (red + white = pink) but the ALLELES themselves don't blend — they remain intact and separate during meiosis. Crossing two pink snapdragons (Rr × Rr) produces red (RR), pink (Rr), and white (rr) in a 1:2:1 ratio — proving the original alleles are preserved. True blending would make everything permanently pink with no way to recover red or white.
ABO Blood Type — Multiple Alleles & Codominance

The ABO blood type system is controlled by one gene with three alleles: IA, IB, and i. IA and IB are codominant with each other; both are dominant over i. Each person has exactly two alleles from the three options.

Lesson 2 Quiz — Patterns of Dominance

5 questions · includes blood type data

1A red snapdragon (RR) is crossed with a white snapdragon (rr). All F1 offspring are pink. Which inheritance pattern is this?
  • A
    Codominance — both red and white appear simultaneously
  • B
    Incomplete dominance — neither allele fully dominates; heterozygotes show an intermediate phenotype (pink)
  • C
    Complete dominance — red is dominant over white
  • D
    Multiple allele inheritance
Pink snapdragons (Rr) demonstrate incomplete dominance: neither allele fully masks the other, producing an intermediate phenotype. The R allele produces red pigment; the r allele produces none; one copy of R produces half the pigment → pink. Crossing two pink plants (Rr × Rr) gives 1 RR (red) : 2 Rr (pink) : 1 rr (white) — a 1:2:1 phenotypic ratio, proving the alleles themselves did not blend.
2A type A mother (IAi) and a type B father (IBi) have children. What blood types are possible in their offspring?
  • A
    Only AB and O
  • B
    Only A and B
  • C
    A, B, and AB only
  • D
    A, B, AB, and O — all four blood types are possible
Cross IAi × IBi: gametes from mom = IA or i; gametes from dad = IB or i. Possible offspring: IAIB (type AB), IAi (type A), IBi (type B), ii (type O). All four ABO blood types are possible from this cross — each with 25% probability. This is a classic genetics question that requires knowing the multiple allele system of ABO.
3In codominance, what is the phenotype of a heterozygous individual?
  • A
    Both alleles are fully expressed simultaneously — the heterozygote shows traits of BOTH homozygotes at the same time
  • B
    An intermediate blend between the two homozygous phenotypes
  • C
    The phenotype of the dominant homozygote only
  • D
    No phenotype is expressed — the alleles cancel each other out
In codominance, both alleles are expressed simultaneously and fully — the heterozygote shows BOTH phenotypes, not a blend. Example: Type AB blood (IAIB) expresses both A AND B antigens on red blood cells — not a weaker version of each. Another example: spotted cattle where black and white hairs are both present in a roan coat. Compare to incomplete dominance (blend) vs. codominance (both fully visible).
4📊 Based on the blood type distribution graph, which blood type is most common in the U.S., and what does the rarity of AB tell us?
  • A
    Type A is most common; AB is rare because the IA allele is rare
  • B
    Type B is most common; AB frequency reflects mixing of populations
  • C
    Type O is most common; AB is rare because it requires inheriting BOTH IA and IB alleles — needing each from a different parent who carries the less-common alleles
  • D
    All blood types are equally common
Type O (~44% in the U.S.) is most common because the recessive i allele is the most frequent allele in most populations, and O requires ii (two recessive alleles). Type AB (~4%) is rarest because it requires inheriting IA from one parent AND IB from the other — both are less common alleles, so the probability of inheriting both together is low. Blood type frequencies follow Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium principles.
5Two pink snapdragons (Rr × Rr) are crossed. What is the expected phenotypic ratio of offspring?
  • A
    3 pink : 1 red (3:1 ratio — same as complete dominance)
  • B
    1 red : 2 pink : 1 white (1:2:1 ratio — incomplete dominance produces three distinct phenotypes)
  • C
    All pink — incomplete dominance always produces intermediate offspring
  • D
    1 red : 1 white only — the pink disappears
Cross Rr × Rr: offspring are 1 RR (red) : 2 Rr (pink) : 1 rr (white) = 1:2:1 phenotypic ratio. This is KEY — with incomplete dominance, the phenotypic ratio (1:2:1) equals the genotypic ratio, unlike complete dominance (where 3:1 phenotypic and 1:2:1 genotypic differ). This 1:2:1 phenotypic ratio is the diagnostic signature of incomplete dominance when crossing two heterozygotes.

How to Set Up a Punnett Square

A Punnett square predicts the probability of genotypes in offspring by mapping all possible gamete combinations. Rules: (1) Write the parents' gametes across the top and side. (2) Each gamete carries ONE allele per gene. (3) Fill in each box by combining the top and side alleles. (4) Calculate ratios from the completed grid.

Example: Pp × Pp (Purple × Purple — both heterozygous)

The 1:1 Ratio = Test Cross Signature
Whenever you see a 1:1 offspring ratio (50% dominant, 50% recessive), it means one parent is HETEROZYGOUS (Pp) and the other is HOMOZYGOUS RECESSIVE (pp). This is the test cross. The 3:1 ratio always means BOTH parents are heterozygous (Pp × Pp). These two ratios are the most tested in genetics.

Lesson 3 Quiz — Punnett Squares & Monohybrid Crosses

5 questions · includes ratio graph

1Two heterozygous parents (Bb × Bb) are crossed. What is the expected phenotypic ratio if B (black) is dominant over b (brown)?
  • A
    1:1 black to brown
  • B
    1:2:1 (BB : Bb : bb)
  • C
    3:1 black to brown — 75% black (BB + Bb + Bb), 25% brown (bb)
  • D
    All black offspring
Cross Bb × Bb: offspring are BB (25%), Bb (50%), bb (25%). Genotypic ratio = 1:2:1. Since B is dominant, BB and Bb both appear black → 3:1 phenotypic ratio. This 3:1 ratio is the classic signature of a heterozygote × heterozygote cross with complete dominance. Remember: phenotypic ratio ≠ genotypic ratio unless you're dealing with incomplete dominance.
2A pea plant with round seeds is crossed with a wrinkled-seed plant (rr). 50% of offspring have round seeds and 50% have wrinkled seeds. What is the genotype of the round-seeded parent?
  • A
    Rr — the 1:1 offspring ratio is the signature of a test cross, indicating the dominant-phenotype parent is heterozygous
  • B
    RR — homozygous dominant
  • C
    rR — same as Rr but written differently
  • D
    Cannot be determined
This is a test cross (dominant phenotype × homozygous recessive). The 1:1 offspring ratio (50% round, 50% wrinkled) proves the round-seeded parent is Rr. If it were RR, all offspring would be Rr (round). The test cross is the definitive way to distinguish Rr from RR — you cannot tell from phenotype alone since both are round.
3What is the probability that a child will have cystic fibrosis (cc) if both parents are carriers (Cc × Cc)?
  • A
    0% — carriers don't pass on disease
  • B
    50%
  • C
    75%
  • D
    25% — a 3:1 ratio means 1 in 4 offspring will be cc (affected)
Cross Cc × Cc: offspring = CC (25%), Cc (50%), cc (25%). The probability of cc (affected with cystic fibrosis) = 25% or 1/4. The probability of being a carrier (Cc) = 50%. The probability of being unaffected AND not a carrier (CC) = 25%. This 25% disease risk for two carrier parents is the most clinically important genetics calculation — used constantly in genetic counseling.
4📊 Based on the cross ratio chart, which cross produces 100% dominant phenotype offspring?
  • A
    Pp × Pp
  • B
    PP × pp — crossing homozygous dominant with homozygous recessive produces all Pp heterozygotes (all dominant phenotype)
  • C
    Pp × pp (test cross)
  • D
    pp × pp
PP × pp: every gamete from the PP parent is P; every gamete from the pp parent is p. All offspring must be Pp — heterozygous, showing the dominant phenotype. This is exactly what Mendel observed in the F1 generation (P cross). 100% dominant phenotype, 0% recessive. This is also the basis of hybrid vigor in agriculture — breeders cross homozygous lines to produce all-heterozygous offspring with maximum trait expression.
5A homozygous dominant plant (TT — tall) is crossed with a heterozygous plant (Tt — tall). What is the expected genotypic ratio?
  • A
    1:2:1 (TT:Tt:tt)
  • B
    3:1 (tall:short)
  • C
    1:1 (TT:Tt) — half homozygous dominant, half heterozygous, but all tall
  • D
    All TT homozygous dominant
Cross TT × Tt: gametes from TT parent = only T; gametes from Tt parent = T or t. Offspring: TT (50%) and Tt (50%). Genotypic ratio = 1:1 (TT:Tt). Phenotypic ratio = all tall (100% — since both TT and Tt are tall with complete dominance). Note: the genotypic ratio is 1:1 here but the phenotypic ratio is not 1:1 — don't confuse the two!

Dihybrid Crosses & Independent Assortment
Tracking two genes at once reveals the 9:3:3:1 ratio — one of the most important numbers in all of genetics, and direct proof of Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment.

  • State the expected 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio and explain what each fraction represents.

Dihybrid Cross: Two Genes at Once

A dihybrid cross tracks two independent genes simultaneously. Mendel crossed plants that were heterozygous for both seed color (Y=yellow, y=green) AND seed shape (R=round, r=wrinkled): YyRr × YyRr. The result was a 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio in F2.

🔑
The Product Rule Shortcut — No Full Punnett Square Needed
Since the two genes assort independently, multiply the individual monohybrid probabilities. Example: What is the probability of a YyRr offspring from YyRr × YyRr? P(Yy) from Yy × Yy = 2/4 = 1/2. P(Rr) from Rr × Rr = 2/4 = 1/2. P(YyRr) = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4. This works ONLY when genes are on different chromosomes (independent assortment). For linked genes, you must use recombination data.
Where the 9:3:3:1 Comes From

Think of it as two independent 3:1 ratios multiplied together: (3 yellow : 1 green) × (3 round : 1 wrinkled) = 9 yellow-round : 3 yellow-wrinkled : 3 green-round : 1 green-wrinkled. This mathematical decomposition works because independent assortment makes the two genes behave as if they are separate events.

🧠
9:3:3:1 Memory Check: "Nine dominate, three plus three are singles, one is doubly recessive"
9 = both dominant traits expressed. 3 = first gene dominant, second recessive. 3 = first gene recessive, second dominant. 1 = both genes recessive. Total = 16 boxes in the 4×4 Punnett square. Always sums to 16.

The Chromosome Theory of Heredity

According to OpenStax, "the behaviors of chromosomes during meiosis correspond perfectly to Mendel's laws." Walter Sutton and Theodor Boveri independently proposed in 1902 that genes are located on chromosomes, based on the parallel behavior of chromosomes and Mendel's factors during cell division.

Mendel's ObservationChromosomal Explanation
Alleles segregate during gamete formationHomologous chromosomes separate at Anaphase I of meiosis
Traits assort independentlyGenes on different chromosomes orient independently at Metaphase I
F1 hybrids are heterozygousOne chromosome (allele) from each parent; diploid cells have two homologs
F2 recessive traits reappearRecessive alleles carried on chromosomes never disappeared — just masked
Gene Linkage & Recombination

Genes on the same chromosome are said to be linked — they tend to be inherited together (as a unit) rather than independently. However, crossing over during Prophase I can separate linked genes by physically exchanging chromosome segments, creating new allele combinations called recombinants.

🔑
Recombination Frequency → Genetic Map Distance
The farther apart two linked genes are on a chromosome, the more likely crossing over occurs between them → higher recombination frequency. 1 centimorgan (cM) = 1% recombination frequency. If genes A and B show 15% recombination, they are ~15 cM apart. Genes with >50% recombination behave as if they're on different chromosomes (independent assortment). This is the basis of genetic linkage maps.

Sex Chromosomes & Sex-Linked Inheritance

According to OpenStax, "the presence of the Y chromosome determines male sex" in humans. Females have two X chromosomes (XX); males have one X and one Y (XY). The Y chromosome is much smaller than the X and carries few genes beyond SRY — the sex-determining region Y gene that triggers male development.

Why X-Linked Recessive Disorders Affect Males More Often

Females (XX) need two copies of the recessive allele to express an X-linked recessive disorder (XaXa). Males (XY) only need one copy because they have only one X chromosome — they are hemizygous and have no second X to mask the recessive allele. This is why color blindness affects ~8% of males but only ~0.5% of females.

Polygenic Inheritance & the Bell Curve

When a trait is controlled by many genes (polygenic), each with small additive effects, the distribution of phenotypes in a population follows a normal (bell-shaped) distribution. This explains continuous variation — traits like height and skin color don't fall into a few discrete categories but exist on a spectrum with most people clustered near the average.

🔑

When one gene masks another (epistasis), the 9:3:3:1 dihybrid ratio collapses into a modified ratio. Example: Labrador coat color — 9 black : 3 brown : 4 yellow (instead of 9:3:3:1). The 4 comes from ee dogs (homozygous recessive at the E locus), which are yellow regardless of their genotype at the B locus. Recognizing modified ratios is a diagnostic skill for epistasis.

    Nondisjunction during meiosis — chromosome 21 fails to separate, producing a gamete with 2 copies; fertilization with a normal gamete (1 copy) = 3 total (trisomy)
  • B
    A point mutation that duplicates chromosome 21
  • C
    Translocation of chromosome 21 always (in all cases)
  • D
    Viral infection during fetal development
Nondisjunction (failure of chromosomes to separate in Meiosis I or II) produces a gamete with 2 copies of chromosome 21. That gamete + a normal gamete (1 copy) = trisomy 21 (47 chromosomes, 3 of chromosome 21). Risk increases with maternal age because human oocytes are arrested in Meiosis I from fetal development until ovulation — older eggs have been arrested longer and are more prone to nondisjunction errors.
17In gel electrophoresis, which DNA fragment migrates FARTHEST from the wells?
  • A
    The largest fragment — more charge = more force
  • B
    All fragments migrate equally far
  • C
    The smallest fragment — smaller molecules move through the agarose gel pores more easily
  • D
    Fragments with the highest GC content
Smaller fragments = farther migration. The agarose gel is a molecular sieve — larger DNA molecules are slowed more by the gel matrix. All DNA has approximately the same charge-to-mass ratio, so the electrical force alone doesn't differentiate sizes. The gel matrix creates the separation: small fragments slip through pores easily (fast, far); large fragments are impeded (slow, near wells). This is the fundamental principle of gel electrophoresis.
18In CRISPR-Cas9, what is the role of the guide RNA?
  • A
    It cuts the DNA directly at the target site
  • B
    It base-pairs with the complementary target DNA sequence, directing the Cas9 nuclease to the correct location in the genome
  • C
    It provides the repair template after Cas9 cuts
  • D
    It activates DNA polymerase to copy the edited gene
The guide RNA (gRNA) is complementary to the target DNA sequence — it base-pairs with it and positions Cas9 to cut precisely at that location. This RNA-guided targeting is what makes CRISPR so programmable: to edit a new gene, you just design a new 20-nucleotide gRNA matching your target. Cas9 then cuts both DNA strands, and the cell's repair machinery either disrupts (NHEJ) or replaces (HDR) the gene.
19📊 Based on the Down syndrome risk graph, at what maternal age does risk exceed approximately 1 in 100?
  • A
    Age 25
  • B

  • D
    Approximately age 40 — at 35 risk is ~1/400; at 40 ~1/100; at 45 ~1/30
Down syndrome risk exceeds 1% (~1/100) at approximately age 40. The risk is relatively low before age 35 (<0.3%), then rises exponentially: 35 → 1/400 (0.25%), 40 → 1/100 (1%), 45 → 1/30 (3.3%). This exponential increase explains why prenatal genetic screening is strongly recommended for mothers over 35 — the "advanced maternal age" threshold used in clinical practice.
20Which statement CORRECTLY links Mendel's laws to chromosome behavior in meiosis?
  • A
    Segregation = homologous chromosomes separating at Anaphase I; Independent Assortment = chromosome pairs orienting independently at Metaphase I — both laws are directly explained by meiosis
  • B
    Mendel's laws only apply to genes on different chromosomes — linked genes follow entirely different rules with no chromosomal explanation
  • C
    Chromosomes have no relationship to Mendel's laws
  • D
    Both laws apply to all genes equally regardless of chromosomal location
The Sutton-Boveri chromosome theory: Segregation = homologs separate in Meiosis I → each gamete gets one allele. Independent Assortment = different chromosome pairs orient independently at Metaphase I → genes on different chromosomes end up in gametes independently. Linked genes (same chromosome) violate independent assortment — they travel together unless crossing over separates them. Mendel got lucky: all 7 of his traits happened to be on different chromosomes.
