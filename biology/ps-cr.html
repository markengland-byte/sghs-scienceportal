Core Content
ATP: The Universal Energy Currency

Energy is the ability to do work or cause change. Every living cell requires a constant input of energy for three main categories of work: chemical work (building large molecules), transport work (moving molecules across membranes), and mechanical work (contracting muscles, moving cilia). Cells must convert energy from their environment into a usable form — and that form is ATP (adenosine triphosphate).

Think of ATP like cash. Your body earns money (catabolism, breaking down food) and spends it (anabolism, building molecules, moving muscles). ATP is the currency that makes those transactions happen. Just as you can't pay your electric bill with a pizza, cells can't directly use glucose to contract a muscle fiber — they must first convert it to ATP.

ATP Hydrolysis — Energy Release
ATP + H₂O → ADP + Pᵢ + ~7.3 kcal/mol energyThe bond between the 2nd and 3rd phosphate groups is broken. ADP can be recharged to ATP using energy from respiration or photosynthesis.

🔨 Catabolism — Breaking Down

  • Complex → simple molecules
  • Releases energy → ATP
  • Example: glucose → CO₂ + H₂O
  • Powers the cell's energy supply

🏗️ Anabolism — Building Up

  • Simple → complex molecules
  • Requires ATP input
  • Example: CO₂ + H₂O → glucose
  • Drives growth and repair

Core Content
The Overall Equation & Where It Happens

Photosynthesis is the only biological process that can capture solar energy and convert it into chemical bonds. According to OpenStax Biology 2e, it "powers 99 percent of Earth's ecosystems." Plants, algae, and cyanobacteria are the only photoautotrophs — organisms that feed themselves using light.

Overall Equation — Photosynthesis
6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂The 6 carbons in glucose come from CO₂. The O₂ released comes from splitting water — proven by heavy oxygen isotope experiments.

Photosynthesis occurs in two stages inside the chloroplast: (1) Light-dependent reactions on the thylakoid membrane — capture sunlight to make ATP and NADPH; (2) Calvin cycle in the stroma — use ATP and NADPH to build glucose from CO₂. Plants also contain accessory pigments (chlorophyll b, carotenoids) that absorb additional wavelengths and transfer that energy to chlorophyll a, broadening the range of usable light.

Key Vocabulary
Click Every Card to Flip It
Photosystem II (P680)
tap
Acts FIRST in the light reactions. Absorbs light at 680nm. Ejects electrons that travel through the ETC. Replaces those electrons by splitting water (photolysis) — where O₂ is produced.
Photolysis
tap
Splitting water using light energy at PS II: 2H₂O → 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ + O₂. H⁺ → gradient for ATP. Electrons → replace ejected electrons in PS II. O₂ → released as byproduct (the O₂ we breathe).
Photosystem I (P700)
tap
Acts SECOND in the light reactions. Absorbs light at 700nm. Re-energizes electrons arriving from PS II via the ETC. Passes them to produce NADPH — the reducing power for the Calvin cycle.
Chemiosmosis
tap
ATP synthesis driven by H⁺ ions flowing through ATP synthase down their concentration gradient. H⁺ builds up in thylakoid lumen → flows through ATP synthase into stroma → rotation drives ADP + Pᵢ → ATP.
NADPH
tap
Electron carrier produced at PS I. Carries high-energy electrons to the Calvin cycle, where they reduce CO₂ to glucose. NADP⁺ + 2e⁻ + H⁺ → NADPH.
Core Content
Step-by-Step: How the Light Reactions Work

Step 1 — PS II absorbs light. An electron is excited and ejected to the ETC. PS II replaces it via photolysis: 2H₂O → 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ + O₂. The O₂ is released.

Step 2 — Electrons flow & H⁺ is pumped. Electrons travel plastoquinone → cytochrome b6f → plastocyanin. The cytochrome b6f complex pumps H⁺ into the thylakoid lumen, building the concentration gradient.

Step 3 — Chemiosmosis makes ATP. H⁺ can only return to the stroma through ATP synthase. This flow physically rotates ATP synthase, driving ADP + Pᵢ → ATP.

Step 4 — PS I makes NADPH. Electrons arriving at PS I are re-energized by a second photon. They reduce NADP⁺ → NADPH, which carries electrons to the Calvin cycle.

⚠️
PS II comes BEFORE PS I
Despite the numbering, Photosystem II acts first. The numbers reflect order of discovery, not function. Electron flow: H₂O → PS II → ETC → PS I → NADPH. Many SOL questions test this with intentionally reversed answer choices.

Key Vocabulary
Click Every Card to Flip It
Calvin Cycle
tap
The second stage of photosynthesis. Occurs in the stroma. Uses CO₂ + ATP + NADPH to build G3P (a 3-carbon sugar). Also called light-independent reactions. Needs 6 turns to produce enough G3P for 1 glucose.
Carbon Fixation
tap
Incorporating inorganic CO₂ into an organic molecule. In Stage 1: CO₂ + RuBP → 3-PGA, catalyzed by RuBisCO. Converts gas (inorganic) to organic — "fixing" carbon into living matter.
RuBisCO
tap
The enzyme that catalyzes carbon fixation: CO₂ + RuBP → 2× 3-PGA. The most abundant enzyme on Earth. Slow (3–10 reactions/sec). Can also bind O₂ causing wasteful photorespiration.
G3P (Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate)
tap
The 3-carbon sugar product of the Calvin cycle. Two G3P → one glucose. Remaining G3P molecules regenerate RuBP to keep the cycle going. Also used to make amino acids and fatty acids.
RuBP
tap
Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate. A 5-carbon CO₂ acceptor molecule. Must be regenerated at the end of each cycle turn using ATP. Without RuBP regeneration, the Calvin cycle stops.
Core Content
Three Stages of the Calvin Cycle

Stage 1 — Carbon Fixation: CO₂ + RuBP (5C) → 2× 3-PGA (3C each), catalyzed by RuBisCO. The cycle must run 3 times to fix enough CO₂ to produce 1 G3P (6 turns for 1 glucose).

Stage 2 — Reduction: Each 3-PGA is reduced to G3P using ATP + NADPH. This is where the energy from the light reactions is actually used to build sugar. Per 6 turns: 12 NADPH + 12 ATP consumed to produce 12 G3P.

Stage 3 — RuBP Regeneration: Five of every six G3P molecules are used to regenerate three RuBP molecules, consuming 3 more ATP. This keeps the cycle running. Without RuBP, no new CO₂ can be fixed.

Overall Calvin Cycle (per glucose)
6CO₂ + 18ATP + 12NADPH → Glucose + 18ADP + 18Pᵢ + 12NADP⁺
🔑
Why "Light-Independent" Is Misleading
The Calvin cycle doesn't directly use photons, but it completely depends on the light reactions for ATP and NADPH. Turn off the light → light reactions stop → ATP and NADPH run out → Calvin cycle stops within seconds. Always say "does not directly require light" not "runs in the dark."

Before entering the Krebs cycle, pyruvate must be converted to Acetyl-CoA. This happens at the inner mitochondrial membrane in a reaction called pyruvate oxidation. The pyruvate dehydrogenase complex simultaneously removes one carbon (released as CO₂), takes electrons (forming NADH from NAD⁺), and attaches the remaining 2-carbon acetyl group to Coenzyme A, forming Acetyl-CoA. The Krebs Cycle — What Happens Each Turn

Acetyl-CoA delivers its 2-carbon acetyl group to a 4-carbon molecule called oxaloacetate (OAA), forming 6-carbon citrate. The citrate then passes through 7 more enzyme-catalyzed steps. During this journey, 2 CO₂ molecules are released (one carbon at a time), accounting for the complete oxidation of the acetyl group's 2 carbons. High-energy electrons are captured by NAD⁺ (3 NADH per turn) and FAD (1 FADH₂ per turn). One GTP or ATP is produced directly by substrate-level phosphorylation. At the end, oxaloacetate is regenerated and ready to accept another Acetyl-CoA for the next turn.

The Krebs cycle produces only 2 ATP directly — not much! Its primary role is to be an electron carrier charging station. It strips electrons from carbon compounds and loads them onto NADH and FADH₂. How the Electron Transport Chain Works

The electron transport chain is a series of protein complexes embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane. Its job is to extract the energy stored in NADH and FADH₂ (produced in glycolysis, pyruvate oxidation, and the Krebs cycle) and use it to build an enormous H⁺ gradient that powers ATP synthase.

Step 1 — Electrons Enter from NADH and FADH₂

NADH donates its electrons to Complex I of the ETC. FADH₂ donates its electrons to Complex II. As these electrons pass through the protein complexes, they move "downhill" in terms of energy — releasing energy at each transfer step. This released energy is used to pump H⁺ ions from the mitochondrial matrix into the intermembrane space (the space between the inner and outer membranes).

Step 2 — H⁺ Builds Up in the Intermembrane Space

As electrons travel through Complexes I, III, and IV, each complex acts as a proton pump — physically transporting H⁺ ions across the membrane into the intermembrane space. This creates a very high H⁺ concentration there and a low concentration in the matrix — a proton gradient (also called an electrochemical gradient) that stores potential energy, like water behind a dam.

Step 3 — Chemiosmosis Produces ATP

The inner mitochondrial membrane is impermeable to H⁺ ions — they can only pass back into the matrix through a special channel enzyme called ATP synthase. As H⁺ flows down its concentration gradient through ATP synthase, the flow of ions physically rotates part of the enzyme. This rotation drives the synthesis of ATP from ADP and Pᵢ — a process called chemiosmosis. ATP synthase is sometimes described as a molecular turbine — H⁺ ions turn it like water turns a water wheel.

Step 4 — O₂ Accepts the Final Electrons

At the end of the chain (Complex IV), the electrons have given up most of their energy. These low-energy electrons are accepted by oxygen (O₂) — the final electron acceptor. Oxygen combines with the electrons and H⁺ ions from the matrix to form water (H₂O): O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂O. This is why aerobic respiration requires oxygen — without it, electrons have nowhere to go and the ETC backs up completely, stopping ATP production.

⚠️
Cyanide Poisoning — Blocking the ETC
Cyanide (CN⁻) kills by binding to Complex IV of the ETC, preventing O₂ from accepting electrons. Electrons back up through the entire chain → no H⁺ is pumped → no ATP is made by the ETC. The cell is limited to only 2 ATP from glycolysis. The brain and heart, which need enormous amounts of ATP, fail within minutes. This is why cyanide poisoning is so rapidly fatal.

Core Content — From OpenStax Ch. 7.5
Why Does Fermentation Exist?

During glycolysis, NAD⁺ is reduced to NADH (Step 6 of glycolysis). Normally, the ETC reoxidizes NADH back to NAD⁺ by passing its electrons to O₂. But when oxygen is absent, the ETC shuts down — and NADH piles up. As NAD⁺ is depleted, glycolysis cannot continue (Step 6 is blocked), and the cell stops making ATP entirely. Fermentation solves this by using an organic molecule — pyruvate or acetaldehyde — as the final electron acceptor instead, regenerating NAD⁺ so glycolysis can keep running.

🔑
The Critical Insight About Fermentation
Fermentation does NOT directly produce ATP. It only regenerates NAD⁺ so that glycolysis can continue producing 2 ATP per glucose. The fermentation steps themselves are just NAD⁺ recycling reactions. All ATP in fermentation comes from glycolysis alone.
Lactic Acid Fermentation

In lactic acid fermentation, pyruvate directly accepts electrons from NADH, forming lactate and regenerating NAD⁺. This reaction is catalyzed by lactate dehydrogenase (LDH). No CO₂ is produced. According to OpenStax, "this type of fermentation is used routinely in mammalian red blood cells and in skeletal muscle that has an insufficient oxygen supply."

Lactic Acid Fermentation
Pyruvate + NADH + H⁺ → Lactate + NAD⁺Occurs in human muscle during intense exercise. Also in yogurt/cheese bacteria (Lactobacillus).

You feel lactic acid buildup as the "burn" during intense exercise — though recent research shows the soreness felt days later (DOMS) has different causes. The lactate is eventually transported to the liver and converted back to pyruvate, then to glucose (gluconeogenesis).

Alcoholic Fermentation

In alcoholic fermentation, pyruvate first undergoes decarboxylation (loses CO₂) to form acetaldehyde. Acetaldehyde then accepts electrons from NADH to form ethanol, regenerating NAD⁺. This two-step process produces CO₂ as a byproduct — the gas that makes bread rise and champagne bubble.

Alcoholic Fermentation
Pyruvate → Acetaldehyde + CO₂ (step 1)
Acetaldehyde + NADH + H⁺ → Ethanol + NAD⁺ (step 2)Occurs in yeasts. Used in brewing beer, making wine, and baking bread.

The two great metabolic pathways are mirror images of each other — not just in chemistry, but in their role in sustaining all life on Earth. Understanding their relationship is the key to understanding the biosphere.

Core Concept
The Two Equations — Complementary Processes
Photosynthesis
6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂Producers only (plants, algae, cyanobacteria). Builds glucose; releases O₂.
Aerobic Cellular Respiration
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ~36–38 ATPAll living cells. Breaks down glucose; consumes O₂; releases CO₂ and water.

Notice that photosynthesis and cellular respiration are chemical opposites — the reactants of one are the products of the other. Photosynthesis consumes CO₂ and H₂O, and produces glucose and O₂. Respiration consumes glucose and O₂, and produces CO₂ and H₂O. They form a perfect cycle at the molecular level.

However, they are not reverse reactions — they use completely different enzymes, occur in different organelles, and are regulated independently. Photosynthesis is an anabolic process (builds); cellular respiration is catabolic (breaks down). Even plants perform both — they photosynthesize in the light and respire 24/7.

🔑
All Living Things Perform Cellular Respiration
Only photoautotrophs (plants, algae, cyanobacteria) perform photosynthesis. But ALL living things — including plants — perform cellular respiration. A plant at night is purely a respirer, consuming its own stored glucose and O₂. During the day, photosynthesis greatly exceeds respiration, producing a net gain of O₂ and glucose.
Core Concept
The Carbon Cycle Connection

Photosynthesis and cellular respiration are the two primary biological engines driving the carbon cycle. Carbon atoms move continuously between the atmosphere (as CO₂), living organisms (as organic molecules), and back to the atmosphere.

Photosynthesis removes CO₂ from the atmosphere and fixes it into organic molecules (glucose, cellulose, proteins, etc.) — carbon enters the living world. Cellular respiration returns CO₂ to the atmosphere by breaking down those organic molecules — carbon exits the living world. Decomposers (bacteria and fungi) also return carbon to the atmosphere by breaking down dead organisms. Humans have disrupted this cycle by burning fossil fuels — releasing CO₂ that was fixed millions of years ago, faster than photosynthesis can reabsorb it.

🌿 How Carbon Enters Life

  • Photosynthesis: CO₂ → glucose
  • Producers are the entry point
  • Carbon stored in organic molecules
  • ~100 billion tons CO₂ fixed/year globally

💨 How Carbon Returns to Air

  • Cellular respiration: glucose → CO₂
  • All organisms respire (including plants)
  • Decomposers break down dead matter
  • Burning (combustion) — including fossil fuels
Core Concept
Energy Flow Through Ecosystems

Energy does NOT cycle — it flows in one direction through ecosystems. Photosynthesis converts solar energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. When organisms eat, that chemical energy transfers from organism to organism. But at each transfer, most of the energy is lost as heat — only about 10% is stored in the body of the consumer. This is the 10% Rule (or 10% energy transfer efficiency).

This is why food chains rarely have more than 4–5 levels (trophic levels): with 10% efficiency, by the time you reach a top predator, there is so little energy available that very few top predators can be supported. A grassy field might support thousands of grasshoppers → hundreds of frogs → dozens of snakes → a handful of hawks.
