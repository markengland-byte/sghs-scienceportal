Discovery of DNA as Genetic Material
For decades scientists debated whether proteins or DNA carried hereditary information. Three elegant experiments settled the question — and the answer changed biology forever.

Transformation
tap
The uptake and incorporation of foreign DNA by a cell, changing its genetic characteristics. Griffith showed that dead smooth bacteria could "transform" live rough bacteria into smooth (virulent) bacteria.
Transforming Principle
tap
The mystery molecule in Griffith's experiment that converted rough (non-virulent) bacteria into smooth (virulent). Avery et al. (1944) identified it as DNA — not protein as many had assumed.
Bacteriophage
tap
A virus that infects bacteria ("phage" = eater). Hershey and Chase used T2 phage — which has a protein coat and DNA core — to determine which component (protein or DNA) enters bacterial cells during infection.

tap
Hershey-Chase used ³⁵S (sulfur) to label proteins and ³²P (phosphorus) to label DNA. Since DNA contains phosphorus but not sulfur, and proteins contain sulfur but not phosphorus, they could trace which molecule entered bacterial cells.

🔑
Why the Hershey-Chase Experiment Was Conclusive
The blender step is genius: blending shears off the phage protein coats from the bacterial surface without lysing the bacteria. Centrifugation pellets the bacteria (heavy) and leaves the empty phage coats in the supernatant (light). Measuring ³²P in the bacterial pellet (DNA went IN) and ³⁵S in the supernatant (protein coat stayed OUT) proved that DNA — not protein — is the molecule that carries genetic instructions into the cell.

Lesson 1 Quiz — Discovery of DNA

5 questions · includes Hershey-Chase data

1In Griffith's 1928 experiment, he injected mice with a mixture of heat-killed smooth (S) bacteria and live rough (R) bacteria. The mice died and live smooth bacteria were recovered. What did this demonstrate?
  • A
    Heat-killed bacteria can spontaneously regenerate when warmed
  • B
    Rough bacteria mutated spontaneously into smooth bacteria
  • C
    Some molecule from the dead S bacteria could "transform" live R bacteria into virulent S bacteria — a transforming principle existed, though its identity was unknown
  • D
    Proteins from dead smooth bacteria restored virulence to rough bacteria
Griffith's transformation experiment showed that something from the dead smooth (virulent) bacteria could chemically convert live rough (non-virulent) bacteria into smooth bacteria — a process he called transformation. The "transforming principle" was some biological molecule (unknown in 1928) that carried genetic information from the S strain and incorporated it into the R strain. Griffith suspected protein; Avery et al. later proved it was DNA.
2Avery, MacLeod, and McCarty (1944) treated S bacteria extract with different enzymes and tested whether transformation still occurred. What was the critical result?
  • A
    Protease (destroys protein) abolished transformation — protein is the genetic material
  • B
    DNase (destroys DNA) was the only enzyme that abolished transformation — definitively identifying DNA as the transforming principle
  • C
    RNase (destroys RNA) abolished transformation — RNA carries genetic information
  • D
    All enzymes abolished transformation equally — multiple molecules carry genetic information
Avery et al. used a process of elimination: destroy each major molecular class and see which destroys the transforming ability. Protease → still transforms. RNase → still transforms. DNase → transformation abolished. Conclusion: DNA is the transforming principle. This was the first direct biochemical evidence that DNA is the genetic material. Despite its elegance, many scientists (including Avery's own director) were skeptical because the prevailing view was that proteins (with their 20 amino acid variety) were more suitable for encoding information than DNA (with only 4 bases).
3In the Hershey-Chase experiment, why did they use ³⁵S to label proteins and ³²P to label DNA?
  • A
    ³⁵S and ³²P were the cheapest radioactive isotopes available in 1952
  • B
    Both isotopes emit the same type of radiation, making comparison easier
  • C
    ³⁵S labels carbohydrates; ³²P labels lipids in the phage
  • D
    Sulfur (S) is found in amino acids (protein) but NOT in DNA; phosphorus (P) is found in the phosphate backbone of DNA but NOT in proteins — so each isotope uniquely labels only one molecule type
The key is biochemical specificity: Sulfur is present in the amino acids methionine and cysteine (therefore in proteins) but absent from DNA. Phosphorus is present in the phosphate groups of the DNA backbone but absent from proteins. So: ³⁵S uniquely labels protein; ³²P uniquely labels DNA. This allows them to track each molecule separately. The experiment's brilliance is in the choice of tracers — they exploit the known biochemical differences between proteins and DNA to distinguish them unambiguously.
4📊 Based on the Hershey-Chase data graph, what does finding ³²P (DNA tracer) primarily in the bacterial PELLET prove?
  • A
    DNA entered the bacterial cells — the phage injected its DNA into the bacteria to direct new phage production; DNA is the genetic material that carries replication instructions
  • B
    DNA remained outside the bacteria attached to the phage coat
  • C
    Bacteria selectively absorbed phosphorus from the culture medium
  • D
    DNA was too large to enter bacteria and was trapped in the pellet by its mass
³²P (DNA label) in the bacterial pellet means phage DNA entered the bacteria — it was injected through the bacterial membrane. The bacteria then used this injected DNA as a template to produce hundreds of new phage. ³⁵S (protein label) stayed in the supernatant (with the empty phage coats that were sheared off by blending). Together: DNA went in and directed replication; protein stayed out. This proved that DNA — not the protein coat — carries the genetic information needed to produce new viruses.
5Why did scientists initially suspect PROTEIN rather than DNA to be the genetic material?
  • A
    Proteins are found only in the nucleus where chromosomes are located
  • B
    DNA was not discovered until after Griffith's experiment
  • C
    Proteins are made of 20 different amino acids (providing enormous information complexity), while DNA appeared to have only 4 repeating bases — proteins seemed more capable of encoding the diversity of life's traits
  • D
    Early experiments consistently showed protein entering bacterial cells during phage infection
The "protein hypothesis" was based on chemical logic: proteins have 20 different amino acid building blocks that can be arranged in virtually limitless combinations — providing more than enough informational capacity to encode hereditary traits. DNA appeared to consist of only 4 bases (A, T, G, C) in a seemingly monotonous repeating pattern (the "tetranucleotide hypothesis"). It seemed implausible that such a simple molecule could encode the complexity of life. Watson and Crick's double helix (1953) revealed that 4 bases are more than sufficient for unlimited information storage through sequence variation.

DNA Structure & the Double Helix
Watson and Crick's 1953 model was one of the greatest scientific achievements of the 20th century — but it was built on the work of at least four other scientists. Understanding DNA's structure explains everything about how it functions.
⏱ ~30 min
📖 OpenStax Biology 2e, 14.2

  • Describe the DNA double helix: antiparallel strands, base pairs (hydrogen bonds), sugar-phosphate backbone, 3.4 nm per turn.

Key Vocabulary
Click Every Card to Flip It
Nucleotide
tap
The monomer of DNA. Consists of three parts: (1) deoxyribose (5-carbon sugar), (2) one phosphate group, (3) one nitrogenous base (A, T, G, or C). Nucleotides link via phosphodiester bonds to form the DNA strand backbone.
Purines vs. Pyrimidines
tap
Purines: Adenine (A) and Guanine (G) — double-ring structures. Pyrimidines: Thymine (T) and Cytosine (C) — single-ring structures. Purines always pair with pyrimidines (A-T, G-C) — keeping the helix width constant at 2 nm. Memory: "Pure As Gold" (Purines = Adenine, Guanine).
Chargaff's Rules
tap
In any DNA molecule: %A = %T and %G = %C. Also: A+G = T+C (purines = pyrimidines). Discovered by Erwin Chargaff in 1950. These ratios are consistent regardless of species — the key clue that A pairs with T and G pairs with C in the double helix.
Antiparallel
tap
The two DNA strands run in opposite directions: one 5'→3' and the other 3'→5'. The 5' end has a free phosphate group; the 3' end has a free hydroxyl (-OH) group. Antiparallel orientation is required for proper base pairing and is essential for replication directionality.
Hydrogen Bonds
tap
The weak bonds holding complementary base pairs together. A-T: 2 hydrogen bonds. G-C: 3 hydrogen bonds. Individually weak, but collectively (billions of base pairs) they stabilize the helix. Their weakness allows the strands to be separated during replication and transcription.
Sugar-Phosphate Backbone
tap
The structural "rails" of the DNA ladder formed by alternating deoxyribose sugars and phosphate groups linked by strong covalent phosphodiester bonds. The bases project inward like "rungs." The backbone is hydrophilic (faces water); the bases are hydrophobic (face inward — stabilized by base stacking).

🧠
Mnemonics for Base Pairing
"A-T: After Thinking" (A pairs with T). "G-C: Got Confused" (G pairs with C). Or simply: the two letters that look similar DON'T pair — A doesn't pair with G; T doesn't pair with C. The two with more hydrogen bonds are G≡C (three bonds, both have extra H-bond donors/acceptors). A=T has two bonds. G-C pairs are stronger and more thermally stable.
🔑
Rosalind Franklin's Critical Contribution
Rosalind Franklin's X-ray crystallography (Photo 51, 1952) provided the key data that revealed: (1) DNA is a helix, (2) it has 2 strands, (3) the sugar-phosphate backbone is on the OUTSIDE, (4) the helix dimensions (3.4 nm pitch, 2 nm width). Watson and Crick used Franklin's data (shared without her knowledge by Wilkins) to build their correct model. Franklin died in 1958 before the Nobel Prize was awarded to Watson, Crick, and Wilkins in 1962 — Nobel Prizes are not awarded posthumously.

Lesson 2 Quiz — DNA Structure

5 questions · includes base composition data

1A DNA sample has 28% adenine. What percentage of the bases are guanine?
  • A
    28% — G equals A by Chargaff's rules
  • B
    22% — A=T (28%+28%=56% of bases), leaving 44% for G+C; G=C so G = 22%
  • C
    44% — G+C equals the remaining percentage
  • D
    72% — G is the complement of A
Chargaff's rules: A=T and G=C. If A = 28%, then T = 28%. A+T = 56%. Remaining: G+C = 100% − 56% = 44%. Since G=C: G = 44%/2 = 22%. Also: C = 22%. Check: 28+28+22+22 = 100%. This calculation is one of the most tested in molecular biology — know it cold: given A%, find T (=A%), then G = C = (100% − 2A%) / 2.
2Adenine is a purine and thymine is a pyrimidine. Why must a purine always pair with a pyrimidine?
  • A
    Purines and pyrimidines have complementary charges that attract each other
  • B
    Purines are water-soluble; pyrimidines are not — they balance each other chemically
  • C
    Purines are only found on the template strand; pyrimidines only on the coding strand
  • D
    A purine (double-ring) paired with a pyrimidine (single-ring) gives a constant 2 nm width — if two purines paired, the helix would bulge wider; two pyrimidines would make it too narrow
The DNA helix maintains a constant 2 nm width because purines (2-ring, large) always pair with pyrimidines (1-ring, small): large + small = constant width. Purine + purine = too wide; pyrimidine + pyrimidine = too narrow. This structural constraint, plus the specific hydrogen bonding geometry (A-T: 2 H-bonds; G-C: 3 H-bonds), means only A-T and G-C pairings are sterically and energetically favored. Mismatched pairs (A-G, T-C) are detected and repaired by DNA mismatch repair machinery.
3The two strands of DNA are described as "antiparallel." What does this mean?
  • A
    The two strands run in opposite directions — one 5'→3' and the other 3'→5'; the 5' end (free phosphate) of one strand is adjacent to the 3' end (free -OH) of the other
  • B
    The two strands have opposite base compositions — one strand is all purines, the other all pyrimidines
  • C
    The two strands wind in opposite directions around the helix axis
  • D
    The two strands are parallel but face opposite directions in 3D space
Antiparallel means the two strands run in opposite 5'→3' directions. If one strand runs 5'→3' left-to-right, the complementary strand runs 5'→3' right-to-left (or 3'→5' left-to-right). This is critical for: (1) Proper base pairing geometry; (2) DNA replication — DNA polymerase can only add nucleotides to the 3' end, so the two strands are synthesized differently (leading vs. lagging). The 5' carbon of deoxyribose attaches to the phosphate group; the 3' carbon has the free -OH where new nucleotides are added.
4📊 Based on Chargaff's data, what is true about ALL the organisms shown in the graph?
  • A
    All organisms have equal amounts of A+T and G+C — the ratio is always 1:1 between these pairs
  • B
    All organisms have the same total base composition (same %A, %T, %G, %C)
  • C
    In every organism %A ≈ %T and %G ≈ %C — but the A+T:G+C ratio varies between species (it reflects species-specific DNA composition)
  • D
    Higher organisms have more G+C than lower organisms
Chargaff's universal rule: %A = %T and %G = %C in any DNA molecule from any organism. But the A+T:G+C ratio varies between species — some organisms have AT-rich DNA; others have GC-rich DNA. Example: Human DNA is ~41% GC; some bacteria have ~70% GC content. The universal A=T, G=C rule was the key clue for Watson and Crick — it implied A pairs specifically with T and G pairs specifically with C in the double helix. Chargaff's rules are the biochemical fingerprint of the double helix structure.
5G-C base pairs are held together by 3 hydrogen bonds; A-T pairs have 2 hydrogen bonds. What is the biological consequence of this difference?
  • A
    DNA regions with more A-T pairs are more stable and harder to separate during replication
  • B
    DNA regions with more G-C pairs require more energy (higher temperature) to separate the strands — GC-rich regions have a higher melting temperature than AT-rich regions
  • C
    G-C pairs provide structural support; A-T pairs are where genes are located
  • D
    The number of hydrogen bonds has no biological significance
More hydrogen bonds = more energy required to separate strands. GC-rich DNA has a higher melting temperature (Tm) than AT-rich DNA. This has practical applications: PCR annealing temperature depends on primer GC content (more GC = higher annealing temperature needed). In gene regulation: promoter regions often have AT-rich sequences (TATA box) — they melt more easily at physiological temperature, facilitating RNA polymerase entry. Genome stability: centromeres and telomeres are often AT-rich; coding regions of thermophilic bacteria living at high temperature tend to have higher GC content for thermal stability.

Semiconservative Replication: The Meselson-Stahl Proof

Three models for DNA replication were proposed after Watson-Crick: conservative (old helix stays together; new helix is all new), semiconservative (each new helix has one old + one new strand), and dispersive (old and new DNA mixed throughout both helices). In 1958, Meselson and Stahl used density-gradient centrifugation with ¹⁵N (heavy) and ¹⁴N (light) nitrogen to prove the model is semiconservative.

🔑
Why DNA Polymerase Needs a Primer
DNA polymerase III can only ADD nucleotides to an existing 3'-OH group — it cannot initiate a new strand de novo. Therefore, the enzyme primase first synthesizes a short RNA primer to provide the initial 3'-OH. DNA pol III then extends from the primer's 3' end. Later, DNA pol I removes the RNA primer (5'→3' exonuclease) and fills in the gap with DNA. Ligase seals the final nick. This requirement for a primer is also the basis for PCR — you must provide synthetic DNA primers for each target sequence.

Lesson 3 Quiz — Replication Overview & Enzymes

5 questions · includes Meselson-Stahl data

1What does "semiconservative replication" mean?
  • A
    The original DNA strands are destroyed during replication — only new strands are conserved
  • B
    The original double helix is entirely conserved — a completely new helix is synthesized alongside it
  • C
    Each new DNA double helix consists of one original (parental) strand and one newly synthesized strand — the original strands serve as templates and are "conserved" in each daughter molecule
  • D
    The original DNA is broken into fragments and distributed between daughter molecules
Semiconservative: each daughter DNA molecule has one original (parental) strand and one new strand. The parental strands are never destroyed — they serve as templates and become part of the daughter molecules. This is "semi" (half) conserved: each parental strand is conserved, but the original double-stranded helix is not (it's split). Meselson-Stahl proved this model by showing that after one replication, ALL DNA was intermediate density (one ¹⁵N strand + one ¹⁴N strand) — not the mix of heavy and light that conservative replication would produce.
2DNA polymerase III can only synthesize DNA in the 5'→3' direction. Why does this create a problem, and what is its biological significance?
  • A
    It means DNA can only replicate when the cell is at a specific temperature
  • B
    Since the template strands are antiparallel, one template can be read 3'→5' (allowing continuous synthesis), but the other template runs 5'→3' — so synthesis on that template must be discontinuous (Okazaki fragments), creating the leading/lagging strand problem
  • C
    It means DNA pol III can only replicate DNA in the S phase of the cell cycle
  • D
    It requires two separate DNA polymerases — one for each strand
Because DNA pol III only works 5'→3', it reads the template 3'→5'. The two template strands are antiparallel: one template runs 3'→5' relative to the replication fork → DNA pol can synthesize continuously in the direction of fork movement (leading strand). The other template runs 5'→3' → DNA pol must move AWAY from the fork to synthesize 5'→3', making short Okazaki fragments that are later joined (lagging strand). This 5'→3' constraint is fundamental to understanding the leading/lagging strand mechanism.
3What is the function of DNA ligase in replication?
  • A
    Ligase unwinds the double helix ahead of the replication fork
  • B
    Ligase removes RNA primers from the lagging strand
  • C
    Ligase synthesizes new DNA at the 3' end of the primer
  • D
    Ligase seals the phosphodiester bond (nick) between adjacent DNA fragments — joining Okazaki fragments after primers are replaced with DNA, producing a continuous lagging strand
After DNA Pol I removes each RNA primer and fills in with DNA, a "nick" remains in the phosphodiester backbone — a break where one fragment ends and the next begins (the 3'-OH of one fragment is adjacent to the 5'-phosphate of the next but not covalently bonded). DNA ligase seals this nick by forming a phosphodiester bond, using ATP (eukaryotes) or NAD⁺ (bacteria) as energy. This joins all the Okazaki fragments into a continuous lagging strand. Ligase is also essential in DNA repair — it seals nicks after repair polymerases fill in gaps.
4📊 Based on the Meselson-Stahl graph, what result at the SECOND replication generation (2nd generation) would have been seen if replication were CONSERVATIVE (not semiconservative)?
  • A
    Conservative replication at generation 2 would show a HEAVY band (the conserved original ¹⁵N/¹⁵N helix) AND a LIGHT band (the all-new ¹⁴N/¹⁴N helices) — but NO intermediate band
  • B
    All DNA would be light (¹⁴N/¹⁴N) at generation 2 with conservative replication
  • C
    Equal amounts of heavy, intermediate, and light bands at generation 2
  • D

The Replication Fork

DNA replication proceeds bidirectionally from each origin of replication, forming two replication forks. At each fork, helicase unwinds the DNA ahead, creating a Y-shaped structure. Both strands serve as templates simultaneously — but since DNA pol III can only work 5'→3', the two strands are synthesized by different mechanisms.

What Is a Mutation?

A mutation is any permanent alteration in the nucleotide sequence of DNA. Mutations can occur in two contexts: germline mutations occur in egg or sperm cells and are inherited by offspring; somatic mutations occur in body cells and affect only the individual (not passed to offspring). Mutations can be caused by replication errors, spontaneous chemical changes, or mutagens (UV light, radiation, chemical carcinogens).

Substitution (Point) Mutations

A substitution replaces one nucleotide with another. Two subtypes:

Frameshift = Catastrophic Change Downstream
Because codons are read in non-overlapping triplets from a fixed start point, adding or removing nucleotides (not in multiples of 3) shifts every downstream codon. ALL amino acids from the mutation site to the end of the protein are changed. Frameshifts almost always produce a completely non-functional protein — and often create a new premature stop codon. Insertions or deletions of 3 nucleotides (or multiples of 3) add or remove whole codons without a frameshift — these can still be pathogenic but are less catastrophic.

Sickle cell disease is caused by a single A→T transversion in codon 6 of the β-globin gene: GAG (glutamic acid, hydrophilic) → GTG (valine, hydrophobic). This one amino acid change: (1) causes hemoglobin molecules to polymerize under low-oxygen conditions; (2) deforms red blood cells into sickle shapes; (3) causes vaso-occlusion, pain crises, anemia, and organ damage. One nucleotide change out of 3.2 billion → life-threatening disease. This example illustrates why the specific location and type of amino acid substitution matters enormously.

Griffith's transformation showed that a "transforming principle" — some biological molecule — could transfer genetic information between bacterial strains. 7What does "semiconservative replication" mean?
  • A
    The entire original double helix is conserved; a completely new double helix is synthesized from free nucleotides
  • C
    The original DNA is broken into fragments and distributed among daughter molecules
  • C
    Each daughter DNA molecule consists of one original (parental) strand and one newly synthesized strand — parental strands serve as templates and are conserved in each daughter
  • D
    Only one strand is replicated; the other parental strand is conserved intact
Semiconservative: each daughter molecule has ONE parental strand (retained/conserved) + ONE new strand. The parental strands are templates — they are never destroyed, but the original double helix is split between two daughter molecules. Meselson-Stahl proved this: after one replication in light ¹⁴N medium, ALL DNA was intermediate density (one heavy ¹⁵N + one light ¹⁴N strand) — consistent only with semiconservative replication.
8What is the function of helicase during DNA replication?
  • A
    Helicase unwinds the double helix at the replication fork by breaking the hydrogen bonds between base pairs — creating the single-stranded templates for DNA polymerase
  • B
    Helicase synthesizes the RNA primer needed by DNA polymerase
  • C
    Helicase removes RNA primers and replaces them with DNA
  • D
    Helicase seals the nicks between Okazaki fragments
Helicase unwinds the double helix by disrupting the hydrogen bonds between base pairs at the replication fork — it travels along the DNA, using ATP to separate the strands and expose single-stranded templates. Ahead of helicase, positive supercoiling builds up (the DNA gets overwound) — relieved by topoisomerase II (DNA gyrase in bacteria). Behind helicase, single-strand binding proteins (SSBPs) stabilize the exposed single strands and keep them available for polymerase.
9Why does DNA polymerase require a primer to begin synthesis?
  • A
    DNA polymerase needs the primer to recognize the correct starting point (origin of replication)
  • B
    The primer provides the energy source for DNA synthesis (similar to ATP)
  • C
    DNA polymerase uses the primer as a template, not a 3'-OH starting point
  • D
    DNA polymerase can only ADD nucleotides to an existing 3'-OH group — it cannot catalyze the formation of the first phosphodiester bond de novo; primase provides the initial 3'-OH by synthesizing a short RNA primer
DNA pol III's active site specifically requires a 3'-OH group to add the next nucleotide — it cannot start from scratch. Primase (RNA polymerase) can start de novo, synthesizing a short RNA primer (~10 nt) that provides the 3'-OH for DNA pol III to extend. After synthesis, RNA primers are removed by DNA pol I (5'→3' exonuclease) and replaced with DNA, then ligase seals the nick. This is also why PCR requires synthetic primers for each target sequence.

11On the lagging strand during DNA replication, what is an Okazaki fragment?
  • A
    A region of the lagging strand where helicase has created a single-stranded bubble
  • B
    The RNA primer that initiates each round of synthesis on the lagging strand
  • C
    A short DNA fragment (~100–200 bp in eukaryotes) synthesized in the direction away from the replication fork — the lagging strand is synthesized as many such discontinuous fragments that are later joined by ligase
  • D
    A mismatched base pair detected by proofreading on the lagging strand
Okazaki fragments are the short DNA pieces synthesized on the lagging strand. Because DNA pol III can only work 5'→3', and the lagging strand template runs in the "wrong" direction relative to the fork, synthesis must proceed in short segments moving away from the fork. Each fragment needs its own RNA primer (from primase), is extended by DNA pol III, then the primer is removed by DNA pol I, the gap filled, and ligase seals the fragments into a continuous strand. Named after Reiji Okazaki who discovered them in 1968.
12DNA ligase seals which type of bond during replication?
  • A
    Hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs
  • B
    Peptide bonds in newly synthesized proteins
  • C
    Glycosidic bonds between the nitrogenous base and deoxyribose
  • D
    Phosphodiester bonds — specifically sealing the nick (break) in the phosphodiester backbone between adjacent DNA fragments after primers have been removed and gaps filled
Ligase specifically seals a nick — a break in one strand of the double helix where the 3'-OH of one fragment is adjacent to the 5'-phosphate of the next but the phosphodiester bond is not yet formed. Ligase uses ATP (eukaryotes) or NAD⁺ (bacteria) to form the phosphodiester bond, joining the fragments. Ligase is also used in recombinant DNA technology to join foreign DNA to vectors, in DNA repair, and in PCR-based applications. It only seals nicks (adjacent fragments already positioned correctly) — it cannot join fragments with gaps between them.
13What is the end-replication problem and why does it only affect LINEAR chromosomes?
  • A
    When the final RNA primer at the 5' end of the lagging strand is removed, there is no upstream DNA to fill in the gap — the chromosome shortens. Circular chromosomes (bacteria) have no ends, so this problem doesn't arise.
  • B
    Linear chromosomes cannot be replicated at all without telomerase — they need a special mechanism for all replication
  • C
    The ends of linear chromosomes are too hard for helicase to unwind, causing replication failure
  • D
    Circular chromosomes have the same problem but their circular topology conceals the shortening
End-replication problem: on a linear chromosome, the outermost RNA primer on the lagging strand is at the very 5' end of the chromosome. When it's removed, there is no upstream 3'-OH for DNA pol I to fill in the gap → the chromosome is shorter by one primer length. Circular chromosomes (bacteria, mitochondria) don't have free ends, so the gap left by primer removal can be filled by extending from the adjacent DNA. Eukaryotes solve the end-replication problem with telomeres + telomerase.

15DNA polymerase III has a 3'→5' exonuclease (proofreading) activity. How does this activity work?
  • A
    It scans ahead of the synthesis site, removing any damaged bases on the template strand
  • B
    After synthesis is complete, it goes back and checks the entire newly-made strand
  • C
    It removes the RNA primer after Okazaki fragment synthesis
  • D
    After each nucleotide addition, the polymerase checks the new base pair; if mismatched, the exonuclease domain removes the incorrect nucleotide from the 3' end, and the correct nucleotide is added in its place — reducing the error rate 100-1,000 fold
Proofreading is immediate and co-replicational: after adding each nucleotide, DNA pol III pauses and checks if the new base pair has the correct geometry (hydrogen bonding and shape). A mismatch creates steric distortion → triggers the 3'→5' exonuclease → removes the last-added (incorrect) nucleotide → polymerase re-tries with the correct nucleotide. 16The codon AGA (Arginine) is changed to AGA at the DNA level, but the mRNA codon changes. If the resulting mRNA codon still specifies arginine, this is a:
  • A
    Missense mutation
  • B
    Nonsense mutation
  • C
    Silent (synonymous) mutation — the nucleotide sequence changed but the encoded amino acid is the same, due to the degeneracy of the genetic code
  • D
    Frameshift mutation
A silent (synonymous) mutation changes the nucleotide sequence but not the amino acid. This is possible because most amino acids are encoded by multiple codons (the genetic code is degenerate). Example: all 6 arginine codons (CGU, CGC, CGA, CGG, AGA, AGG) code for the same amino acid — a mutation between any of them is silent. Silent mutations are most common at the third codon position (wobble position) and have no effect on protein function, though they can rarely affect mRNA stability or translation rate.
