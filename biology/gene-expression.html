The Central Dogma
Francis Crick formulated the Central Dogma in 1958 — the fundamental principle that information flows from DNA to RNA to protein. It is called a "dogma" not because it cannot be questioned, but because it was proposed before the evidence was complete. The evidence turned out to be overwhelming.
⏱ ~20 min
📖 OpenStax Biology 2e, 15.1
🎯 Lesson Objectives
  • State the Central Dogma: DNA → RNA → Protein.
  • Distinguish transcription (DNA→RNA) from translation (RNA→Protein).
  • Explain the two known exceptions: reverse transcription (RNA→DNA) and direct RNA replication.
  • Explain why information flows in this direction and not in reverse (protein→RNA).
Key Vocabulary
Click Every Card to Flip It
Central Dogma
tap
The fundamental principle of molecular biology: genetic information flows from DNA → RNA → Protein. Proposed by Francis Crick (1958). Describes how inherited information in DNA is decoded to make functional proteins.
Transcription
tap
The synthesis of RNA from a DNA template. Occurs in the NUCLEUS (eukaryotes). DNA → mRNA. Uses RNA polymerase. The RNA copy carries the gene's coding information to the ribosome. Similar chemistry to DNA replication but produces a single-stranded RNA product.
Translation
tap
The synthesis of protein from an mRNA template. Occurs at RIBOSOMES in the cytoplasm. mRNA → Protein. Ribosomes "read" the mRNA three nucleotides (one codon) at a time and tRNA delivers the matching amino acid. Produces the polypeptide chain.
Reverse Transcription
tap
An exception to the Central Dogma: RNA → DNA. Carried out by retroviruses (HIV) using the enzyme reverse transcriptase. The viral RNA genome is copied into DNA, which integrates into the host chromosome. Basis for antiretroviral drugs and PCR-based RNA detection (RT-PCR).
Gene Expression
tap
The process by which the information encoded in a gene is used to produce a functional product (usually a protein). Includes both transcription AND translation. Different genes are expressed in different cell types at different times — this is how all cells in your body (same DNA) can perform different functions.
RNA Polymerase
tap
The enzyme that synthesizes RNA from a DNA template during transcription. Unlike DNA polymerase, RNA polymerase does NOT need a primer — it can start a new chain de novo. It synthesizes RNA in the 5'→3' direction by reading the DNA template strand 3'→5'. Three types in eukaryotes (I, II, III); RNA Pol II makes mRNA.

🔑
Why Information Cannot Flow Protein → RNA (The True Dogma)
The original Central Dogma states that information cannot flow from protein back to nucleic acid. No enzyme can "read" a protein sequence and synthesize the corresponding DNA or RNA. This is the truly inviolable rule — the two exceptions (RNA→DNA, RNA→RNA) violate the simplified version but not the core principle. There is no known mechanism for protein→DNA or protein→RNA information flow. This means genetic information is always stored in nucleic acids, never in proteins.

5 questions · includes compartmentalization data

1The Central Dogma of molecular biology describes the flow of genetic information. Which sequence is CORRECT?
  • A
    Protein → RNA → DNA
  • B
    RNA → DNA → Protein
  • C
    DNA → RNA → Protein — transcription converts DNA to mRNA; translation converts mRNA to protein
  • D
    DNA → Protein → RNA
The Central Dogma: DNA → RNA (transcription) → Protein (translation). DNA stores hereditary information in the nucleus. RNA polymerase transcribes a gene into mRNA. Ribosomes translate the mRNA codon by codon into a polypeptide chain. Information always flows this direction in normal cellular function. The term "dogma" is historical — Crick used it before verification, but the evidence is now overwhelming.
2HIV is a retrovirus. Its genome is single-stranded RNA. After entering a cell, it converts its RNA genome to DNA. What enzyme carries out this process?
  • A
    DNA polymerase — it copies RNA using normal replication machinery
  • B
    Reverse transcriptase — an enzyme that synthesizes DNA from an RNA template (RNA→DNA), the opposite of normal transcription
  • C
    RNA polymerase — it runs the transcription reaction backwards
  • D
    Ribonuclease — it degrades RNA and uses the nucleotides to build DNA
Reverse transcriptase: synthesizes DNA from an RNA template — the reverse of normal transcription. HIV packages reverse transcriptase in its virion. After infection: reverse transcriptase copies viral RNA → DNA; the DNA integrates into the host chromosome (becoming the "provirus") where it is expressed using host cellular machinery. Antiretroviral drugs (nucleoside/nucleotide reverse transcriptase inhibitors = NRTIs) target this enzyme. Reverse transcriptase is also used in the lab for RT-PCR (detecting RNA viruses like SARS-CoV-2).
3Transcription and translation occur at different locations in eukaryotic cells. Why is this separation important?
  • A
    The nucleus is warmer, which is required for transcription but would damage ribosomes
  • B
    DNA must be translated into protein before it can be transcribed into RNA
  • C
    Ribosomes are too large to enter the nucleus where transcription occurs
  • D
    Separation allows eukaryotes to process the pre-mRNA (add cap, poly-A tail, remove introns) before it reaches the ribosome — ensuring only mature, correct mRNA is translated. Prokaryotes lack this capability since they have no nucleus (transcription and translation are coupled).
The nuclear/cytoplasmic compartmentalization allows eukaryotes to extensively process pre-mRNA before translation. This includes: 5' capping (protects from degradation, helps ribosome recognition), 3' polyadenylation (stability, nuclear export), and splicing (removes introns, can join exons in different combinations = alternative splicing). Prokaryotes: no nucleus → transcription and translation coupled — ribosomes begin translating the mRNA while it's still being transcribed. This prokaryotic "cotranscriptional translation" is faster but allows no processing.
4Why is the statement "information cannot flow from protein to nucleic acid" considered the true, inviolable core of the Central Dogma?
  • A
    No known enzyme can read a protein's amino acid sequence and synthesize a corresponding nucleic acid — protein sequences carry no mechanism for directing their own encoding back into DNA or RNA
  • B
    Proteins are in the cytoplasm and cannot access the DNA in the nucleus
  • C
    Proteins are made of amino acids which are chemically incompatible with nucleotide synthesis
  • D
    The Central Dogma was proven absolute — no exceptions exist at all
The absolute rule: protein sequence information CANNOT be converted back to nucleic acid sequence. There is no "reverse translation" enzyme. Even prions (misfolded proteins that cause other proteins to misfold) don't violate this — they propagate structural information, not sequence information. The exceptions (RNA→DNA via reverse transcriptase; RNA→RNA via RNA-dependent RNA polymerase) don't violate the core principle because they are nucleic acid to nucleic acid transfers, not protein to nucleic acid. Crick's insight was that the information alphabet of proteins (20 amino acids) has no chemical equivalence to nucleic acids (4 bases).
5📊 Based on the compartmentalization graph, what is the key difference between eukaryotes and prokaryotes in terms of gene expression?
  • A
    Eukaryotes perform translation in the nucleus; prokaryotes do not
  • B
    Prokaryotes require RNA processing before translation; eukaryotes do not
  • C
    Eukaryotes separate transcription (nucleus) from translation (cytoplasm), allowing RNA processing; prokaryotes couple transcription and translation simultaneously in the cytoplasm — ribosomes begin translating mRNA while it is still being transcribed
  • D
    Both eukaryotes and prokaryotes couple transcription and translation in the nucleus
Prokaryotes: no nucleus → mRNA is translated as fast as it's transcribed (coupled). Ribosomes attach to the mRNA 5' end before transcription is even complete. Very fast but no RNA processing possible. Eukaryotes: nuclear membrane separates transcription from translation → pre-mRNA is transcribed in the nucleus → extensively processed (cap, poly-A, splicing) → exported through nuclear pores → translated on cytoplasmic ribosomes. This separation takes more time but enables much more complex gene regulation and diverse protein products.

The tRNA: Translation's Adapter Molecule

The tRNA is the key adapter in translation. Each tRNA has two critical regions: (1) the anticodon loop — three nucleotides complementary to a specific mRNA codon; and (2) the 3' CCA end (acceptor stem) — where the specific amino acid is covalently attached by aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase enzymes.

🔑
The Ribosome Is a Ribozyme — RNA Does the Catalysis
The peptidyl transferase activity that forms peptide bonds during translation is catalyzed by the 23S rRNA (prokaryotes) / 28S rRNA (eukaryotes) — not by ribosomal proteins. This means the ribosome is a ribozyme — an RNA molecule with enzymatic activity. This discovery (2009 Nobel Prize, Steitz, Ramakrishnan, Yonath) supports the "RNA world" hypothesis that RNA preceded both DNA and proteins in early evolution.

Lesson 2 Quiz — RNA Types & Structure

5 questions · includes RNA composition data

1RNA contains uracil where DNA contains thymine. What is the base-pairing rule for uracil?
  • A
    Uracil pairs with guanine in RNA
  • B
    Uracil pairs with adenine (like thymine does in DNA) — both uracil and thymine have a 2-hydrogen bond interaction with adenine
  • C
    Uracil pairs with cytosine in RNA
  • D
    Uracil pairs with thymine when RNA forms a hybrid with DNA
Uracil pairs with Adenine — it has the same hydrogen-bonding pattern as thymine (two H-bonds with A). Uracil is essentially thymine without the 5-methyl group. Why does RNA use uracil instead of thymine? Thymine is made from uracil by adding a methyl group (requiring an extra enzyme and energy). Since RNA is a temporary molecule that doesn't need the extra stability that the methyl group provides, uracil suffices. Also: if uracil appeared in DNA by deamination of cytosine (a common mutation), it can be recognized and repaired — but in RNA, uracil is normal and would be ignored.
2A tRNA has the anticodon 3'-UAC-5'. Which mRNA codon does it recognize, and which amino acid does it carry?
  • A
    mRNA codon UAC; carries tyrosine
  • B
    mRNA codon AUC; carries isoleucine
  • C
    mRNA codon CAU; carries histidine
  • D
    mRNA codon AUG; carries methionine — the anticodon 3'-UAC-5' is complementary and antiparallel to the codon 5'-AUG-3' (the start codon)
Anticodon-codon pairing is complementary AND antiparallel. Anticodon 3'-UAC-5' pairs with: 5'-AUG-3' (mRNA). Read 5'→3': the codon is AUG — the universal start codon. AUG codes for Methionine (Met). This is the initiator tRNA. When the ribosome encounters AUG: the initiator Met-tRNA (carrying methionine, with anticodon 3'-UAC-5') base-pairs with the start codon. Translation always begins with methionine — though it is often removed post-translationally.
3The ribosome is described as a "ribozyme." What does this mean?
  • A
    The ribosome's catalytic activity (peptide bond formation) is carried out by its rRNA component, not its protein components — rRNA is the catalyst (ribozyme = RNA enzyme)
  • B
    The ribosome synthesizes RNA as well as protein
  • C
    Ribosomal proteins catalyze peptide bond formation while rRNA provides structural support only
  • D
    Ribosomes contain both RNA and enzymes, making them "ribonucleoenzymes"
The ribosome is a ribozyme because its catalytic center — the peptidyl transferase center (PTC) — is composed of rRNA, not protein. The 23S rRNA (prokaryotes) / 28S rRNA (eukaryotes) catalyzes the formation of peptide bonds between amino acids. This was confirmed by crystallographic studies of the ribosome (Steitz, Ramakrishnan, Yonath — 2009 Nobel Prize in Chemistry). The discovery that RNA can be catalytic supported the "RNA world" hypothesis and resolved the "chicken and egg" problem of how proteins could have evolved before the DNA replication machinery needed to encode them.
4📊 Based on the RNA composition graph, which type of RNA is most abundant in a typical cell? Why?
  • A
    mRNA — cells need large amounts to make all their proteins
  • B
    tRNA — one tRNA is needed for every codon translated
  • C
    rRNA (~80%) — ribosomes contain 3-4 rRNA molecules and cells contain thousands of ribosomes, each actively translating; the structural rRNA vastly outnumbers the transient mRNAs
  • D
    All three types are present in equal amounts
rRNA constitutes ~80% of total cellular RNA because ribosomes are enormously abundant: a rapidly dividing E. coli cell has ~15,000 ribosomes; a mammalian cell has millions. Each ribosome contains 3 (prokaryotes) or 4 (eukaryotes) rRNA molecules. rRNAs are large (1,500–5,000+ nucleotides) and stable. mRNA, despite encoding all proteins, is only ~5% because individual mRNAs are typically short-lived (half-life of minutes to hours in the cytoplasm). tRNA (~15%) is stable and reused many times.
5RNA is less stable than DNA. What structural feature of RNA makes it more susceptible to hydrolysis?
  • A
    The uracil bases are more reactive than thymine bases and are easily degraded
  • B
    RNA has a 2'-OH group on the ribose sugar — this hydroxyl group can attack the adjacent phosphodiester bond, catalyzing self-cleavage (hydrolysis), making RNA intrinsically less stable than DNA
  • C
    RNA is single-stranded and lacks the protective double-helix structure of DNA
  • D
    RNA molecules are smaller than DNA and therefore degrade faster
The 2'-OH group on ribose is the key: in RNA, the 2'-OH of ribose is free → under slightly alkaline conditions, this -OH attacks the adjacent 3',5'-phosphodiester bond → RNA is cleaved (2',3'-cyclic phosphate intermediate). DNA lacks the 2'-OH (has H instead) → cannot undergo this cleavage → inherently more stable. This is why: (1) DNA is better for long-term hereditary storage; (2) RNA serves as a temporary, disposable message; (3) RNA extraction requires much more careful technique than DNA extraction. Why use RNA at all? Because single-stranded RNA can fold into complex functional 3D structures (ribozymes, aptamers).

Memory: "Template is Transcribed; Coding = same as mRNA Code"
Template strand: read by RNA pol, produces the complementary RNA. Coding strand: NOT read, but has the same sequence as the mRNA (just with T→U). When scientists write "the gene sequence," they typically write the coding strand (5'→3'). When RNA pol reads the template, the RNA produced is identical to the coding strand — so the coding strand is also called the "sense" strand.

5 questions · includes transcription rate data

1A gene's template strand has the sequence 3'-ATCGTA-5'. What is the sequence of the mRNA transcribed from this template?
  • A
    3'-ATCGTA-5' — the mRNA is identical to the template
  • B
    5'-ATCGTA-3' — the mRNA is the same sequence written 5'→3'
  • C
    5'-UAGCAU-3' — RNA pol reads 3'→5' and synthesizes the complement with U instead of T
  • D
    3'-TAGCAT-5' — the DNA complement of the template
RNA pol reads template 3'→5' and synthesizes mRNA 5'→3'. Template 3'-ATCGTA-5': A→U, T→A, C→G, G→C, T→A, A→U = UAGCAU. Written 5'→3': 5'-UAGCAU-3'. Note: T in DNA becomes U in RNA (uracil replaces thymine). The mRNA is complementary to the template strand AND the same sequence as the coding strand (with U instead of T). Remember: RNA pol reads template 3'→5', produces RNA 5'→3'.
2The TATA box is found in eukaryotic promoters ~25 bp upstream of the transcription start site. What is its function?
  • A
    The TATA box is transcribed into the 5' UTR of the mRNA and helps ribosome binding
  • B
    TATA-binding protein (TBP) recognizes and binds the TATA box, initiating assembly of the general transcription factor complex (TFIID, TFIIB, etc.) and RNA Pol II at the transcription start site
  • C
    The TATA box signals the end of transcription and triggers RNA polymerase release
  • D
    The TATA box is part of the mRNA that is translated into the signal peptide
TATA box (TATAAA): an AT-rich promoter element bound by TBP (TATA-Binding Protein). TBP binding dramatically bends the DNA → creates a platform for recruiting general transcription factors (TFIID, TFIIB, TFIIF, TFIIE, TFIIH) and RNA Pol II → forms the pre-initiation complex (PIC) → TFIIH helicase unwinds the DNA at the start site → transcription begins. Many genes lack a TATA box and use alternative promoter elements (Inr, BRE, DPE). AT-richness of the TATA box makes it easier to melt (unwind) the DNA duplex — important for RNA pol access.
3RNA polymerase does NOT require a primer to begin transcription. Why is this different from DNA polymerase?
  • A
    RNA is shorter than DNA and easier to initiate de novo
  • B
    RNA polymerase uses a pre-existing RNA strand from a previous cycle as its primer
  • C
    RNA polymerase uses ATP rather than NTPs, and ATP can self-initiate
  • D
    RNA polymerase can catalyze the formation of the first phosphodiester bond between two free nucleotides de novo — DNA polymerase cannot because its active site geometry requires an existing 3'-OH to position the incoming nucleotide correctly
RNA polymerase has a different active site geometry that allows it to initiate synthesis without a primer — it can bring together two free NTPs and form the first phosphodiester bond de novo. DNA polymerase III has a strict requirement for a pre-existing 3'-OH because of how its active site positions the incoming dNTP — without the 3'-OH, the chemistry cannot proceed. The tradeoff: RNA pol has NO proofreading activity (no 3'→5' exonuclease), so RNA synthesis is less accurate than DNA replication. But since RNA is a temporary molecule, occasional errors are tolerable.
4📊 Based on the transcription rate comparison, why might prokaryotic gene expression respond faster to environmental changes than eukaryotic expression?
  • A
    Prokaryotic RNA polymerase transcribes faster AND translation begins simultaneously during transcription (coupled) — a new protein can be produced within seconds of gene activation; eukaryotes must complete transcription, process mRNA, export it, and then translate it — taking much longer
  • B
    Prokaryotes have smaller genomes so there is less DNA to search through
  • C
    Prokaryotic ribosomes are smaller and translate mRNA faster
  • D
    Eukaryotic RNA polymerase is slower because it produces longer mRNAs
Prokaryotic speed advantage: (1) Faster RNA pol (~80 nt/sec vs ~30 nt/sec); (2) No nucleus — ribosomes begin translating mRNA while it's still being transcribed (coupled transcription-translation); (3) No RNA processing needed. A prokaryotic cell can respond to a sugar source and start producing the metabolic enzymes within ~1-2 minutes. Eukaryotic response takes longer: transcription (~minutes) + processing (~minutes) + nuclear export + translation. This is why fast-responding organisms like bacteria can outcompete each other on seconds-to-minutes timescales.
5What determines which strand of DNA is used as the template strand for a given gene?
  • A
    The template strand is always the strand closest to the centromere
  • B
    Both strands are always used as templates simultaneously by two RNA polymerases
  • C
    The promoter sequence determines which strand RNA polymerase binds and in which direction it reads — the promoter is directional, specifying both the template strand AND the direction of transcription for each gene
  • D
    RNA polymerase randomly selects either strand for different transcription events
The promoter is directional: it specifies both which strand RNA pol will use as template AND which direction it will move along the DNA. Different genes on the same chromosome can use different strands as their template — some genes on the (+) strand and others on the (−) strand — as long as each has its own directional promoter. This is why genes are depicted with arrows in genome browser views: the arrow direction shows which strand is the coding strand and which way RNA pol moves. RNA pol doesn't randomly choose — it's recruited specifically to each gene's directional promoter.

RNA Processing

Introns vs. Exons

The pre-mRNA of most human genes contains non-coding sequences (introns) interrupting coding sequences (exons). The average human gene has ~8 introns; some have over 100. Introns must be precisely removed and exons joined — a single nucleotide error in splicing can cause disease (many splicing mutations cause human genetic disorders).

🔑
Alternative Splicing: One Gene → Many Proteins
Alternative splicing is the process of including or excluding different exons in the final mRNA. Example: the DSCAM gene in Drosophila can produce over 38,000 different proteins from a single gene through alternative splicing — more than the total number of genes in the fly! In humans, ~95% of multi-exon genes are alternatively spliced. This is why humans (~20,000 genes) can produce an estimated 90,000+ different proteins.
🧠
Memory: "EXons are EXpressed; INtrons stay IN the nucleus"
Exons: the sequences that end up in the mature mRNA and are expressed (translated). Introns: intervening sequences removed during splicing; they remain in the nucleus (as lariat structures) and are degraded. Exons are the coding parts; introns are the interruptions. In the final mature mRNA, only exon sequences remain — joined together in order.

Why a Triplet Code?

With 4 bases, a doublet code (4² = 16 codons) could only encode 16 amino acids — not enough for the 20 standard amino acids. A triplet code (4³ = 64 codons) encodes more than enough: 20 amino acids + start + stop signals, with room to spare. That "room to spare" gives us the degeneracy of the code — most amino acids are encoded by multiple codons.

Prokaryotic Initiation Uses the Shine-Dalgarno Sequence Instead of Scanning
Prokaryotes don't scan from a 5' cap. Instead, the 30S subunit recognizes a purine-rich sequence ~5-10 bases upstream of the AUG start codon called the Shine-Dalgarno (SD) sequence (consensus: AGGAGG). The SD sequence base-pairs directly with the 16S rRNA of the 30S subunit, positioning the ribosome precisely at the AUG. This allows prokaryotic mRNAs to be polycistronic — multiple start codons on one mRNA, each with its own SD sequence. Most eukaryotic mRNAs are monocistronic (one protein per mRNA).

Termination: Three Stop Codons, One Release

When a stop codon (UAA, UAG, or UGA) enters the A site, no aminoacyl-tRNA matches it. Instead, release factors (RF1 or RF2 in prokaryotes; eRF1 in eukaryotes) bind the A site and mimic tRNA shape. The release factor triggers the peptidyl transferase to add water (hydrolysis) instead of an amino acid → the polypeptide chain is released from the P-site tRNA. The ribosome subunits then dissociate from the mRNA.

Polyribosomes (Polysomes): Assembly Line Efficiency

Once the first ribosome has translated far enough from the 5' end, a second ribosome can load onto the mRNA's 5' cap and begin scanning. Multiple ribosomes translate the same mRNA simultaneously — called a polyribosome (polysome). This dramatically increases protein production per mRNA molecule: a single mRNA with 10 ribosomes produces 10 protein copies simultaneously.

🧠
Direction Summary: Ribosome → mRNA reads 5'→3'; protein grows N-terminus → C-terminus
RNA pol reads template 3'→5' and makes mRNA 5'→3'. Ribosome moves along mRNA 5'→3'. New amino acids are always added to the C-terminal end of the growing polypeptide (the growing chain is attached to the P-site tRNA at its C-terminus; the new amino acid's amino group attacks it). Therefore protein grows N→C while mRNA is read 5'→3'. The ribosome moves one codon (3 nt) per elongation cycle, adding one amino acid, taking ~50 msec per cycle.

Why Silent Mutations Are Not Always "Silent"
Silent mutations change the DNA/mRNA sequence without changing the amino acid — usually no protein effect. But exceptions exist: (1) A silent mutation in a splice site can disrupt splicing → retained intron → frameshift; (2) A silent mutation can affect mRNA secondary structure → altered mRNA stability or translation rate; (3) A synonymous codon change may use a rare codon → slower translation → altered protein folding. These exceptions are important in disease — some "synonymous" SNPs in human disease genes affect phenotype through non-obvious mechanisms.

Selective toxicity exploits structural differences between prokaryotic (70S = 30S+50S) and eukaryotic (80S = 40S+60S) ribosomes. Tetracycline binds the 30S A-site region (16S rRNA) → blocks aminoacyl-tRNA entry → halts elongation in bacteria. The eukaryotic 40S subunit (18S rRNA) is structurally different → tetracycline binds much more weakly → human ribosomes are largely unaffected. This is the pharmacological basis for antibiotic safety. Mitochondria (with 70S-like ribosomes) can be affected at high antibiotic doses — explaining some rare mitochondrial side effects.

Alternative splicing: the same pre-mRNA can be spliced differently in different tissues by tissue-specific splicing factors (SR proteins, hnRNPs) that bind exonic/intronic splicing enhancers (ESEs/ISEs) or silencers (ESSs/ISSs) → spliceosome includes or skips different exons → multiple distinct protein isoforms. ~95% of human multi-exon genes undergo alternative splicing.
